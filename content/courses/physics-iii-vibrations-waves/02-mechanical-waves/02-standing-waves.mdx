# Standing Waves and Boundary Conditions

## The Big Picture

A **standing wave** is a stationary oscillation pattern formed by interference of two waves traveling in opposite directions. Standing waves emerge whenever waves are confined — strings between fixed points, sound in pipes, electromagnetic fields in cavities. They have discrete allowed frequencies (the **normal modes**), each a "natural way" the system likes to vibrate. This discreteness is the seed of quantum mechanics.

## Intuition First — Constructive Interference Locked in Place

Imagine a wave traveling right hitting a wall and reflecting back. The right-mover and left-mover superpose:

$$y(x, t) = A\sin(kx - \omega t) + A\sin(kx + \omega t) = 2A\sin(kx)\cos(\omega t)$$

The position dependence $\sin(kx)$ is **fixed in space**; the time dependence $\cos(\omega t)$ is uniform. The wave doesn't travel — it oscillates in place.

Some positions ($\sin(kx) = 0$) never move — **nodes**.
Others ($\sin(kx) = \pm 1$) oscillate with maximum amplitude — **antinodes**.

## Normal Modes on a Fixed String

For string of length $L$, fixed at both ends: $y(0, t) = y(L, t) = 0$.

The first BC requires the standing wave form $\sin(kx)\cos(\omega t)$.
The second BC requires $\sin(kL) = 0 \Rightarrow kL = n\pi$ for $n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$

**Allowed wavelengths:** $\lambda_n = 2L/n$.

**Allowed frequencies:** $f_n = nc/(2L)$.

The lowest, $f_1 = c/(2L)$, is the **fundamental**. Higher modes are **harmonics**.

## Boundary Conditions

| Boundary type | Mathematical form |
|---|---|
| Fixed (rigid) | $y = 0$ |
| Free (open) | $\partial y/\partial x = 0$ |

**Open pipe (both ends open):** $\lambda_n = 2L/n$, same as fixed string.
**Closed-open pipe:** $\lambda_n = 4L/(2n-1)$ — only odd harmonics.

This is why a clarinet (closed-open) sounds different from a flute (open-open) of the same length.

## Energy in a Standing Wave

Energy oscillates between kinetic and potential — but stays in place. Real strings continually transfer energy to the surrounding air (radiation), so the standing wave gradually decays. Q-factor of a string can be ~1000.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Standing waves don't carry energy."**

Energy is *stored* but not *transported* in an ideal standing wave. Real systems leak energy through boundaries (sound radiation) or damping.

**Misconception 2: "Only the fundamental exists."**

All harmonics can be excited simultaneously. The combination determines **timbre** — why a violin and trumpet at the same fundamental pitch sound different.

**Misconception 3: "Modes only at integer multiples."**

Only for ideal 1D strings with simple BCs. Real strings have slight stiffness causing **inharmonicity**. 2D drumheads and 3D cavities have non-integer modal ratios.

## Engineering Connection

- **Musical instruments:** strings, pipes, drums — all standing-wave systems with carefully shaped boundary conditions.
- **Microwave ovens:** standing waves in a metal cavity; rotating turntable to even out hot spots.
- **Lasers:** standing wave between mirrors picks out specific frequencies → coherent light.
- **Building resonances:** structures have natural standing-wave modes; avoid driving frequencies near these.
- **Optical fibers:** transverse modes are standing waves; single-mode fibers carry only the fundamental.
- **Atomic structure:** electron "orbitals" are essentially standing-wave solutions to the Schrödinger equation.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** String, $L = 1$ m, $c = 200$ m/s. Fundamental frequency?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$f_1 = c/(2L) = 100$ Hz.

Harmonics: 100, 200, 300, ... Hz.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Open-open pipe length 0.5 m, $c = 343$ m/s. First three resonant frequencies?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$f_n = nc/(2L) = n\cdot 343$ Hz.

So 343, 686, 1029 Hz.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3. Engineering: Concert hall.** Hall length 30 m, $c = 343$ m/s. Lowest standing wave frequency?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Treating as open-open (idealization): $f_1 = 343/60 \approx 5.7$ Hz.

This is below human hearing (20 Hz minimum), but the room modes do interact with bass instruments and PA systems. Modal analysis is critical for concert hall acoustic design.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Conceptual.** Why does a clarinet (closed-open pipe) play an octave lower than a flute (open-open) of the same length?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Open-open: $f_1 = c/(2L)$.
Closed-open: $f_1 = c/(4L)$.

The closed-open fundamental is **half** the open-open — one octave lower.

Geometrically: closed-open standing wave has 1/4 wavelength in the pipe (node at closed end, antinode at open). Open-open has 1/2 wavelength. Same wavelength relationship as the famous "Helmholtz resonator" → "open pipe" distinction.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5.** A string is plucked at its center. Which harmonics are excited?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Plucking at $L/2$ produces an initial shape with a peak at $L/2$. Only modes with **antinodes at $L/2$** are strongly excited.

Mode $n$ has antinode at $L/2$ when $\sin(n\pi/2) = 1$, i.e., **odd $n$**: $n = 1, 3, 5, \ldots$.

So mainly odd harmonics. Even harmonics are absent (because they have nodes at $L/2$). This gives a characteristic timbre — and why guitarists pluck different positions for different sounds.
</ExA>
