# The Wave Equation

## The Big Picture

A **wave** is a disturbance that travels through space, carrying energy and information but not matter. The mathematical statement of "wave propagation" is the **wave equation** — a single second-order PDE that governs strings, fluids, electromagnetic fields, and even gravitational waves. Solving and understanding this one equation unlocks the entire study of wave phenomena.

## Intuition First — Restoring Force + Inertia + Spatial Coupling

A wave needs three things:
1. **Inertia** (mass): so motion can persist.
2. **Restoring force**: pulls particles back toward equilibrium.
3. **Coupling between neighbors**: so disturbance at one place pushes neighbors.

Combine: at point $x$, the net restoring force depends on neighbors' positions — i.e., on the **second spatial derivative** $\partial^2 y/\partial x^2$. Inertia means acceleration $\partial^2 y/\partial t^2$ equals this force per mass.

## Derivation: Wave on a String

A taut string with tension $T$, linear mass density $\mu$, small displacement $y(x, t)$.

Consider a small segment between $x$ and $x + dx$. The tension at each end pulls along the string direction. The vertical component of net force:

$$F_y \approx T\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\bigg|_{x+dx} - T\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\bigg|_x = T\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2}dx$$

Mass: $\mu\,dx$. Acceleration: $\partial^2 y/\partial t^2$.

Newton's law:
$$\mu\,dx\,\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2} = T\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2}dx$$

$$\boxed{\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2} = c^2\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2}, \quad c = \sqrt{T/\mu}}$$

The **wave equation**. $c$ is the **wave speed** — set by tension and density.

## General Solution: d'Alembert

The wave equation has solutions of the form:

$$y(x, t) = f(x - ct) + g(x + ct)$$

- $f(x - ct)$: any shape, moving right at speed $c$ (without distortion).
- $g(x + ct)$: any shape, moving left at speed $c$.

So *any* wave is a sum of left-mover plus right-mover. This is **dispersionless** propagation — the shape doesn't change in time.

## Harmonic Waves

A particularly useful basis: sinusoidal waves.

$$y(x, t) = A\cos(kx - \omega t)$$

where:
- $k$ = **wave number** (rad/m), $\omega$ = **angular frequency** (rad/s)
- **Wavelength** $\lambda = 2\pi/k$
- **Period** $T = 2\pi/\omega$
- **Speed** $c = \omega/k = \lambda f$ — the **dispersion relation**

For ideal strings (and many media), $c$ is constant: nondispersive.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Particles travel with the wave."**

For mechanical waves, particles oscillate in place (transverse or longitudinal). The wave pattern moves; the medium doesn't.

**Misconception 2: "Wave speed = particle speed."**

Distinct! Wave speed $c$ depends on medium properties. Particle speed depends on amplitude and frequency. For small waves on string, particle speed $\ll c$.

**Misconception 3: "Wave equation is 1D only."**

Generalizes to 3D: $\partial^2 y/\partial t^2 = c^2\nabla^2 y$. Same structure, $\nabla^2$ replaces $\partial^2/\partial x^2$.

## Engineering Connection

- **Acoustic waves in pipes:** wave equation for pressure $p(x, t)$ with $c = \sqrt{\gamma RT/M}$ (sound speed in gases).
- **Vibrations in beams** (slender): the Euler-Bernoulli equation is 4th-order, not 2nd — distinct from string but still wave-like.
- **EM waves in vacuum:** $\partial^2 \mathbf E/\partial t^2 = c^2\nabla^2\mathbf E$ from Maxwell's equations, $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0\varepsilon_0}$.
- **Seismic waves:** P-waves and S-waves in earth obey wave equations; wave speeds reveal subsurface properties.
- **Ultrasound imaging:** wave equation with variable $c$ for tissue properties enables image reconstruction.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** String tension 50 N, mass density 0.01 kg/m. Wave speed?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$c = \sqrt{50/0.01} = \sqrt{5000} \approx 70.7$ m/s.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Sound wave with $f = 1000$ Hz in air ($c = 343$ m/s). Wavelength?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\lambda = c/f = 343/1000 = 0.343$ m.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3. Engineering: Guitar string.** $L = 0.65$ m, $\mu = 5\times 10^{-4}$ kg/m. Want fundamental at 110 Hz. Find tension.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Fundamental wavelength: $\lambda = 2L = 1.3$ m. Speed: $c = f\lambda = 143$ m/s.

$T = \mu c^2 = 5\times 10^{-4}\cdot 143^2 \approx 10.22$ N.

Guitar strings are typically tensioned to 70-120 N for various pitches — this string is light/loose.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Conceptual.** A wave $y(x, t) = A\sin(kx - \omega t)$ moves which direction?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
At time $t$, the wave looks like $\sin(kx - \omega t)$. To follow a specific feature (e.g., a crest), set $kx - \omega t = $ const, so $x$ increases with $t$.

**Moves to the right** (+x direction) at speed $\omega/k$.

If instead the form were $\sin(kx + \omega t)$, it would move left.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5.** Verify that $y(x, t) = (x - ct)^2$ satisfies the wave equation.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\partial_t y = -2c(x - ct)$. $\partial_t^2 y = 2c^2$.

$\partial_x y = 2(x - ct)$. $\partial_x^2 y = 2$.

Check: $\partial_t^2 y = c^2\partial_x^2 y$ gives $2c^2 = c^2(2)$ ✓.

So *any* function of $(x - ct)$ satisfies the wave equation — this is d'Alembert's theorem in action.
</ExA>
