# Diffraction and Resolution

## The Big Picture

**Diffraction** is the bending of waves around obstacles or through openings. Far from being a quirk, it sets fundamental limits on resolution in every imaging system — telescopes, microscopes, chip lithography, your eye. Knowing the diffraction limit tells you what's achievable; pushing past it requires deep tricks (super-resolution microscopy, immersion lithography). Diffraction is *the* limit physics imposes on imaging.

## Single-Slit Diffraction

Light through a slit of width $a$ produces a diffraction pattern with central bright peak and weaker side lobes.

**Position of first minimum:**

$$\sin\theta = \lambda/a$$

The angular width of the central peak: $\Delta\theta \approx 2\lambda/a$.

**Larger slits ⇒ narrower diffraction** (less spreading).
**Smaller slits ⇒ broader diffraction** (more spreading).

This is a wave-mechanical version of the Heisenberg uncertainty principle.

## Circular Aperture

For a circular hole (e.g., telescope objective) of diameter $D$:

$$\sin\theta = 1.22\lambda/D \quad \text{(first minimum)}$$

This is the **Airy disk**. The 1.22 factor comes from solving Bessel functions for the first zero.

## Resolution Limit (Rayleigh Criterion)

Two point sources are **just resolvable** when the central maximum of one falls on the first minimum of the other. Angular separation:

$$\theta_{\min} = 1.22\lambda/D$$

For a telescope with $D = 1$ m, $\lambda = 550$ nm: $\theta_{\min} \approx 7\times 10^{-7}$ rad ≈ 0.14 arcsec.

## Numerical Aperture (NA) and Microscopes

For microscopes: resolution $\approx \lambda/(2\,\text{NA})$ where NA is the numerical aperture.

Visible light microscopes: NA ~ 1.4 (oil immersion), $\lambda \sim 500$ nm → resolution ~ 180 nm. Cannot see smaller details directly.

**Beating the diffraction limit:**
- Electron microscopes ($\lambda \sim 0.01$ nm)
- X-ray crystallography
- Super-resolution fluorescence (STED, STORM)

## Diffraction Gratings

Many parallel slits with spacing $d$ produce sharp peaks at:

$$d\sin\theta = m\lambda$$

Used in spectrometers to separate wavelengths. Higher-order $m$ gives more dispersion.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Diffraction matters only for very small apertures."**

Diffraction occurs at every aperture, but is significant when $\lambda$ is comparable to $a$. For visible light through a 1 mm hole, diffraction angle ~ $5\times 10^{-4}$ rad — small but measurable. For radio waves through a building doorway, diffraction is dramatic.

**Misconception 2: "Bigger telescope just collects more light."**

It also gives better **resolution** by $D^{-1}$ scaling. Hubble (2.4 m) sees details ~ 0.05 arcsec; James Webb (6.5 m) ~ 0.02 arcsec at same wavelength.

**Misconception 3: "Diffraction limit is absolute."**

Classical limit; modern microscopy techniques (STED, PALM, STORM) routinely beat it by exploiting photophysics. Limit is for **linear** imaging — nonlinear methods can do better.

## Engineering Connection

- **Camera lenses:** small aperture ($\beta$ stop) gives sharp deep focus *until* diffraction takes over (~f/16 for modern sensors).
- **Lithography:** Intel chips made with EUV light ($\lambda = 13.5$ nm) to push diffraction-limited features down to ~10 nm.
- **Radio telescopes:** very long baseline interferometry (VLBI) synthesizes virtual apertures of Earth-diameter — sharper than any single telescope.
- **Acoustics:** speaker directionality depends on $\lambda/D$. Subwoofers (long $\lambda$) are omnidirectional; tweeters are directional.
- **Radar:** small antennas + short wavelength → fine angular resolution.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Single slit $a = 0.1$ mm, $\lambda = 600$ nm. First minimum angle?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\sin\theta = 600\times 10^{-9}/10^{-4} = 6\times 10^{-3}$.

$\theta \approx 6\times 10^{-3}$ rad ≈ 0.34°.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Telescope $D = 0.5$ m, $\lambda = 550$ nm. Resolution?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\theta = 1.22\cdot 550\times 10^{-9}/0.5 \approx 1.34\times 10^{-6}$ rad ≈ 0.28 arcsec.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3. Engineering: Hubble vs Earth telescope.** Hubble has $D = 2.4$ m, $\lambda = 550$ nm. Best ground telescope (without adaptive optics) limited by atmospheric seeing $\sim 1$ arcsec. Compare resolutions.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Hubble: $\theta = 1.22\cdot 550\times 10^{-9}/2.4 \approx 2.8\times 10^{-7}$ rad ≈ 0.058 arcsec.

Ground: ~1 arcsec.

Hubble has ~17× better resolution. Even though giant ground telescopes (10 m class) would be diffraction-limited to ~0.015 arcsec, atmosphere ruins this without adaptive optics.

This is why we put telescopes in space.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Photolithography.** EUV at $\lambda = 13.5$ nm. With NA = 0.33, what's the minimum feature size?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Resolution: $\lambda/(2\,\text{NA}) = 13.5/(0.66) \approx 20$ nm.

EUV lithography enables 7 nm and 5 nm CMOS nodes through this physics. Pushing to smaller required higher NA and clever pattern doubling/quadrupling tricks.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why are bigger telescopes built primarily to improve resolution, not just collect more light?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Light collection scales as $D^2$ — area. Useful for faint objects.

Resolution scales as $D^{-1}$ — angular sharpness. Useful for *distinguishing* close objects.

At a certain point, light collection plateaus (we can integrate longer), but resolution remains forever bounded by aperture. Distant exoplanet imaging, galactic structure, gravitational lensing — all resolution-limited science.

That's why every next-generation telescope is bigger: the resolution improvement is what enables new discoveries.
</ExA>
