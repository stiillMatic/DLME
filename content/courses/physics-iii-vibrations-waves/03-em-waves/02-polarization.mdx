# Polarization and Energy Transport

## The Big Picture

Unlike sound (a scalar pressure wave), light is a **vector** wave with two perpendicular polarization states. Polarization affects how light interacts with materials: polarizers, glare reduction, LCD displays, fiber optics, and quantum cryptography all depend on it. Understanding polarization completes the basic picture of EM wave physics.

## Linear Polarization

For wave traveling in $+\hat{x}$: $\mathbf E$ lies in $yz$-plane. Two independent directions:
- $\hat{y}$-polarized: $\mathbf E = E_0\hat{y}\cos(kx - \omega t)$
- $\hat{z}$-polarized: $\mathbf E = E_0\hat{z}\cos(kx - \omega t)$

Linear polarization at angle $\theta$ from $\hat y$: $\mathbf E = E_0(\hat y\cos\theta + \hat z\sin\theta)\cos(kx - \omega t)$.

## Circular Polarization

Combine perpendicular components with 90° phase shift:

$$\mathbf E = E_0[\hat y\cos(kx - \omega t) \pm \hat z\sin(kx - \omega t)]$$

The tip of $\mathbf E$ traces a circle in the $yz$-plane — at a fixed location, the field rotates as a function of time.

**Right or left circular** depending on rotation sense.

## Elliptical Polarization

General case: arbitrary amplitudes and phase. The tip traces an ellipse.

Linear and circular are special cases of elliptical.

## Polarizers and Malus's Law

A polarizer transmits only one polarization component. For linear input at angle $\theta$ to polarizer axis, transmitted intensity:

$$I = I_0\cos^2\theta$$

Two crossed polarizers (90° apart): no transmission.
Three polarizers at 0°, 45°, 90°: transmission ≈ 25% (counterintuitively nonzero!).

## Unpolarized Light

Sunlight, incandescent bulbs: random superposition of all polarizations. A single polarizer transmits half (by symmetry).

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Polarization is a property of all waves."**

Only transverse waves can be polarized (sound — longitudinal — cannot). EM waves are transverse, so they have polarization.

**Misconception 2: "A polarizer blocks 100% of perpendicular polarization."**

Real polarizers have finite extinction ratio. Ideal Malus's law assumes perfect polarizers.

**Misconception 3: "Three polarizers can't transmit more than two."**

Three polarizers at intermediate angles **do** transmit more than two crossed polarizers — a striking demonstration of how each polarizer "resets" the polarization state.

## Engineering Connection

- **LCD screens:** light passes through 2 crossed polarizers; liquid crystal twists polarization to control transmission per pixel.
- **Polarized sunglasses:** block horizontal polarization (sunlight reflected off water/road is horizontally polarized).
- **3D movies (old style):** circularly polarized projection; each eye sees one circular polarization.
- **Stress analysis (photoelasticity):** polarized light through stressed plastic reveals stress patterns.
- **Quantum cryptography (BB84):** uses polarization states as quantum bits, exploiting that measurement disturbs polarization.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Unpolarized light, intensity 100 W/m². Through one polarizer?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Half passes: 50 W/m².
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Two polarizers at 60°. Linearly polarized input (along first) at $I_0 = 80$ W/m². Output?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$I = I_0\cos^2 60° = 80\cdot 0.25 = 20$ W/m².
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Three polarizers at 0°, 45°, 90°. Unpolarized input intensity $I_0$. Output?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
After P1: $I_0/2$ (linear polarization along P1).

After P2 at 45° from P1: $(I_0/2)\cos^2 45° = I_0/4$.

After P3 at 90° from P1, 45° from P2: $(I_0/4)\cos^2 45° = I_0/8$.

Final: $I_0/8 = 12.5\%$. Three polarizers transmit more than zero crossed pair — startling but mathematically clear.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: LCD calculation.** A pixel uses 2 crossed polarizers and a liquid crystal that rotates polarization by $\theta$. Find $\theta$ for 50% transmission.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Light passes P1 (polarized along P1). Crystal rotates by $\theta$. P2 axis 90° from P1, so angle from rotated light to P2 axis is $(90 - \theta)°$.

Transmission: $\cos^2(90 - \theta) = \sin^2\theta$.

For 50%: $\sin^2\theta = 0.5 \Rightarrow \theta = 45°$.

LCD bias controls the crystal rotation, varying transmission per pixel. Display contrast = polarizer extinction ratio × LCD modulation.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why are sunglasses polarized horizontally (blocking horizontal)?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Light reflecting off horizontal surfaces (water, roads) at glancing angle has **horizontal polarization** preferentially (Brewster's angle physics).

By blocking horizontal, polarized sunglasses cut glare more effectively than equal-intensity neutral filters. You see less reflected light but normal light unimpeded.

Same principle protects fishermen from glare while enabling them to see beneath water surface.
</ExA>
