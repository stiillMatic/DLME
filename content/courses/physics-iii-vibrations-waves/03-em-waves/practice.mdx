# Chapter 3 Practice — Electromagnetic Waves

<ExQ>
**1.** EM wave frequency 1 GHz. Wavelength?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\lambda = c/f = 3\times 10^8/10^9 = 0.3$ m. Microwave region.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** $E_0 = 50$ V/m EM wave. Average intensity?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\langle S\rangle = \tfrac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 c E_0^2 = 0.5\cdot 8.854\times 10^{-12}\cdot 3\times 10^8\cdot 2500 \approx 3.32$ W/m².
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Two polarizers at 30°. Unpolarized input 100 W/m². Output?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
P1: 50 W/m² (unpolarized → half).

P2: $50\cos^2 30° = 50\cdot 0.75 = 37.5$ W/m².
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: WiFi range.** WiFi transmits 100 mW at 2.4 GHz isotropically. At what distance is intensity 1 μW/m² (typical detection threshold)?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Isotropic: intensity $\propto 1/(4\pi r^2)$.

$P/(4\pi r^2) = 10^{-6} \Rightarrow r^2 = 0.1/(4\pi\cdot 10^{-6}) \approx 7958$.

$r \approx 89$ m. Realistic for indoor WiFi (with antennas providing some directionality).
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why does polarized light from sky give photographers richer colors?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Skylight is partially polarized (90° from sun direction). A polarizing filter blocks the polarized scattered light, reducing washout.

Result: deeper blue sky, more saturated colors. Specific to landscape photography. Polarizers are useless for portraits or when shooting toward/away from sun.

Application of EM theory in everyday craft.
</ExA>
