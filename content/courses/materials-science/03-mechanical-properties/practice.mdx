# Chapter 3 Practice — Mechanical Properties

<ExQ>
**1.** A material has $E = 100$ GPa, $\sigma_y = 200$ MPa. Modulus of resilience?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$U_R = \sigma_y^2/(2E) = (2\times 10^8)^2/(2\times 10^{11}) = 0.2$ MJ/m³.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Two materials: A ($\sigma_y = 800, \varepsilon_f = 0.05$); B ($\sigma_y = 300, \varepsilon_f = 0.40$). Which is tougher?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Toughness ≈ $\sigma\cdot\varepsilon$.

A: $40$ MJ/m³. B: $120$ MJ/m³.

**B is tougher** despite lower yield — its much greater ductility wins.

This is why mild steel (low yield, high ductility) is preferred for crash structures over hardened steel.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Engineering: A bolt rated for 50 kN with safety factor 3. Given $\sigma_y = 600$ MPa, find required area.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Allowable stress: $200$ MPa. Required: $A = 50000/(2\times 10^8) = 2.5\times 10^{-4}$ m² = 250 mm².

Diameter: $d = \sqrt{4A/\pi} = 17.8$ mm. Round up to standard 20 mm bolt.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Conceptual.** Why does cold working a metal increase its yield strength but decrease ductility?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Cold working introduces dislocations. More dislocations → mutual entanglement → harder to move → higher yield.

But near-saturation of dislocations means little additional plastic deformation possible before fracture → lower ductility.

Reversed by annealing (heat treatment), which removes dislocations.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5.** Hardness HRC = 50 corresponds roughly to UTS = 1700 MPa. Estimate yield strength (assume yield ≈ 0.7 × UTS).
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\sigma_y \approx 0.7\cdot 1700 = 1190$ MPa.

Hardness tests are non-destructive and quick — used in field as a proxy for tensile properties.
</ExA>
