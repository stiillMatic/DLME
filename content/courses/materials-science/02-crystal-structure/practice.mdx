# Chapter 2 Practice — Crystal Structure

<ExQ>
**1.** Atomic packing factor of FCC?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
APF = 4 atoms × volume each / unit cell volume = 0.74. Highest possible for cubic packing.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Iron has BCC at room temp, $a = 0.287$ nm. Atomic radius?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
BCC: body diagonal = $4r$, body diagonal = $a\sqrt 3$.

$r = a\sqrt 3/4 = 0.287\cdot 1.732/4 = 0.124$ nm.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3. Engineering: Aluminum density.** Al has FCC, $a = 0.405$ nm, atomic mass 27 g/mol. Compute density.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
4 atoms × 27 g/mol / (0.405e-9)³ / 6.022e23 = ...
$V_{cell} = 6.64\times 10^{-29}$ m³.

$\rho = (4\cdot 27\times 10^{-3})/(6.64\times 10^{-29}\cdot 6.022\times 10^{23}) = 0.108/0.04 = 2700$ kg/m³.

Matches measured Al density ✓.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Conceptual.** Why are FCC metals more ductile than BCC?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
FCC has 12 close-packed slip systems; BCC has 48 but with much higher Peierls (lattice friction) stress. FCC slip is easier.

FCC stays ductile down to cryogenic temperatures (austenitic stainless). BCC has a ductile-brittle transition (carbon steel cold weather embrittlement).
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Multi-step. HCP magnesium.** $a = 0.321$ nm, $c = 0.521$ nm. Compute c/a ratio. Compare to ideal HCP (1.633).
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$c/a = 0.521/0.321 = 1.623$. Very close to ideal.

Slight deviations from ideal $c/a$ correlate with anisotropy in mechanical properties — Mg has different stiffness along $a$ vs $c$ axis.
</ExA>
