# Conformations of Alkanes and Cycloalkanes

## The Big Picture

Single bonds in organic molecules can rotate freely (no $\pi$ barrier). This generates infinitely many 3D shapes — **conformations** — but only a few are particularly stable. Understanding conformational analysis is essential for explaining molecular flexibility, reactivity (some reactions only proceed in specific conformations), and the structure of biological macromolecules like proteins and DNA.

## Ethane Conformations

Two extremes:
- **Staggered**: H atoms on adjacent carbons are maximally separated. Energy minimum.
- **Eclipsed**: H atoms aligned. ~12 kJ/mol higher (steric repulsion + torsional strain).

Rotation barrier is small (~12 kJ/mol) — at room temperature, ethane rotates freely (~10¹⁰ rotations/sec).

**Newman projection:** view down the C–C bond; near carbon as point, far carbon as circle.

## Butane Conformations

CH₃–CH₂–CH₂–CH₃: viewing down C2–C3. The two methyl groups orient at angles 0°, 60°, 120°, ...

Relative energies:
- **Anti** (180°): methyls opposite. Lowest energy.
- **Gauche** (60° or 300°): methyls 60° apart. ~3.8 kJ/mol higher than anti.
- **Eclipsed** (0°): methyls aligned. ~18 kJ/mol higher (highest barrier).

Most butane molecules exist in the anti conformation at room temperature.

## Cycloalkanes

Rings introduce **ring strain**:

| Ring | Strain energy (kJ/mol) | Shape |
|---|---|---|
| Cyclopropane | 116 | Triangle (very strained) |
| Cyclobutane | 110 | Puckered square |
| Cyclopentane | 26 | Envelope/twist |
| **Cyclohexane** | **0** | **Chair** (no strain) |
| Cycloheptane | 27 | Twist-chair |

Cyclohexane is special: chair conformation has all bond angles at 111° (close to tetrahedral) and all H atoms in staggered relations. Hence: zero strain.

## Cyclohexane Chair

The chair has two types of hydrogen positions:
- **Axial**: pointing along ring axis.
- **Equatorial**: pointing outward, in ring "plane."

**Ring flip:** chair → chair conversion swaps axial and equatorial. Substituents prefer **equatorial** to avoid 1,3-diaxial clashes.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Bonds rotate freely without energy cost."**

There are torsional and steric barriers. At very low temperatures, conformations can be "frozen out."

**Misconception 2: "Cyclohexane is flat."**

Flat hexagon would have 120° angles (vs 109.5° for sp³). Severely strained. Real cyclohexane is puckered into chair — fundamental for steroid chemistry, sugars, etc.

**Misconception 3: "All conformations have the same chemistry."**

Often false. Reactions can be strongly conformation-dependent (e.g., E2 elimination requires anti-periplanar arrangement of leaving groups).

## Engineering Connection

- **Drug design:** active conformation may be different from most-populated; "drug-likeness" considers conformational flexibility.
- **Polymer flexibility:** Tg (glass transition) depends on conformational changes.
- **Sugar chemistry:** glucose has α/β anomers based on conformation around C1.
- **Lipid membrane fluidity:** kinks from cis-double bonds disrupt packing → lower viscosity at body temperature.
- **Protein folding:** secondary structures (α-helix, β-sheet) are specific conformations.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Stable conformation of butane around C2-C3 bond?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**Anti**: methyl groups at 180° apart. Lowest energy.

(Gauche is 3.8 kJ/mol higher; eclipsed conformations are barriers.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Which has more ring strain: cyclopropane or cyclohexane?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**Cyclopropane** by far. 60° angles vs ideal 109.5° = severe strain (~116 kJ/mol).

Cyclohexane in chair has 111° angles, all staggered — zero strain. Most stable cycloalkane.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** In methylcyclohexane, why is the methyl axially destabilized?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Axial methyl clashes with other axial hydrogens on same side (1,3-diaxial interactions). Steric repulsion ~7 kJ/mol.

Equilibrium favors equatorial methyl ~95:5 at room temperature.

This is a key concept in stereochemistry of cyclic systems.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Membrane fluidity.** Why do unsaturated fats have lower melting points than saturated fats?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Saturated fats: all sp³ chains, can pack tightly into all-anti zigzag conformations. Strong London forces → high melting point.

Unsaturated (cis double bond): kink in chain → disrupted packing → weaker intermolecular forces → lower melting point.

Cell membranes use this: cold-blooded animals incorporate more unsaturated lipids to keep membranes fluid at low temperatures.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why is the ring flip of cyclohexane fast at room temperature but observable by NMR at low temperatures?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Ring flip activation energy ~45 kJ/mol. Rate ~10⁵/sec at 298 K — too fast for room-temp NMR (timescale ~ms) to see axial/equatorial as separate.

At ~−100°C, rate slows to NMR-detectable. Distinct axial and equatorial proton signals appear.

This is a classical example of "dynamic NMR" — used in measuring activation energies of conformational changes.
</ExA>
