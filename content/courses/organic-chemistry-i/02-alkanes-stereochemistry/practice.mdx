# Chapter 2 Practice — Alkanes and Stereochemistry

<ExQ>
**1.** Methylcyclohexane: methyl equatorial vs axial — which is more stable, and by how much?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**Equatorial** more stable by ~7 kJ/mol due to 1,3-diaxial interactions of axial methyl.

Equilibrium: ~95% equatorial at room temperature.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Is 2,3-dichlorobutane chiral? How many stereoisomers?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Two stereocenters. Could be up to 4 stereoisomers.

But meso form possible (if it has internal mirror plane). Stereoisomers:
- (R,R)
- (S,S) [enantiomer of R,R]
- (R,S) = (S,R) by symmetry — meso (achiral despite stereocenters)

**3 stereoisomers**, of which 2 are chiral and 1 is meso.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** What is the structural difference between cis-2-butene and trans-2-butene?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Cis: methyl groups on same side of double bond.
Trans: methyl groups on opposite sides.

These are **diastereomers** (geometric isomers). Different boiling points, densities, reactivities.

Trans is more stable (no steric clash). For longer alkenes, this affects melting points dramatically.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Polymer tacticity.** Polypropylene has CH₃ groups attached to alternating carbons. Isotactic (all same side) vs syndiotactic (alternating sides) vs atactic (random). Which is most crystalline?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**Isotactic** crystallizes well — uniform stereochemistry allows tight packing into helical conformation.

Atactic: random configurations resist packing → amorphous, soft, lower melting.

Industrially: Ziegler-Natta or metallocene catalysts produce isotactic polypropylene for strong fibers and plastics.

This is stereochemistry **at the polymer level** — controllable via catalyst design (Nobel Prize 1963 and 2005).
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why doesn't free rotation around single bonds prevent chirality?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Free rotation around C–C bonds changes conformation, not configuration. Mirror image relationship is invariant under bond rotation.

To interconvert enantiomers, you'd have to *break* bonds (or pass through transition state with planar geometry — racemization).

So chirality is preserved through normal vibrations and rotations — only chemistry (bond breaking) can convert one enantiomer to another.
</ExA>
