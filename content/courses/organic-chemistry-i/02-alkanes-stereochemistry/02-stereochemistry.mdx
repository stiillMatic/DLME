# Chirality and Stereoisomerism

## The Big Picture

Some molecules are **chiral** — they exist in two non-superimposable mirror-image forms (like your hands). The two forms (**enantiomers**) have identical physical properties except for how they rotate polarized light and how they interact with other chiral things. In biology, this asymmetry is everywhere — natural amino acids are all "L," sugars are mostly "D," and drug enantiomers can have completely different effects.

## Chirality

A **chiral center** in organic chemistry is typically a carbon with **four different** substituents. Such a carbon is **stereogenic**.

A molecule is **chiral** if it has no improper rotation axis (most commonly, no mirror plane).

## Enantiomers vs Diastereomers

| Type | Relationship |
|---|---|
| Enantiomers | Non-superimposable mirror images |
| Diastereomers | Stereoisomers that are not mirror images |
| Constitutional isomers | Different atom connectivity |

For $n$ chiral centers, up to $2^n$ stereoisomers possible (often fewer due to symmetry — e.g., meso compounds).

## R/S Notation (Cahn-Ingold-Prelog)

1. Rank the four substituents by priority (higher atomic number = higher rank).
2. Orient the molecule with lowest-priority group pointing away.
3. Look at remaining three: clockwise = **R**; counterclockwise = **S**.

## Optical Activity

Chiral molecules rotate plane-polarized light. Two enantiomers rotate in opposite directions.

- (+) or **d**-: clockwise (dextrorotatory)
- (−) or **l**-: counterclockwise (levorotatory)

Optical rotation magnitude depends on concentration, path length, wavelength, temperature.

Note: (+) vs (−) is independent of R/S — must measure empirically.

## Biological Significance

Most biomolecules are chiral. Cells produce and recognize only one enantiomer:
- Amino acids: L-form (almost all, glycine is achiral)
- Sugars: D-form (D-glucose dominant in metabolism)
- DNA helix: right-handed

This homochirality is one of biology's deepest mysteries.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Chiral = asymmetric."**

Chirality requires absence of *improper symmetry elements* (mirror planes, inversion centers). A molecule can have *some* symmetry and still be chiral.

**Misconception 2: "All chiral molecules have a stereogenic center."**

Some chiral molecules have axial chirality (allenes, biaryls) or planar chirality (substituted ferrocenes) without a single stereogenic carbon.

**Misconception 3: "Enantiomers have identical properties."**

Identical in **achiral** environments — same melting point, boiling point, density. Different in **chiral** environments: optical rotation, biological activity, interactions with other chiral molecules.

## Engineering Connection

- **Drug development:** thalidomide tragedy showed enantiomers can have opposite effects (one sedative, one teratogenic).
- **Pharmaceutical manufacturing:** chiral synthesis or chiral chromatography to separate enantiomers.
- **Asymmetric catalysis:** Nobel-prize-winning catalysts that produce one enantiomer selectively.
- **Flavor and fragrance:** (R)-carvone smells like spearmint, (S)-carvone smells like caraway. Same molecule, different smell!
- **Liquid crystals (LCDs):** cholesteric phases use chirality to twist molecules.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Is 2-bromobutane chiral?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
C2 has four substituents: H, Br, CH₃, CH₂CH₃ — all different. **Yes, chiral**.

Exists as (R)- and (S)-2-bromobutane enantiomers.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Is 2-bromopropane chiral?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
C2 has H, Br, CH₃, CH₃ — two methyls identical. **Not chiral**.

No stereogenic center. The molecule has a mirror plane.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Lactic acid CH₃CH(OH)COOH. Identify the stereocenter and assign priorities.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Central C has: OH, COOH, CH₃, H.

Priorities (by atomic number / mass): OH > COOH (O > C, but at first atom both 6... wait COOH starts with C-OOH, OH starts with O). Actually: OH has O directly; COOH starts with C. So OH > COOH.

Priorities: 1. OH 2. COOH 3. CH₃ 4. H.

Orient with H behind; if 1→2→3 is clockwise: R-lactic acid. Counterclockwise: S-lactic acid.

(Natural lactic acid in muscles is L = S form.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Drug enantiomers.** Ibuprofen is sold as a racemic mixture. Only one enantiomer is active. Why is it still effective?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
S-ibuprofen is the active form (binds to COX enzyme). R-ibuprofen is less active.

Fortunately, the body **interconverts** R-ibuprofen to S-ibuprofen via metabolism. So racemic mixture works.

For drugs without such interconversion (like thalidomide), enantioselective synthesis or separation is critical.

This biological metabolism is part of "chiral switching" — converting racemic drugs to single-enantiomer products.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Two enantiomers have the same density, boiling point, and most physical properties. Yet they can taste different. Why?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Taste receptors are **chiral proteins**. They distinguish enantiomers because protein binding sites are themselves asymmetric.

When (R) molecule binds, makes specific contacts. (S) molecule cannot match the same contacts in the chiral pocket.

This is why (R)-carvone smells like spearmint and (S)-carvone like caraway — same atoms, different shape, different receptor interaction.

Biology is chiral throughout; achiral measurements miss the most relevant differences.
</ExA>
