# Carbocation Stability and Rearrangements

## The Big Picture

A **carbocation** is a positively charged carbon — short on electrons, very reactive. The stability of a carbocation determines whether SN1/E1 reactions can happen at all, and what side products to expect. Carbocations sometimes **rearrange** — migrating hydrogens or methyl groups to form more stable species — leading to unexpected products. Recognizing rearrangement-prone substrates is a key skill.

## Stability Order

$$\text{tertiary} > \text{secondary} > \text{primary} \gg \text{methyl}$$

Special: **allyl** (CH₂=CHCH₂⁺) and **benzyl** (PhCH₂⁺) cations are stabilized by resonance — comparable to or exceeding tertiary stability.

## Why Tertiary is More Stable

Two effects:

**1. Inductive effect:** alkyl groups are slightly electron-donating, partially neutralizing positive charge.

**2. Hyperconjugation:** adjacent C–H σ bonds overlap with empty p orbital on cation, donating electron density.

More adjacent alkyl groups → more hyperconjugation and induction → more stable cation.

## Resonance Stabilization

Allyl cation: $\text{CH}_2=\text{CHCH}_2^+ \leftrightarrow \text{CH}_2^+\text{-CH=CH}_2$.

Charge delocalized over two carbons. Each "resonance structure" contributes ~50%. Highly stabilized.

Benzyl: charge delocalized into the aromatic ring (4 resonance structures).

## Rearrangements

Less stable cations sometimes rearrange to more stable ones via:

**Hydride shift:** an H with bonding electrons moves to adjacent C⁺.
**Methyl shift:** a CH₃ with bonding electrons moves.

Both happen on the picosecond timescale.

**Example:** 2° cation adjacent to 3° carbon often rearranges:

CH₃-CH⁺-C(CH₃)₃ → CH₃-CH₂-C⁺(CH₃)₃ (no, that's not stable... wait — the 3° one would need to lose H)

Actually: in (CH₃)₂CH-CH⁺-CH₃ (a 2°), a hydride from adjacent C can migrate to form (CH₃)₂C⁺-CH₂-CH₃ (3°) — more stable.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Methyl shifts are rare."**

Common in SN1/E1 conditions where rearrangement to tertiary is thermodynamically favored.

**Misconception 2: "Rearrangement only happens slowly."**

Migrations are fast (picosecond) — happens DURING the reaction, not after. Products reflect the rearranged cation.

**Misconception 3: "Stable cations don't rearrange."**

Tertiary cations are stable — no driver to rearrange (no better cation available). They proceed to product directly.

## Engineering Connection

- **Petroleum refining:** carbocation rearrangements in catalytic cracking convert straight-chain to branched alkanes (higher octane).
- **Synthetic chemistry:** anticipating rearrangements avoids unwanted products.
- **Terpene biosynthesis:** all-trans isoprene polymerization with cation rearrangements builds complex natural products (steroids, terpenes).
- **Acid-catalyzed processes:** ethanol → ethylene, isomerization of alkanes, etc.
- **Friedel-Crafts alkylation:** carbocation electrophile rearranges, leading to mixtures.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Rank: primary, secondary, tertiary, allylic cation by stability.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Allylic ≈ tertiary > secondary > primary.

(Resonance can rival or beat hyperconjugation/induction.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Will 1-bromo-2,2-dimethylpropane undergo SN1?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Primary substrate → SN1 would form primary cation → unstable.

BUT: the cation can rearrange. After ionization, hydride or methyl shift from the adjacent C (which has 3 methyls) gives a tertiary cation.

So: ionization is followed by **rapid rearrangement**, then nucleophilic attack → rearranged products.

This is the classic "1-bromo-2,2-dimethylpropane" gotcha — looks like it shouldn't undergo SN1, but does via rearrangement.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** What product when 3-methyl-2-bromobutane treated with H₂O in heat?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Original cation: 2°, adjacent to 3° carbon. Hydride shift to give 3° cation:

CH₃-CH(Br)-CH(CH₃)₂ → CH₃-CH⁺-CH(CH₃)₂ → (CH₃)₃C⁺-CH₃ (after shift).

Water attacks the more stable 3° cation → CH₃-C(OH)(CH₃)-CH₃ (2-methyl-2-butanol).

Major product is **rearranged**, not the direct SN1 product (2-methyl-3-butanol).

Diagnostic: organic chemistry exam favorite.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Octane improvement.** Why are branched alkanes (e.g., isooctane) preferred to straight-chain alkanes (n-octane) in gasoline?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Octane rating measures resistance to engine knock (premature detonation).

Branched alkanes burn more uniformly → higher octane rating (isooctane = 100, n-octane = −20).

In refining, **isomerization** uses acid catalysis. Straight-chain alkanes form secondary cations that rearrange (hydride/methyl shifts) to branched isomers.

Lewis-acid catalysts (zeolites) accelerate cation chemistry in industrial reactors. Modern gasoline depends on this.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** A reaction gives an unexpected product. How can you tell if rearrangement is responsible?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Compare expected (direct) product carbon skeleton with observed.

Rearrangement signs:
- **Carbon skeleton changed**: methyl group has shifted positions.
- **More-substituted center has new bond**: cation went to tertiary or stable spot.

Other tests:
- Isotope labeling (where did each H/C end up?)
- Trapping with fast nucleophiles can intercept primary cation before rearrangement.

Rearrangement is the "smoking gun" for cation intermediates. SN2 (concerted, no cation) doesn't show this behavior.
</ExA>
