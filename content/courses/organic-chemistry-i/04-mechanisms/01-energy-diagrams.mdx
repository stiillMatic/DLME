# Energy Diagrams and Transition States

## The Big Picture

A **reaction coordinate diagram** plots energy as the system moves from reactants to products. Peaks are **transition states** (high-energy bottlenecks); valleys are **intermediates** (metastable species). The shape of this diagram tells you everything about reaction rate, selectivity, and reversibility. Reading energy diagrams fluently is a core organic chemistry skill.

## Anatomy of a Diagram

- **x-axis**: reaction coordinate (progress from reactants to products)
- **y-axis**: free energy $G$

Key features:
- **Reactants and products**: minima at start and end
- **Transition state (‡)**: top of barrier
- **Intermediates**: minima between transition states
- **Activation energy** $E_a$: from reactants to TS
- **Reaction energy** $\Delta G$: from reactants to products

## Single-step vs Multi-step

**SN2 (concerted):** one TS, no intermediate.
**SN1 (stepwise):** two TS's, one intermediate (carbocation).

For multi-step:
- **Rate-determining step (RDS)**: the slowest step (highest TS).
- Pre-equilibrium can occur if barrier returning is similar or higher.

## Arrhenius Equation

$$k = A e^{-E_a/RT}$$

Rate constant grows exponentially with temperature. Rule of thumb: rate doubles per 10 K increase (for $E_a \sim 50$ kJ/mol).

Logarithmic form: $\ln k = \ln A - E_a/(RT)$. Plot $\ln k$ vs $1/T$ to extract $E_a$.

## Hammond Postulate

The TS resembles whichever side is closer in energy:
- **Exothermic reaction**: TS resembles reactants ("early TS").
- **Endothermic reaction**: TS resembles products ("late TS").

For SN1: cation intermediate is endothermic → TS resembles cation. Carbocation stability determines TS stability determines rate. This is why tertiary substrates accelerate SN1.

## Kinetic vs Thermodynamic Control

When multiple products form:
- **Kinetic control**: lower-barrier product dominates at low T, short time.
- **Thermodynamic control**: lower-energy product dominates at high T, equilibrium.

Often different! Classic example: Diels-Alder selectivity, 1,2 vs 1,4 addition.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "$E_a$ is the energy stored in the bond."**

$E_a$ is the energy needed to **reach the transition state** — the bond energy needed to break partially as the new bond forms.

**Misconception 2: "Lower $\Delta G$ = faster reaction."**

$\Delta G$ controls equilibrium, $E_a$ controls rate. An exergonic reaction can be slow if barrier is high; an endergonic reaction can be fast if barrier is low.

**Misconception 3: "Catalysts change $\Delta G$."**

Catalysts lower $E_a$ but **don't change** $\Delta G$ — they affect rate, not equilibrium. Both forward and reverse rates accelerate equally.

## Engineering Connection

- **Process design:** target the rate-determining step for optimization.
- **Catalysis:** lower the activation energy of the RDS → faster turnover.
- **Selectivity:** parallel pathways with different barriers → use temperature/conditions to favor desired product.
- **Reaction safety:** know exothermic reactions and their activation barriers to prevent runaway.
- **Drug stability:** Arrhenius extrapolation from accelerated aging studies predicts shelf life.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Rate constant doubles when T increases from 300 K to 310 K. $E_a$?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Arrhenius ratio: $\ln 2 = E_a/R\cdot(1/300 - 1/310)/$ ... 

$E_a = R\ln 2/(1/300 - 1/310) = 8.314\cdot 0.693/(0.0001075) \approx 53600$ J/mol = 53.6 kJ/mol.

Typical for organic reactions.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** SN1 reaction: which is rate-determining, ionization or nucleophile attack?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**Ionization** (carbocation formation) is the slow, rate-determining step. Carbocation is high energy, so the barrier to form it is large.

Nucleophile attack on the cation is fast (the cation is reactive).

This is why SN1 rate is independent of nucleophile concentration — the RDS doesn't involve the nucleophile.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3. Engineering: Drug shelf life.** A drug degrades at rate $k = 10^{-7}$ s⁻¹ at 25°C, $E_a = 80$ kJ/mol. Time to 10% degradation at 4°C (refrigerated)?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Find $k$ at 4°C (277 K): $\ln(k_2/k_1) = -E_a/R(1/T_2 - 1/T_1)$.

$1/277 - 1/298 = 2.55\times 10^{-4}$.

$\ln(k_2/k_1) = -80000/8.314\cdot 2.55\times 10^{-4} \approx -2.45$.

$k_2/k_1 = e^{-2.45} \approx 0.086$.

$k_2 \approx 8.6\times 10^{-9}$ s⁻¹.

Time for 10% degradation: $0.1/k_2 \approx 1.16\times 10^7$ s ≈ 4.5 months.

Refrigeration extends life ~12× (would have been ~12 days at 25°C). Why drugs are stored cold.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Conceptual.** Why doesn't increasing temperature always increase yield?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Higher T speeds up **all** reactions — including reverse and competing reactions.

If desired product is kinetically favored but thermodynamically unstable, higher T can flip selectivity to a competing thermodynamic product.

Also: higher T shifts unfavorable equilibria (Le Chatelier) → may decrease yield of exergonic products.

Process engineers balance T for: rate (need it high enough), selectivity (avoid side products), and equilibrium yield (don't push wrong direction).
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5.** A reaction has two parallel paths: kinetic product with $E_a = 50$ kJ/mol, thermodynamic with $E_a = 80$ kJ/mol but lower final energy. At low T and short time, which dominates?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**Kinetic** (lower $E_a$ → faster path). At low T, kinetic product is "trapped" because reverse reactions and equilibration are slow.

At high T or long times, thermodynamic product becomes accessible — system equilibrates to lower-energy product.

This is **kinetic vs thermodynamic control**. Classic example: bromination of cyclopentadiene gives 1,2-product (kinetic) at low T, 1,4-product (thermodynamic) at high T.
</ExA>
