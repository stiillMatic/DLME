# Chapter 4 Practice — Mechanisms

<ExQ>
**1.** Rate at 25°C is 0.001 s⁻¹. At 75°C, with $E_a = 100$ kJ/mol?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\ln(k_2/k_1) = (100000/8.314)(1/298 - 1/348) = 12033\cdot 4.83\times 10^{-4} = 5.81$.

$k_2/k_1 = e^{5.81} \approx 333$.

$k_2 \approx 0.333$ s⁻¹. 333× faster.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Identify rate-determining step of E1 mechanism.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**Ionization** (loss of leaving group to form carbocation) — same as SN1.

Subsequent base removal of β-H is fast on the reactive cation.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Most stable: methyl, vinyl, allyl, benzyl cation?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**Benzyl** — extensive resonance into aromatic ring (4 resonance structures).

Benzyl > allyl > methyl > vinyl. (Vinyl cation is **very** unstable; vinyl substrates rarely undergo SN1/E1.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Catalyst design.** A homogeneous catalyst lowers $E_a$ from 90 kJ/mol to 50 kJ/mol at 60°C. Rate enhancement factor?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$k_{\text{cat}}/k_{\text{uncat}} = \exp((90-50)\cdot 1000/(8.314\cdot 333)) = \exp(14.45) \approx 1.9\times 10^6$.

**~2 million × faster.** Lowering $E_a$ by 40 kJ/mol is huge — catalyst makes industrially impractical reaction feasible.

Typical industrial catalysts target 30-60 kJ/mol barrier reduction.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why are zeolites used as catalysts in oil refining?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Zeolites have:
- **Acidic sites** that generate carbocations from alkanes.
- **Shape-selective pores** that allow only certain molecular sizes/shapes through.

So they catalyze hydrocarbon rearrangements (branching, cracking) with selectivity for specific products.

The combination: acid + selectivity = isooctane production at refinery scale. Without zeolites, modern gasoline economy would be impossible.

Zeolites also catalyze SN1-like reactions inside their pores — a classic case of "designed environment for chemistry."
</ExA>
