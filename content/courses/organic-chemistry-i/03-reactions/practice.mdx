# Chapter 3 Practice — Substitution and Elimination

<ExQ>
**1.** CH₃CH₂Br + NaOH (in DMSO, cold). Mechanism, product, stereo?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Primary substrate, strong nucleophile, polar aprotic, cold → **SN2**.

Product: CH₃CH₂OH (ethanol). No stereochemistry issue (primary, no stereocenter).
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Cyclohexyl bromide + NaOH/EtOH, heat. Mechanism, product?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Secondary, strong base, heat → **E2**.

Product: cyclohexene. One major product (only one type of β-H, all equivalent).

(Some SN2 product — cyclohexanol — also forms.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Methylcyclohexyl bromide where Br is axial. With NaOH/heat. Major product?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
E2 requires anti-periplanar H to Br. Only axial H's on adjacent carbons fit.

If methyl is also on a neighboring carbon, can lose H there only if that H is axial (which depends on methyl position).

If H next to methyl is anti-periplanar, gives more substituted alkene (Zaitsev).

This is a classic ring-stereo example — the Br geometry determines accessible products.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Pharmaceutical synthesis.** A drug substrate with leaving group, secondary chiral carbon, treated with strong nucleophile in polar aprotic solvent. What's the stereochemical outcome?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Conditions favor SN2 → backside attack → **inversion** of configuration at the chiral carbon.

For pharmaceutical applications: enables converting one enantiomer to the other via inversion (after introducing leaving group with retention).

Combined with chiral pool synthesis, this is a common strategy to access either drug enantiomer.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why don't tertiary alcohols undergo SN2?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Three alkyl groups around the tertiary carbon block backside approach of the nucleophile. Steric hindrance makes SN2 transition state energetically inaccessible.

Plus: tertiary substrates form stable carbocations → SN1 takes over (if leaving group is good).

So tertiary alcohols (after activation to tosylate or via H⁺) follow SN1/E1 pathways, not SN2.

This is why drug syntheses involving tertiary stereocenters typically use different strategies (asymmetric catalysis, stereospecific elimination/addition).
</ExA>
