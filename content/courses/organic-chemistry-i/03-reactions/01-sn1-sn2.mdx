# SN1 and SN2 Mechanisms

## The Big Picture

**Nucleophilic substitution** (SN) is one of the most fundamental reactions in organic chemistry — a nucleophile (electron-rich) attacks a carbon, kicking out a leaving group (electron-poor). The two mechanisms (SN1 and SN2) compete based on substrate structure, nucleophile, solvent, and leaving group. Understanding when each happens lets you predict rates, products, and stereochemistry.

## SN2: Bimolecular, Concerted

**One step:**
- Nucleophile attacks carbon from back side (opposite to leaving group)
- Bond forms while bond breaks
- Stereochemistry: **inversion** (Walden inversion)

Kinetics: $\text{rate} = k[\text{substrate}][\text{Nu}^-]$ — **second order**.

**Favored when:**
- **Methyl, primary substrates** (steric access)
- Strong nucleophile
- Polar aprotic solvent (DMSO, DMF, acetone)
- Good leaving group (I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻ > F⁻)

## SN1: Unimolecular, Stepwise

**Two steps:**
1. Substrate ionizes: leaving group departs, forming a **carbocation**.
2. Nucleophile attacks carbocation (from either face → racemic product).

Stereochemistry: **racemization** (loss of chirality), since carbocation is planar (sp²).

Kinetics: $\text{rate} = k[\text{substrate}]$ — **first order**.

**Favored when:**
- **Tertiary substrates** (stable carbocation)
- Weak nucleophile
- Polar protic solvent (water, alcohol)
- Good leaving group

## Substrate Reactivity

| Substrate | SN1 rate | SN2 rate |
|---|---|---|
| Methyl | Very slow | Very fast |
| Primary | Slow | Fast |
| Secondary | Moderate | Moderate |
| Tertiary | Fast | Very slow (steric block) |

Methyl: SN2 only. Tertiary: SN1 only. Secondary: depends on conditions.

## Carbocation Stability

Tertiary > Secondary > Primary > Methyl

Hyperconjugation (overlap with C–H σ bonds on adjacent carbons) and inductive effects stabilize carbocations with more alkyl groups.

This is why SN1 needs ≥ secondary substrate to proceed.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "SN1 always gives racemic products."**

Most cases yes, but **ion pairs** can persist briefly, leading to partial inversion (more inversion than retention). Pure racemic outcome only for truly free carbocations.

**Misconception 2: "Stronger nucleophile = faster reaction always."**

For SN2, yes. For SN1, **nucleophile doesn't appear in the rate law** — strength doesn't affect rate. (It does affect product, especially in mixtures.)

**Misconception 3: "All SN reactions are nucleophilic."**

Some "substitution" reactions are electrophilic (e.g., aromatic substitution). SN refers specifically to nucleophilic attack on sp³ carbon with departing leaving group.

## Engineering Connection

- **Drug synthesis:** controlled stereochemistry via SN2 (specific stereochemistry needed for activity).
- **Polymer modification:** functional group installation via SN reactions on polymer backbones.
- **Solvent selection in chemical processes:** SN1 vs SN2 favored by different solvent types.
- **Green chemistry:** designing solvents to direct desired mechanism.
- **Biochemistry:** enzymatic SN2 reactions (e.g., methylation of DNA) are stereospecific.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** CH₃Br + OH⁻. Mechanism?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Methyl substrate, strong nucleophile → **SN2**.

Product: CH₃OH + Br⁻. Single transition state.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** (CH₃)₃C-Br + H₂O. Mechanism?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Tertiary, weak nucleophile, protic solvent → **SN1**.

Step 1: (CH₃)₃C⁺ + Br⁻.
Step 2: (CH₃)₃C⁺ + H₂O → (CH₃)₃C-OH₂⁺ → (CH₃)₃C-OH + H⁺.

Product: (CH₃)₃C-OH (tert-butanol). Racemic if substrate was chiral.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3. Stereochemistry.** (R)-2-bromobutane + I⁻ → ? (with strong nucleophile, polar aprotic solvent)
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Conditions favor SN2 (secondary, strong nucleophile).

SN2 inverts: (R)-substrate → (S)-product.

Product: (S)-2-iodobutane. Note the inversion of priority due to halogen change can complicate R/S assignment — check carefully.

(Actually, with priorities I > Br: if substrate was R, the product configuration is inverted but the descriptor might still be S or R depending on priority change.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Pharma scale-up.** A drug synthesis step is SN1 on a chiral substrate, but the desired product is one specific enantiomer. What does this imply for the process?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
SN1 produces racemic mixture. To get single enantiomer:

1. **Use enantioselective conditions** (chiral catalyst): forces preferential attack from one face.
2. **Separate enantiomers** afterward via chiral chromatography or crystallization with chiral resolving agent.
3. **Switch to SN2** (if substrate allows): preserves stereochemistry with inversion.

Industrial practice often involves option 3 — redesigning the substrate to enable SN2 chemistry. Stereochemistry control is a top consideration in drug manufacturing.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why does SN2 require back-side attack?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
The nucleophile's lone pair attacks the σ* (antibonding) orbital of the C–LG bond. This orbital is concentrated on the back side of the C, opposite to the leaving group.

Front-side attack would mean overlap with the bonding orbital (electron density) — repulsive.

Geometric constraint: 180° angle between Nu and LG → Walden inversion (like an umbrella flipping inside out).

This rigid geometry is why SN2 stereochemistry is so predictable, and why SN2 fails on tertiary substrates (back side blocked by alkyl groups).
</ExA>
