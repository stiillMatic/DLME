# E1 and E2 Eliminations

## The Big Picture

**Elimination** reactions are the alternative to substitution: instead of the nucleophile attaching to carbon, a **base** removes an H from a β-carbon while the leaving group departs, forming a double bond (alkene). Like substitution, there are two mechanisms (E1 and E2) competing based on conditions. SN and E reactions often compete — and the conditions you choose determine which dominates.

## E2: Bimolecular, Concerted

**One step:**
- Base removes β-H (the one adjacent to the C bearing the leaving group)
- Leaving group departs
- Both happen in one concerted step → π bond forms

**Stereochemistry:** anti-periplanar — H and LG must be on opposite sides with their bonds parallel.

Kinetics: $\text{rate} = k[\text{substrate}][\text{base}]$ — **second order**.

**Favored:** strong base, hot, secondary or tertiary substrate.

## E1: Unimolecular, Stepwise

**Two steps:**
1. Substrate ionizes (same as SN1): leaving group departs, carbocation forms.
2. Base removes β-H from carbocation → alkene.

Kinetics: $\text{rate} = k[\text{substrate}]$ — **first order**.

**Favored:** weak base, polar protic solvent, tertiary substrate.

## Zaitsev's Rule

When multiple β-hydrogens are available, elimination usually gives the **more substituted** alkene (thermodynamically more stable, more hyperconjugation).

Exception (Hofmann's rule): bulky bases (e.g., t-butoxide) sterically hindered → remove less hindered H → less substituted alkene.

## Competition: SN1/E1 and SN2/E2

| Substrate | Strong base | Weak base/nucleophile |
|---|---|---|
| Primary | SN2 | (slow SN2) |
| Secondary | E2 | SN1/E1 mixture |
| Tertiary | E2 | E1/SN1 |

Heat favors elimination (entropy + breaking C-H + C-LG bonds).

Cold favors substitution.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Same substrate gives same product."**

Conditions matter enormously. (CH₃)₃CBr with cold NaOH gives mostly substitution (t-BuOH); same substrate with hot NaOEt gives mostly elimination (isobutylene).

**Misconception 2: "E2 requires base."**

E2 needs a base (to remove the β-H). But sometimes the same species (e.g., OH⁻) can act as nucleophile (SN2) or base (E2) — substrate determines which wins.

**Misconception 3: "Zaitsev always wins."**

Bulky bases follow Hofmann's rule (less substituted alkene). Steric effects flip the selectivity.

## Engineering Connection

- **Petroleum cracking:** elimination reactions break alkanes into alkenes (feedstocks for polymers).
- **Drug synthesis:** elimination steps introduce double bonds critical for biological activity.
- **Industrial dehydration of alcohols:** acid-catalyzed E1 produces alkenes (ethanol → ethylene).
- **Polymer functionalization:** controlled elimination creates functional groups for further chemistry.
- **Biofuel from cellulosic ethanol:** dehydration step converts ethanol to ethylene for petrochemicals.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** 2-bromobutane + KOH (in ethanol, heat). Major mechanism and product?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Secondary substrate, strong base, heat → **E2**.

Possible alkenes:
- 1-butene (CH₂=CH-CH₂-CH₃) — terminal, less substituted.
- 2-butene (CH₃-CH=CH-CH₃) — internal, more substituted.

By Zaitsev: **2-butene** is the major product (more substituted).
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Tert-butyl chloride in ethanol (mild conditions). Mechanism?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Tertiary substrate, weak base, polar protic → mixture of **SN1** and **E1**.

SN1: tert-butyl ethyl ether.
E1: isobutylene (2-methylpropene).

Ratio depends on temperature. Higher T → more elimination.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** 2-bromopentane + bulky base (t-BuO⁻). Predict major product.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Hofmann elimination — bulky base favors less substituted alkene.

Possible products: 1-pentene (terminal) or 2-pentene (internal).

Bulky base prefers to remove the more accessible terminal H → **1-pentene major**.

Standard "Hofmann switch" with t-BuOK or LDA bases.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Industrial ethylene from ethanol.** Dehydration of ethanol at 200°C with H₂SO₄. Mechanism, product?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Acid catalysis: protonate OH (good leaving group H₂O), then E1.

Step 1: CH₃CH₂OH + H⁺ → CH₃CH₂-OH₂⁺.
Step 2: ionize → CH₃CH₂⁺ (primary cation, somewhat unstable but possible at high T).
Step 3: lose β-H → CH₂=CH₂ (ethylene).

This is essentially how ethylene is made from biomass-derived ethanol — a major industrial process for "green plastics."
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why does E2 require anti-periplanar arrangement of H and leaving group?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
For concerted elimination, the σ C-H bond and σ* C-LG orbital must align for π bond formation.

Anti-periplanar (180°) gives optimal alignment: parallel orbitals overlap into the forming π bond.

Other arrangements (gauche, eclipsed) don't permit smooth orbital overlap → much higher barrier.

This is why cyclohexane elimination requires axial H and axial LG (both diaxial) — locked anti-periplanar geometry. Equatorial substituents must ring-flip to react.

This stereoelectronic requirement is a powerful predictor of organic reactivity.
</ExA>
