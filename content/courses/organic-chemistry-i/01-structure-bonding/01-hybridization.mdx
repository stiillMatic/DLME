# Hybridization and Molecular Geometry

## The Big Picture

Carbon's ability to form four bonds with diverse geometries is what makes organic chemistry possible. **Hybridization** explains how atomic orbitals (s, p, d) mix to produce the geometries we observe: tetrahedral, trigonal planar, linear. Understanding hybridization lets you predict shapes, bond angles, and reactivity of any organic molecule from first principles.

## The Three Main Hybridizations of Carbon

| Hybridization | Geometry | Bond angle | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| sp³ | Tetrahedral | 109.5° | Methane CH₄, ethane C-C |
| sp² | Trigonal planar | 120° | Ethylene C=C, benzene |
| sp | Linear | 180° | Acetylene C≡C, CO₂ |

The number of orbitals mixed = the number of $\sigma$-bonded substituents (or lone pairs counted as substituents).

## How Hybridization Works

Carbon has electronic configuration 1s² 2s² 2p². The 2s and 2p orbitals are nondegenerate (different energies). But when bonded to substituents, **mixing** lowers the total energy:

- **sp³**: one s + three p orbitals → four equivalent sp³ orbitals (each 25% s, 75% p)
- **sp²**: one s + two p → three sp² orbitals (33% s, 67% p) + one unhybridized p
- **sp**: one s + one p → two sp orbitals (50% s, 50% p) + two unhybridized p

## Sigma vs Pi Bonds

- **σ bonds**: head-on overlap of hybrid orbitals — strong, rotation-free.
- **π bonds**: side-by-side overlap of unhybridized p orbitals — weaker than σ, but lock rotation.

- Single bond (C–C): 1 σ
- Double bond (C=C): 1 σ + 1 π
- Triple bond (C≡C): 1 σ + 2 π

## VSEPR Theory

**Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion**: electron pairs (bonding + lone) arrange to maximize separation.

| Pairs | Geometry | Angle |
|---|---|---|
| 2 | Linear | 180° |
| 3 | Trigonal planar | 120° |
| 4 | Tetrahedral | 109.5° |
| 5 | Trigonal bipyramidal | 90°, 120° |
| 6 | Octahedral | 90° |

Lone pairs occupy more space than bonding pairs → smaller actual bond angles in NH₃, H₂O (~107°, 104.5° instead of 109.5°).

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Carbon is always sp³."**

Carbon adopts whatever hybridization minimizes energy for the bonding situation. Aromatic carbons are sp²; carbynes are sp.

**Misconception 2: "Hybridization is observed."**

Hybridization is a mental/mathematical model. The actual electron density is the observable, and hybrid orbitals are a convenient basis. Other bases (delocalized MOs) work just as well — and sometimes better.

**Misconception 3: "Bond angles match VSEPR exactly."**

VSEPR is approximate. Real molecules have lone pair-bond pair repulsions, electronegativity effects, ring strain, etc. that distort ideal angles.

## Engineering Connection

- **Diamond:** all-sp³ carbon → tetrahedral 3D network → hardness and high thermal conductivity.
- **Graphene:** all-sp² carbon → planar hexagonal → unique electrical properties.
- **Carbon nanotubes:** rolled-up graphene → sp² framework with mechanical strength.
- **Drug design:** molecule shape (set by hybridization) determines binding to receptors.
- **Polymer properties:** chain conformations depend on sp³ (flexible) vs sp²/aromatic (rigid).
- **Solar cells (organic):** π-systems in conjugated polymers conduct electrons.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Hybridization of carbon in CO₂?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
CO₂ is O=C=O — central C has two σ bonds (to oxygens). **sp hybridized**, linear (180°).
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Hybridization of carbon in benzene (C₆H₆)?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Each C has 3 σ bonds (to 2 neighbors and 1 H). **sp²**. The remaining p orbital perpendicular to ring forms delocalized π system (aromaticity).

Bond angle: 120°.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Hybridization of nitrogen in NH₃?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
N has 3 σ bonds + 1 lone pair = 4 "groups". **sp³**.

Geometry: trigonal pyramidal (109.5° ideal, but lone pair compresses to ~107°).
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Carbon nanotube.** Single-walled CNT contains sp² carbons in honeycomb lattice. Why does this give exceptional mechanical strength?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
sp² carbons form strong σ bonds in plane (similar to graphene), and the π system adds delocalization energy.

The hexagonal arrangement is highly symmetric and load-distributing. Young's modulus ~1 TPa — among the strongest materials known.

Mechanically, the curvature into a tube doesn't disrupt the sp² σ framework significantly for reasonable diameters. Hence the structural strength.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why can't ethylene (C₂H₄) rotate freely about its C=C double bond?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Double bond = 1 σ + 1 π. The π bond requires parallel p orbitals; rotating one C relative to the other breaks this overlap.

Energy to break π bond: ~270 kJ/mol — much more than thermal energy. So free rotation is hindered, and **cis/trans isomers** exist for substituted alkenes.

This is why retinal (in vision) and DNA chemistry depend so heavily on alkene geometry.
</ExA>
