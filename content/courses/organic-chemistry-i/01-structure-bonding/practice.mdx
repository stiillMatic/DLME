# Chapter 1 Practice — Structure and Bonding

<ExQ>
**1.** Hybridization of all carbons in propene (CH₃CH=CH₂)?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
CH₃ (terminal): sp³ (4 σ bonds).
CH=CH₂: both sp² (3 σ bonds each, 1 π in double bond).
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Predict geometry of SF₄ (sulfur with 4 F and 1 lone pair).
</ExQ>
<ExA>
5 electron pairs → trigonal bipyramidal electron geometry. Lone pair in equatorial position.

Molecular shape: **see-saw**.

(Lone pair takes equatorial slot to minimize repulsion.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Draw all major resonance structures of formate (HCOO⁻).
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Like acetate but with H instead of CH₃. Two equivalent structures with C=O and –O⁻ alternating.

Hybrid: H–C with both C-O equivalent.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Conjugated polymer.** Polyacetylene (CH=CH)ₙ shows electrical conductivity. Why is its conductivity higher than polyethylene (CH₂CH₂)ₙ?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Polyacetylene: alternating single/double bonds = conjugation. Resonance delocalizes π electrons along chain → low-energy electrons in extended π system → conductive when doped.

Polyethylene: saturated sp³ carbons, all electrons localized in σ bonds. Insulator.

This is the basis of organic semiconductors and OLEDs. Discovery led to 2000 Nobel Prize.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why does sp² benzene contribute to its planarity?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
sp² hybridization places 3 σ orbitals in a plane (120° angles). The unhybridized p orbital is perpendicular to this plane.

For π overlap to occur in the ring, all p orbitals must be parallel — requires planar geometry. The molecule "chooses" planarity to maximize π bonding (aromatic stabilization).

Non-planar conformations sacrifice π overlap energy. So benzene is essentially flat.

This is why aromatic rings are typically planar — geometry is dictated by orbital overlap requirements.
</ExA>
