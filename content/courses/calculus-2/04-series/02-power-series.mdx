# Power Series and Radius of Convergence

## The Big Picture

A **power series** is a function defined by an infinite series in powers of $(x - c)$:

$$f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n(x - c)^n$$

The set of $x$ where it converges is an **interval of convergence**, with **radius of convergence** $R$. Power series are the natural way to define special functions ($e^x$, $\sin x$, Bessel functions) and the foundation of analytic methods in PDEs and complex analysis.

## Radius of Convergence

Apply ratio test to $|a_{n+1}(x-c)/a_n|$:

$$|x - c|\lim|a_{n+1}/a_n| < 1$$

So $R = \lim|a_n/a_{n+1}|$ (if the limit exists).

The series converges absolutely for $|x - c| < R$ and diverges for $|x - c| > R$. Endpoints $|x-c| = R$ require separate analysis.

## Examples

| Series | $a_n$ | $R$ |
|---|---|---|
| $\sum x^n$ | 1 | 1 |
| $\sum x^n/n!$ | $1/n!$ | $\infty$ |
| $\sum x^n/n$ | $1/n$ | 1 |
| $\sum n!\,x^n$ | $n!$ | 0 |

The last has radius zero ÔÇö converges only at $x = 0$.

## Taylor and Maclaurin Series

If $f$ is infinitely differentiable at $c$:

$$f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{f^{(n)}(c)}{n!}(x - c)^n$$

(Maclaurin: $c = 0$.)

When this converges to $f$ inside its radius of convergence, $f$ is **analytic** at $c$.

## Operations on Power Series

Within their radius of convergence, power series can be:
- **Differentiated term-by-term**
- **Integrated term-by-term**
- **Multiplied** (Cauchy product)
- **Substituted** (e.g., replace $x$ with $x^2$)

These operations preserve the radius of convergence.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Power series equals function for all $x$ where defined."**

Only inside the radius of convergence. Outside, the series diverges and gives nonsense.

**Misconception 2: "Radius of convergence is symmetric ÔÇö converges in $|x| \leq R$."**

The *radius* $R$ is symmetric, but **endpoints** $\pm R$ may behave differently. Always check endpoints separately.

**Misconception 3: "All smooth functions equal their Taylor series."**

Some smooth functions ($e^{-1/x^2}$ at 0) have all derivatives zero but aren't zero ÔÇö Taylor series doesn't represent them. Such functions are **not analytic**.

## Engineering Connection

- **Special functions:** Bessel, Legendre, Hermite ÔÇö all defined via power series solutions to ODEs (Frobenius method).
- **Numerical computation:** $\sin x$, $\cos x$, $\ln$, $\arctan$ ÔÇö implemented by truncated Taylor series in math libraries.
- **Perturbation methods:** small-parameter expansions $u = u_0 + \varepsilon u_1 + \varepsilon^2 u_2 + \cdots$ are power series.
- **Control systems:** Pad├ę approximants and series expansions of transfer functions.
- **Quantum mechanics:** wavefunctions often expressed as power series with specific recurrences.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Find $R$ for $\sum x^n/(n+1)$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$|a_n/a_{n+1}| = (n+2)/(n+1) \to 1$. So $R = 1$.

Endpoints: $x = 1$: $\sum 1/(n+1)$ diverges (harmonic-like). $x = -1$: alternating, converges.

Interval of convergence: $[-1, 1)$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Find $R$ for $\sum (n!)^2 x^n / (2n)!$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$|a_n/a_{n+1}| = (2n+2)(2n+1)/((n+1)^2) \to 4$.

So $R = 4$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Find Maclaurin series of $f(x) = 1/(1-x^2)$ and its radius.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$1/(1-u) = \sum u^n$ for $|u| < 1$. Substitute $u = x^2$:

$f(x) = \sum x^{2n}$ for $|x^2| < 1$, i.e., $|x| < 1$.

Radius: $R = 1$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Solving ODEs.** Find power series solution of $y' - y = 0$, $y(0) = 1$, in form $y = \sum a_n x^n$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$y' = \sum n a_n x^{n-1} = y$ requires $a_n = a_{n-1}/n$.

With $a_0 = 1$: $a_n = 1/n!$.

$y = \sum x^n/n! = e^x$ Ôťô.

Power series methods solve ODEs algebraically ÔÇö basis of Frobenius method for variable-coefficient cases.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why are power series so important compared to other infinite series?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
- **Closed under calculus:** differentiation/integration term-by-term within $R$.
- **Define functions:** $e^x$, $\sin x$ etc are *defined* by power series.
- **Analytic continuation:** uniquely extend functions known on small regions.
- **Computation:** truncated power series give approximations with controlled error.

In physics/engineering, when an exact closed form is unavailable, power series often *is* the answer ÔÇö we just keep enough terms for required accuracy.
</ExA>
