# Parametric Curves and Their Calculus

## The Big Picture

Many curves can't be expressed as $y = f(x)$ — circles, helices, projectile paths, robot trajectories. **Parametric equations** describe curves by a third variable $t$ (often time): $x = x(t)$, $y = y(t)$. This view is more general and natural for motion. Calculus on parametric curves — slopes, arc length, areas — is essential for trajectory design, kinematics, and computer graphics.

## Parametric Form

A curve in 2D: $(x(t), y(t))$ for $t \in [a, b]$.

**Examples:**
- Line: $x = x_0 + at$, $y = y_0 + bt$.
- Circle: $x = r\cos t$, $y = r\sin t$.
- Cycloid (rolling circle): $x = r(t - \sin t)$, $y = r(1 - \cos t)$.
- Helix (3D): $x = r\cos t$, $y = r\sin t$, $z = ct$.

## Slope of Tangent

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}$$

(Chain rule, treating $t$ as the parameter and dividing.)

**Example:** Circle. $dx/dt = -r\sin t$, $dy/dt = r\cos t$. So $dy/dx = -\cos t/\sin t = -\cot t$.

## Arc Length

$$L = \int_a^b\sqrt{(dx/dt)^2 + (dy/dt)^2}\,dt$$

For circle of radius $r$ over $[0, 2\pi]$: $L = \int 0^{2\pi} r\,dt = 2\pi r$ ✓.

## Area Enclosed

For closed parametric curve traversed counterclockwise:

$$A = \frac{1}{2}\oint(x\,dy - y\,dx) = \frac{1}{2}\int_a^b(x\,\dot y - y\,\dot x)\,dt$$

(Green's theorem in disguise.)

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Parametric curves can always be converted to $y = f(x)$."**

Not always — circle would need $y = \pm\sqrt{R^2 - x^2}$, two branches. Spirals (e.g., logarithmic spiral) can't be expressed as a single $y = f(x)$ at all.

**Misconception 2: "Faster $t$ = different curve."**

Different parameterizations of the *same curve* trace it at different speeds. The geometric curve is unchanged. But arc length integral is invariant — any parameterization gives the same length.

**Misconception 3: "$dy/dx = (dy/dt)(dx/dt)$ — multiply, not divide."**

Divide. $dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt)$. Arises from $dy/dx = (dy/dt)\cdot(dt/dx) = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt)$.

## Engineering Connection

- **Robotic trajectories:** end-effector path described parametrically; speed profile separate from path.
- **Mechanism design:** linkage outputs (e.g., 4-bar) are parametric in the input angle.
- **CFD streamlines:** trajectory of fluid particles parameterized by time.
- **Gear tooth profiles:** involute curves are parametric.
- **CAD curves:** splines, Bezier curves — all parametric.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Eliminate the parameter: $x = 2t, y = t^2$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$t = x/2$, so $y = (x/2)^2 = x^2/4$. Parabola.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Find $dy/dx$ for $x = t^2 + 1, y = t^3 - t$ at $t = 2$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$dx/dt = 2t = 4$. $dy/dt = 3t^2 - 1 = 11$.

$dy/dx = 11/4$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Arc length of $x = \cos t, y = \sin t$ from 0 to $\pi$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\sqrt{\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t} = 1$. $L = \int_0^\pi 1\,dt = \pi$ (half circumference).
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Cycloid.** A cycloid is $x = r(t - \sin t), y = r(1 - \cos t)$. Find arc length over one arch ($t = 0$ to $2\pi$).
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\dot x = r(1-\cos t), \dot y = r\sin t$.

$\dot x^2 + \dot y^2 = r^2(1 - 2\cos t + \cos^2 t + \sin^2 t) = 2r^2(1 - \cos t) = 4r^2\sin^2(t/2)$.

$\sqrt{\cdot} = 2r|\sin(t/2)|$.

$L = \int_0^{2\pi}2r\sin(t/2)\,dt = -4r\cos(t/2)|_0^{2\pi} = -4r(-1 - 1) = 8r$.

A cycloid arch length = $8r$ (independent of $\pi$!) — surprising elegant result.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why is arc length parameterization-independent?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Substituting a different parameter $\tau(t)$ gives Jacobian factor $d\tau/dt$ in the integral, which cancels with how $dx/d\tau, dy/d\tau$ rescale. Geometric length is a property of the *curve*, not the parameterization.

Important: speed varies with parameterization, but total length is invariant.
</ExA>
