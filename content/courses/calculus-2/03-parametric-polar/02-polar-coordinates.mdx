# Polar Coordinates and Polar Curves

## The Big Picture

Cartesian coordinates $(x, y)$ are great for rectangles. **Polar coordinates** $(r, \theta)$ are better when the geometry involves circles, spirals, or rotational symmetry. Many curves have ugly Cartesian equations but elegant polar ones â€” circles, spirals, cardioids. Calculus in polar (slopes, area, arc length) requires modified formulas with the right Jacobian factors.

## Conversion

$$x = r\cos\theta, \quad y = r\sin\theta$$
$$r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}, \quad \theta = \arctan(y/x)$$

(Beware: $\arctan$ has limited range; need to consider quadrant.)

## Polar Curves

Equation: $r = f(\theta)$.

| Curve | Polar equation |
|---|---|
| Circle of radius $a$, centered at origin | $r = a$ |
| Circle through origin, radius $a$ | $r = 2a\cos\theta$ (centered at $(a, 0)$) |
| Cardioid | $r = a(1 + \cos\theta)$ |
| Rose with $n$ petals (odd $n$) | $r = a\cos(n\theta)$ |
| Archimedean spiral | $r = a\theta$ |

## Calculus in Polar

**Tangent slope:** Use parametric form with $\theta$:
$x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$, $dy/dx = (y'_\theta)/(x'_\theta)$.

**Area enclosed by polar curve from $\theta_1$ to $\theta_2$:**
$$A = \frac{1}{2}\int_{\theta_1}^{\theta_2} r^2\,d\theta$$

**Arc length:**
$$L = \int_{\theta_1}^{\theta_2}\sqrt{r^2 + (dr/d\theta)^2}\,d\theta$$

The factors come from the polar Jacobian.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Negative $r$ means imaginary."**

Negative $r$ means the point is in the *opposite* direction along the angle ray. Sometimes useful for tracing curves; sometimes excluded by convention. State clearly.

**Misconception 2: "Polar area uses $r\,d\theta$ alone."**

The factor of $1/2$ AND $r^2$ are essential. Sectors of a circle: area $= \tfrac{1}{2}r^2\theta$. Polar area generalizes this.

**Misconception 3: "Cartesian and polar give different results."**

For a fixed region, both coordinate systems give the same numerical answer. Polar is just often *easier* â€” fewer terms, simpler boundaries.

## Engineering Connection

- **Antenna patterns:** radiation patterns naturally polar (gain vs angle).
- **Robotic arm workspace:** circular reach often easiest in polar.
- **Spiral grooves (records, springs, screw threads):** $r = a\theta$ describes them.
- **Wind rose diagrams:** wind direction and frequency in polar.
- **Astronomical orbits:** ellipses around focal point (Sun) â€” Kepler equations natural in polar.

<ExQ>
**2.** Convert $r = 2\cos\theta$ to Cartesian.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Multiply by $r$: $r^2 = 2r\cos\theta \Rightarrow x^2 + y^2 = 2x \Rightarrow (x-1)^2 + y^2 = 1$.

Circle of radius 1 centered at $(1, 0)$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Area enclosed by $r = 2 + 2\cos\theta$ (cardioid).
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$A = \tfrac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi}(2+2\cos\theta)^2\,d\theta = 2\int_0^{2\pi}(1 + 2\cos\theta + \cos^2\theta)\,d\theta$

$\int_0^{2\pi}1\,d\theta = 2\pi$. $\int\cos\theta = 0$. $\int\cos^2\theta = \pi$.

$A = 2(2\pi + 0 + \pi) = 6\pi$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4.** Arc length of $r = e^\theta$ from $\theta = 0$ to $2\pi$ (logarithmic spiral).
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$dr/d\theta = e^\theta$. $\sqrt{r^2 + (r')^2} = \sqrt{2}e^\theta$.

$L = \sqrt 2\int_0^{2\pi}e^\theta\,d\theta = \sqrt 2(e^{2\pi} - 1) \approx 754.7$.
</ExA>
