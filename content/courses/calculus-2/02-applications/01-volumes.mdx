# Volumes by Slicing and Shells

## The Big Picture

Many engineering shapes — tanks, pipes, turbine blades, lenses — are obtained by rotating 2D regions around an axis. Computing their volume becomes a 1D integral once we slice the solid into cross-sections. The two main techniques are **disk/washer method** (slices perpendicular to the axis of rotation) and **cylindrical shells** (slices parallel to the axis). Both transform 3D volume problems into 1D calculus.

## Method 1: Disk / Washer (Perpendicular Slicing)

For a solid of revolution about the $x$-axis, with outer radius $R(x)$ and inner radius $r(x)$:

$$V = \int_a^b \pi[R(x)^2 - r(x)^2]\,dx$$

If solid (no hole), $r(x) = 0$ and we get **disks**: $V = \pi\int_a^b R(x)^2\,dx$.

With a hole: **washers**.

## Method 2: Cylindrical Shells (Parallel Slicing)

Rotate around $y$-axis, radius $x$, height $h(x)$, thickness $dx$. Each shell has surface area $2\pi x\cdot h(x)$.

$$V = \int_a^b 2\pi x\,h(x)\,dx$$

When to use which:
- Disks: when integration variable matches the axis of rotation.
- Shells: when integration variable is *perpendicular* to the axis, or disks would require solving for inverse.

## General Slicing (Any Cross-Section)

Volume = $\int A(x)\,dx$ where $A(x)$ is the area of the cross-section at $x$.

This generalizes: any solid (not just revolutions) can be sliced. Famous example: solid with circular base whose vertical slices are equilateral triangles.

## Worked Example: Sphere

Rotate $y = \sqrt{R^2 - x^2}$ around $x$-axis from $-R$ to $R$:

$$V = \pi\int_{-R}^R (R^2 - x^2)\,dx = \pi[R^2 x - x^3/3]_{-R}^R = \pi(R^3 - R^3/3 - (-R^3 + R^3/3)) = \frac{4\pi R^3}{3}$$

Standard sphere volume — derived in 2 lines.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Always use disks."**

Sometimes shells are dramatically easier — when the function is more naturally expressed in $x$ but rotation is about $y$-axis, shells avoid solving for inverse.

**Misconception 2: "Forgot $\pi$."**

Each disk has area $\pi R^2$. Forgetting $\pi$ is a common error — every revolution-volume integral includes it.

**Misconception 3: "$dx$ is always the thickness."**

For shells: $dx$ is the *radial* thickness. For disks: $dx$ is the slice thickness (perpendicular to axis). Different physical interpretations.

## Engineering Connection

- **Tank design:** volume of cylindrical, conical, spherical tanks computed via these methods.
- **Lens design:** lens volume from rotation of profile curve.
- **Turbine/pump impellers:** complex profiles integrated via washer method.
- **Material amounts:** how much material to cast a cone-shaped part.
- **Fluid volumes:** in irregularly shaped containers.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Volume of cone (radius $R$, height $h$) by integration.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$y = (R/h)x$ for $x \in [0, h]$ (rotated about $x$-axis).

$V = \pi\int_0^h (Rx/h)^2\,dx = \pi R^2/h^2 \cdot h^3/3 = \pi R^2 h/3$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Volume from rotating $y = x^2$ on $[0, 2]$ around $x$-axis.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$V = \pi\int_0^2 x^4\,dx = \pi\cdot 32/5 = 32\pi/5 \approx 20.1$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Same region rotated around $y$-axis (use shells).
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$V = 2\pi\int_0^2 x\cdot x^2\,dx = 2\pi\int x^3\,dx = 2\pi\cdot 4 = 8\pi \approx 25.1$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Tank.** A cylindrical tank, radius 2 m, height 5 m. With base shape $y = x^2/4$ (parabolic bottom). Volume?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Solid bounded above by $y = 5$, below by $y = x^2/4$, with $x^2 \leq 16$ (i.e., $|x| \leq 4$, but let's take radius 2 → $|x| \leq 2$).

Disk method (rotated): $V = \pi\int_0^5 r^2(y)\,dy$ where $r^2 = 4y$ from inverting $y = r^2/4$.

For $y \in [0, 1]$ (parabolic bottom): $V_{\text{bottom}} = \pi\int_0^1 4y\,dy = 2\pi$.

For $y \in [1, 5]$ (cylinder): $V = \pi\cdot 4\cdot 4 = 16\pi$.

Total: $18\pi \approx 56.5$ m³.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** When does the shell method beat the disk method?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
- When the integration is cleaner in one variable.
- When inverting the curve to express it as $x = f(y)$ is hard.

Example: rotating $y = e^x$ on $[0, 1]$ about $y$-axis. Disk method needs $x = \ln y$, awkward limits. Shell method: $V = 2\pi\int_0^1 x e^x\,dx$ — solvable via parts.
</ExA>
