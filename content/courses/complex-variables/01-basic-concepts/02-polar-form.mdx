# Polar Form and Euler's Formula

Every complex number $z = x + iy$ can be written in **polar form**:

$$z = r e^{i\theta} = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)$$

where $r = |z| = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}$ and $\theta = \arg(z) = \arctan(y/x)$.

## Euler's Formula

$$e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$$

This is the bridge between exponential and trigonometric functions. At $\theta = \pi$:

$$e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0$$

**Engineering significance:** Any oscillation $A\cos(\omega t + \phi)$ is the real part of $Ae^{i(\omega t + \phi)}$. Working with exponentials is far easier algebraically than with trigonometric functions — this is why complex numbers dominate AC circuit analysis and vibration theory.

## Multiplication in Polar Form

$$z_1 z_2 = r_1 r_2\, e^{i(\theta_1+\theta_2)}$$

Multiplication **rotates** and **scales**: moduli multiply, arguments add.

## De Moivre's Theorem

$$(e^{i\theta})^n = e^{in\theta} \implies (\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)^n = \cos(n\theta) + i\sin(n\theta)$$

## Roots of Complex Numbers

The $n$-th roots of $z = re^{i\theta}$ are:

$$z^{1/n} = r^{1/n} e^{i(\theta + 2\pi k)/n}, \quad k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1$$

There are exactly $n$ roots, equally spaced on a circle of radius $r^{1/n}$.

## Phasor Representation

In AC circuits and vibrations, quantities of the form $A\cos(\omega t + \phi)$ are represented as phasors $\tilde{A} = Ae^{i\phi}$ (complex amplitude). The physical quantity is $\text{Re}[\tilde{A}e^{i\omega t}]$.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
Write $z = -1 + i\sqrt{3}$ in polar form $re^{i\theta}$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$r = \sqrt{1+3} = 2$

$\theta = \arctan(\sqrt{3}/(-1))$ — in the second quadrant: $\theta = \pi - \pi/3 = 2\pi/3$

$$z = 2e^{i\cdot 2\pi/3}$$
</ExA>

<ExQ>
Use De Moivre's theorem to find $(\cos30° + i\sin30°)^6$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$$(\cos30°+i\sin30°)^6 = \cos(180°)+i\sin(180°) = -1 + 0i = \boxed{-1}$$
</ExA>

<ExQ>
Find all cube roots of $z = 8$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Write $8 = 8e^{i\cdot 0}$. Cube roots: $r^{1/3} = 2$, $\theta_k = 2\pi k/3$ for $k = 0, 1, 2$.

$$z_0 = 2e^{0} = 2$$
$$z_1 = 2e^{i\cdot 2\pi/3} = 2(-\tfrac{1}{2}+i\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}) = -1+i\sqrt{3}$$
$$z_2 = 2e^{i\cdot 4\pi/3} = -1-i\sqrt{3}$$
</ExA>
