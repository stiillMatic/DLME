# Evaluating Real Integrals via Contour Integration

One of the most striking applications of residues: computing real integrals that are difficult or impossible by real-variable methods.

## Type 1: Integrals of Rational Trigonometric Functions

$$\int_0^{2\pi} R(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)\,d\theta$$

Substitute $z = e^{i\theta}$, $dz = ie^{i\theta}d\theta$:

$$\cos\theta = \frac{z+z^{-1}}{2}, \quad \sin\theta = \frac{z-z^{-1}}{2i}, \quad d\theta = \frac{dz}{iz}$$

The integral becomes a contour integral around the unit circle $|z|=1$.

**Example:**

$$\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\theta}{2+\cos\theta} = \oint_{|z|=1}\frac{dz/iz}{2+(z+z^{-1})/2} = \oint_{|z|=1}\frac{2\,dz}{i(z^2+4z+1)}$$

Poles of $z^2+4z+1 = 0$: $z = -2\pm\sqrt{3}$. Only $z_0 = -2+\sqrt{3} \approx -0.27$ is inside $|z|=1$.

$$\text{Res} = \frac{2}{i(2z+4)}\bigg|_{z=z_0} = \frac{2}{i \cdot 2\sqrt{3}} = \frac{1}{i\sqrt{3}}$$

$$\int = 2\pi i \cdot \frac{1}{i\sqrt{3}} = \frac{2\pi}{\sqrt{3}}$$

## Type 2: Improper Integrals $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\,dx$

Close the contour with a large semicircle in the upper half-plane. If $f(z) \to 0$ faster than $1/|z|$ as $|z| \to \infty$, the semicircle contributes nothing (Jordan's lemma).

**Example:**

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{dx}{1+x^2}$$

Poles of $1/(1+z^2)$: $z = \pm i$. Upper half-plane pole: $z = i$.

$$\text{Res}(f, i) = \lim_{z\to i}(z-i)\frac{1}{(z-i)(z+i)} = \frac{1}{2i}$$

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{dx}{1+x^2} = 2\pi i \cdot \frac{1}{2i} = \pi$$

(Known result: $\arctan(x)\big|_{-\infty}^{\infty} = \pi$ ✓)

## Type 3: Fourier Integrals

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)e^{i\omega x}\,dx$$

Use a semicircle in upper or lower half-plane depending on sign of $\omega$. Essential for computing Fourier transforms of rational functions — used in signal processing and structural dynamics.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{dx}{(1+x^2)^2}$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Pole at $z = i$ of order 2 in the upper half-plane.

$$\text{Res}\left(\frac{1}{(1+z^2)^2}, i\right) = \lim_{z\to i}\frac{d}{dz}\left[(z-i)^2\cdot\frac{1}{(z-i)^2(z+i)^2}\right] = \lim_{z\to i}\frac{d}{dz}\frac{1}{(z+i)^2}$$

$$= \lim_{z\to i}\frac{-2}{(z+i)^3} = \frac{-2}{(2i)^3} = \frac{-2}{-8i} = \frac{1}{4i}$$

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{dx}{(1+x^2)^2} = 2\pi i\cdot\frac{1}{4i} = \boxed{\frac{\pi}{2}}$$
</ExA>

<ExQ>
In a mechanical vibration problem, the frequency-domain transfer function is $H(\omega) = 1/(-\omega^2 + 2i\zeta\omega_n\omega + \omega_n^2)$. Explain why contour integration in the complex $\omega$-plane is used to find the time-domain impulse response $h(t)$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
The impulse response is the inverse Fourier transform:

$$h(t) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}H(\omega)e^{i\omega t}\,d\omega$$

This is a Fourier integral of a rational function — exactly Type 3. The poles of $H(\omega)$ are the complex natural frequencies $\omega = \omega_n(-i\zeta \pm \sqrt{1-\zeta^2})$, lying in the lower half-plane (for $\zeta > 0$, i.e., a stable system).

- For $t > 0$: close in lower half-plane, pick up both poles → oscillating decaying exponential $e^{-\zeta\omega_n t}\sin(\omega_d t)$
- For $t < 0$: close in upper half-plane, no poles → $h(t) = 0$ (causality)

This is how the Green's function of a vibrating system is computed analytically.
</ExA>
