# Principal Component Analysis (PCA)

## The Big Picture

Real datasets are high-dimensional, but often the "interesting" variation lives in a much lower-dimensional subspace. **PCA** finds the directions of largest variance and projects onto them. The result: a low-dimensional representation that preserves most of the information, ideal for visualization, compression, and feeding into downstream ML models. It's just SVD applied to mean-centered data — but the engineering implications are enormous.

## Intuition First — Find the Long Axis of the Cloud

Imagine a 3D point cloud shaped like a flattened pancake. Most variance is along the pancake's long axis; some along its width; very little along its thinness.

PCA finds these three axes — sorted by how much variance they capture. Project onto the first two and you have a 2D picture of the pancake that retains 99% of the structure.

This generalizes to any dimension. PCA literally **rotates the coordinate system** so the new axes (principal components) align with directions of decreasing variance.

## The Algorithm

Given data matrix $X$ where rows are samples, columns are features:

1. **Center the data:** subtract column means. $X_c = X - \bar{X}$.
2. **Compute covariance:** $C = X_c^T X_c / (n - 1)$, a symmetric $d\times d$ matrix.
3. **Eigendecompose** $C$: $C = V\Lambda V^T$. Eigenvalues sorted: $\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq \cdots$.
4. **Project** onto top $k$ eigenvectors: $Z = X_c V_k$.

Equivalently, via SVD: $X_c = U\Sigma V^T$. Singular values squared (divided by $n-1$) are the variances along each principal direction.

## What Each Quantity Means

- **Principal components** = columns of $V$. Orthogonal directions of decreasing variance.
- **Variance explained** by component $i$: $\lambda_i / \sum_j \lambda_j$.
- **Scores** $Z = X_c V_k$ are the coordinates in the new basis.

## How Many Components?

Common heuristics:
- **Variance threshold:** keep enough components for 95% (or 90%, 99%) of total variance.
- **Scree plot:** plot eigenvalues; find the "elbow" where adding components stops helping much.
- **Domain knowledge:** physically motivated number of latent factors.

## Worked Example

Suppose 2D data with covariance matrix $C = \begin{pmatrix}4 & 2\\ 2 & 3\end{pmatrix}$.

Eigenvalues: $\lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 8 = 0 \Rightarrow \lambda = (7\pm\sqrt{17})/2 \approx 5.56, 1.44$.

Variance ratios: $5.56/7 = 79\%$ vs $1.44/7 = 21\%$.

The first PC explains 79% of variance — most information.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "PCA gives the most informative features."**

PCA gives directions of largest **variance**, not necessarily most "informative" for a task. For supervised problems, this can fail — variance might correlate with noise, not with target. **LDA** or **supervised PCA** addresses this.

**Misconception 2: "Centering doesn't matter."**

It does — without centering, you'd get directions toward the data mean, not toward the variance structure. **Always center.** (Sometimes also scale features to unit variance — "standardize.")

**Misconception 3: "Principal components are interpretable features."**

Sometimes — PCs are linear combinations of original features. The combination may or may not have natural interpretation. Be cautious assigning meaning ("eigenfaces" do happen to look face-like; many other domains don't have such luck).

**Misconception 4: "PCA is the best dimensionality reduction."**

It's *one* method, optimal for variance preservation under linear projection. Nonlinear methods (t-SNE, UMAP, autoencoders) capture nonlinear structure but lose other guarantees.

## Engineering Connection

- **Sensor data compression:** monitoring system with 100 sensors → reduce to 5 components for telemetry transmission.
- **Image processing:** "eigenfaces" — face recognition by projecting onto top PCs.
- **Vibration analysis:** **POD** (proper orthogonal decomposition) is PCA on fluid velocity / displacement fields.
- **Quality control:** detect anomalies by monitoring out-of-PCA-subspace excursions.
- **Feature engineering for ML:** decorrelate features before training; reduce dimension to fight curse of dimensionality.
- **Modal analysis** in structural dynamics: PCs of measured response fields = empirical modes.

## Practice Problems

### Warm-up

<ExQ>
**1.** Data has covariance $C = \begin{pmatrix}5 & 0\\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}$. First PC?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$C$ is diagonal; eigenvectors are standard basis vectors. First PC = $(1, 0)^T$ (corresponding to eigenvalue 5, the larger).

First PC explains $5/6 \approx 83\%$ of variance.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** If 4 PCs have eigenvalues $\lambda = 10, 4, 1, 0.1$, what fraction of variance is captured by the top 2?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Top 2: $10 + 4 = 14$. Total: $10 + 4 + 1 + 0.1 = 15.1$.

Fraction: $14/15.1 \approx 92.7\%$.

So 2 dimensions captures almost all variance — strong signal that data is essentially 2D.
</ExA>

### Application

<ExQ>
**3.** Center the data $X = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 2\\ 3 & 4\\ 5 & 6\end{pmatrix}$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Column means: $\bar X = (3, 4)$.

$X_c = \begin{pmatrix}-2 & -2\\ 0 & 0\\ 2 & 2\end{pmatrix}$.

Notice all rows of $X_c$ are multiples of $(1, 1)$ — data lies on a line through origin in centered coordinates. Rank 1.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Strain field analysis.** A strain gauge array gives a $1000\times 50$ matrix (1000 time samples × 50 sensors). PCA reveals top 3 PCs explain 98% variance. What does this say physically?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
The 50-dimensional sensor response really lives in a 3D subspace — there are effectively **3 dominant modes** of strain.

Physical interpretation: the structure is responding with 3 dominant modes of deformation; the 50 sensors are highly correlated, redundantly measuring these few modes. This corresponds to the first 3 vibrational mode shapes (or quasi-static deformation modes).

This is the foundation of **modal identification** from operational data.
</ExA>

### Conceptual

<ExQ>
**5. True or False.** PCA is invariant under rotations of the data. Justify.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**True (essentially).** Rotating the data $X \to XR$ for an orthogonal $R$ leads to PCs rotated correspondingly: same variance structure, expressed in new axes.

What PCA *does* depend on: **scaling** of features. If one feature has 1000× the scale of another, PCA will be dominated by that feature. This is why standardization (dividing each column by its std deviation) is often used.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**6.** Why does PCA work with the eigendecomposition of the **covariance matrix** rather than the data matrix directly?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
The eigenvectors of $C = X_c^T X_c/(n-1)$ are the directions of variance.

**Why covariance?** $C_{ij}$ = covariance between features $i$ and $j$. The eigendecomposition finds an orthogonal basis where features become **uncorrelated** ($C$ becomes diagonal in the eigen-basis).

Equivalently, this is the right-singular-vector matrix from SVD of $X_c$. Eigendecomposition of $C$ and SVD of $X_c$ give the same PCs — but SVD is more numerically stable (avoids forming $C$ explicitly).

For very large data, SVD with random projection or iterative methods is the practical algorithm.
</ExA>
