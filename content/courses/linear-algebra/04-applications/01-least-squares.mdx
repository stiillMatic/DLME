# Least Squares and Regression

## The Big Picture

Real engineering data is noisy. Real measurements have error. So when you have a system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ with **more equations than unknowns**, there's usually *no exact solution* — every choice of $\mathbf{x}$ leaves a residual. **Least squares** picks the $\mathbf{x}$ that minimizes the total squared error. It's the linear algebra of fitting models to data, and it's the conceptual foundation of all statistical and ML estimation.

## Intuition First — Projection

Geometrically: $\mathbf{b}$ doesn't live in the column space of $A$. The "best" we can do is **project** $\mathbf{b}$ onto Col($A$) and solve $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}_{\text{proj}}$.

The projection minimizes the distance $\|\mathbf{b} - A\mathbf{x}\|$ — i.e., the **residual**.

So least squares = projecting + solving the resulting solvable system.

## The Normal Equations

The least-squares solution satisfies:

$$A^TA\,\mathbf{x} = A^T\mathbf{b}$$

**Derivation:** the residual $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{b} - A\mathbf{x}$ should be orthogonal to Col($A$), i.e., $A^T\mathbf{r} = 0$. Expanding: $A^T(\mathbf{b} - A\mathbf{x}) = 0 \Rightarrow A^TA\mathbf{x} = A^T\mathbf{b}$.

If $A^TA$ is invertible:

$$\mathbf{x}^* = (A^TA)^{-1}A^T\mathbf{b}$$

The matrix $(A^TA)^{-1}A^T$ is the **left pseudo-inverse** of $A$ (sometimes written $A^+$).

## Worked Example: Linear Regression

Fit $y = m x + c$ to data points $(x_i, y_i)$. The system:

$$\begin{pmatrix}x_1 & 1\\ x_2 & 1\\ \vdots & \vdots\\ x_n & 1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}m\\ c\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}y_1\\ y_2\\ \vdots\\ y_n\end{pmatrix}$$

That's $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ with $n$ rows, 2 unknowns. Apply normal equations:

$$\begin{pmatrix}\sum x_i^2 & \sum x_i\\ \sum x_i & n\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}m\\ c\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}\sum x_iy_i\\ \sum y_i\end{pmatrix}$$

This 2x2 system gives the classical regression coefficients.

## Beyond Linear: Polynomial Regression

Fit $y = c_0 + c_1 x + c_2 x^2 + \cdots + c_p x^p$ by setting up:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix}1 & x_1 & x_1^2 & \cdots\\ 1 & x_2 & x_2^2 & \cdots\\ \vdots\end{pmatrix}$$

Same machinery — different basis.

## Why "Squared" Error?

Why minimize $\sum r_i^2$ instead of $\sum |r_i|$ or $\sum r_i^4$?

1. **Smoothness:** squared error is differentiable everywhere; normal equations are linear.
2. **Statistical justification:** if errors are Gaussian, least squares = maximum likelihood.
3. **Geometric clarity:** corresponds to orthogonal projection.

Different objectives exist (L1 / Huber for robustness), but L2 is by far most common.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Least squares always has a unique solution."**

Requires $A^TA$ invertible, i.e., $A$ has full column rank. Otherwise infinite solutions — use **regularization** (Ridge regression) or **pseudo-inverse**.

**Misconception 2: "Use $\mathbf{x} = A^{-1}\mathbf{b}$ for overdetermined systems."**

$A^{-1}$ doesn't exist for non-square $A$. The normal equations or pseudo-inverse $A^+$ are the proper tools.

**Misconception 3: "Solving normal equations directly is best."**

Numerically poor. $A^TA$ has condition number $\kappa(A)^2$ — squared sensitivity. For better numerical stability use QR decomposition or SVD.

**Misconception 4: "Least squares is only for linear models."**

You can fit *nonlinear* models that are *linear in parameters* (like polynomials, splines, Fourier series) — still linear least squares.

For models nonlinear in parameters, need iterative nonlinear least squares (Gauss-Newton, Levenberg-Marquardt).

## Engineering Connection

- **Calibrating sensors:** fit voltage→pressure relationship from calibration measurements.
- **System identification:** estimate transfer function parameters from input-output data.
- **Computer vision:** estimate camera homography from corresponding points.
- **GPS positioning:** triangulate position from satellite range measurements — over-determined system, least squares.
- **Machine learning:** linear regression is the simplest predictive model and the building block for many others.
- **Curve fitting in experiments:** smooth noisy measurements with polynomial / spline regression.

## Practice Problems

### Warm-up

<ExQ>
**1.** Fit $y = mx$ to points $(1, 2), (2, 3), (3, 4)$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$A = (1, 2, 3)^T$, $\mathbf{b} = (2, 3, 4)^T$.

$A^TA = 14$, $A^T\mathbf{b} = 2 + 6 + 12 = 20$.

$m = 20/14 = 10/7 \approx 1.43$.

Best line through origin has slope $\approx 1.43$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Fit $y = c$ (constant) to $y$-values $\{2, 4, 5, 9\}$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$A = (1, 1, 1, 1)^T$, $\mathbf{b} = (2, 4, 5, 9)^T$.

$A^TA = 4$, $A^T\mathbf{b} = 20$.

$c = 5$. **Just the mean** — the least-squares constant is always the sample mean.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Spring constant from data.** Force-extension data: $(F, x) = (10, 0.5), (20, 1.1), (30, 1.4), (40, 2.0)$ N, mm. Find best $k$ for $F = kx$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Fit $F = kx$, so $A = (0.5, 1.1, 1.4, 2.0)^T$, $\mathbf{b} = (10, 20, 30, 40)^T$.

$A^TA = 0.25 + 1.21 + 1.96 + 4 = 7.42$.
$A^T\mathbf{b} = 5 + 22 + 42 + 80 = 149$.

$k = 149/7.42 \approx 20.08$ N/mm.

Spring constant is approximately 20 N/mm. The data has slight nonlinearity (real springs aren't perfectly Hookean), but least squares gives a useful linear estimate.
</ExA>

### Conceptual

<ExQ>
**5. True or False.** Least squares minimizes the maximum residual. Justify.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**False.** Least squares minimizes the **sum of squared** residuals — i.e., the 2-norm.

Minimizing the maximum residual (∞-norm) is a different problem called **Chebyshev approximation** — solved by linear programming, not normal equations.

Least squares is sensitive to outliers (squaring amplifies large residuals); robust methods like L1 (sum of absolute values) are less affected.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**6.** Why is $A^TA$ always symmetric, and why does this matter for least squares?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$(A^TA)^T = A^T(A^T)^T = A^TA$ — symmetric by construction.

Why it matters:
- Symmetric positive-semidefinite matrices have **real, nonnegative eigenvalues**.
- Numerical solvers can use specialized algorithms (Cholesky factorization) — twice as fast as general LU.
- The condition number squares: $\kappa(A^TA) = \kappa(A)^2$, sometimes causing numerical issues.

The symmetric structure is what makes least squares so tractable — but the condition-squaring warns us to use QR or SVD methods for ill-conditioned systems.
</ExA>
