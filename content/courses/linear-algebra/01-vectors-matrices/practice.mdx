# Chapter 1 Practice — Vectors and Matrices

<ExQ>
**1.** $\mathbf{u} = (2, -1, 3)$, $\mathbf{v} = (1, 4, -2)$. Find $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$2 - 4 - 6 = -8$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Compute $AB$ where $A = \begin{pmatrix}1&2\\3&4\end{pmatrix}$, $B = \begin{pmatrix}5&6\\7&8\end{pmatrix}$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$AB = \begin{pmatrix}19 & 22\\ 43 & 50\end{pmatrix}$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Find inverse of $A = \begin{pmatrix}2&1\\1&1\end{pmatrix}$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\det A = 1$. $A^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix}1 & -1\\ -1 & 2\end{pmatrix}$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Conceptual.** Why are $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = 0$ vectors said to be orthogonal?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = \|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|\cos\theta$. Zero implies $\cos\theta = 0$, i.e., $\theta = 90°$ — perpendicular.

This generalizes to higher dimensions where "perpendicular" can't be visualized, but inner-product = 0 still defines orthogonality.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Engineering.** A robot arm with three joints has Jacobian $J = 3\times 3$ matrix relating joint velocities to end-effector velocity. Why must $\det J \neq 0$ for the arm to move freely?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\det J = 0$ means $J$ is singular — some directions of end-effector motion are unreachable from joint motions. This is a **kinematic singularity**.

Examples: when arm is fully extended, can't pull "in" along the line of extension. Designers avoid trajectories that pass near singular configurations.
</ExA>
