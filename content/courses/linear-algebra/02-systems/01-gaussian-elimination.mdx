# Gaussian Elimination

## The Big Picture

Solving $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ — a system of linear equations — is the central computational task of engineering. Gaussian elimination is the workhorse algorithm: a systematic procedure to reduce a system to a triangular form that's trivially solvable. Every numerical linear algebra package implements this (with optimizations); every FEA solver uses it as a subroutine. Understanding it gives you the foundation for everything that follows.

## Intuition First — Triangularize, Then Back-Substitute

Imagine three equations in three unknowns. If only the first equation involved all three unknowns, the second involved two, the third one — you could solve them trivially from bottom to top. That triangle shape is the **goal** of elimination.

Gaussian elimination uses three operations that don't change the solution:
1. **Swap** two rows
2. **Scale** a row by a nonzero constant
3. **Add** a multiple of one row to another

By applying these systematically, we zero out entries below the diagonal — creating the triangle.

## The Algorithm

For an augmented matrix $[A | \mathbf{b}]$:

1. Use row 1 to eliminate the first column below row 1
2. Use row 2 to eliminate the second column below row 2
3. Continue until upper triangular
4. **Back-substitute** to solve for each variable from bottom to top

The result is **row echelon form** (REF).

Continuing further (zeroing above the pivots, normalizing pivots to 1) gives **reduced row echelon form** (RREF) — unique.

## Worked Example

Solve:
$$\begin{cases}2x + y + z = 8\\ -3x - y + 2z = -11\\ -2x + y + 2z = -3\end{cases}$$

Augmented matrix:
$$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & 1 & 1 & 8 \\ -3 & -1 & 2 & -11 \\ -2 & 1 & 2 & -3\end{array}\right]$$

Row 2 ← R2 + (3/2)R1; Row 3 ← R3 + R1:
$$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & 1 & 1 & 8 \\ 0 & 1/2 & 7/2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 3 & 5\end{array}\right]$$

Row 3 ← R3 − 4 R2:
$$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & 1 & 1 & 8 \\ 0 & 1/2 & 7/2 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & -11 & 1\end{array}\right]$$

Back-substitute:
- $-11 z = 1 \Rightarrow z = -1/11$
- $\tfrac{1}{2}y + \tfrac{7}{2}(-1/11) = 1 \Rightarrow y = 2 + 7/11 = 29/11$
- $2x + 29/11 - 1/11 = 8 \Rightarrow x = (88 - 28)/22 = 30/11$

(Solution: $(30/11, 29/11, -1/11)$.)

## Three Possible Outcomes

After elimination:

1. **Unique solution:** every column has a pivot → exactly one $\mathbf{x}$.
2. **Infinite solutions:** some columns have no pivot (free variables) → entire solution family.
3. **No solution:** a row $[0\;0\;\cdots\;0\;|\;c]$ with $c \neq 0$ → contradiction ($0 = c$).

Reading the structure of the row-echelon form tells you which case you're in.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Pivot must be nonzero."**

If a pivot position is zero, **swap rows** to get a nonzero entry there. If no row has a nonzero entry in that column, that variable becomes free.

**Misconception 2: "Row reduction changes the solution."**

The three elementary operations preserve the solution set. The system *looks* different, but it has identical solutions.

**Misconception 3: "Always reduce to RREF."**

For solving systems, REF + back-substitution is enough. RREF is useful for understanding structure (rank, null space basis) but is more work to compute.

**Misconception 4: "If $A$ has more columns than rows, no solution."**

Wrong — usually means *infinite* solutions (more unknowns than equations). "Tall" systems (more rows) often have no solution; "wide" systems have many.

## Engineering Connection

- **Circuit analysis (Kirchhoff's laws):** systems of linear equations for currents and voltages.
- **Truss analysis:** force balance at each joint → linear system in member forces.
- **Finite element analysis:** stiffness equations $K\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{f}$ solved via LU decomposition (a refined Gaussian elimination).
- **Linear regression:** normal equations $A^TA\,\mathbf{x} = A^T\mathbf{b}$ solved via elimination.
- **Heat conduction with finite differences:** the discretized PDE is a linear system.

## Practice Problems

### Warm-up

<ExQ>
**1.** Solve via elimination: $x + y = 5$, $2x - y = 1$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\left[\begin{array}{cc|c}1 & 1 & 5\\ 2 & -1 & 1\end{array}\right] \to \left[\begin{array}{cc|c}1 & 1 & 5\\ 0 & -3 & -9\end{array}\right]$.

$-3y = -9 \Rightarrow y = 3$. $x = 5 - 3 = 2$.

Solution: $(2, 3)$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Solve: $x + 2y + z = 4$, $2x + y - z = 3$, $x + y + 2z = 5$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$R_2 \to R_2 - 2R_1$: $0, -3, -3, -5$.
$R_3 \to R_3 - R_1$: $0, -1, 1, 1$.

$R_3 \to R_3 - R_2/3$: $0, 0, 2, 1 + 5/3 = 8/3$.

So $z = 4/3$. From $R_2$: $-3y - 3(4/3) = -5 \Rightarrow y = 1/3$. From $R_1$: $x = 4 - 2/3 - 4/3 = 2$.

Solution: $(2, 1/3, 4/3)$.
</ExA>

### Application

<ExQ>
**3.** Solve $\begin{cases}x + y = 3\\ 2x + 2y = 7\end{cases}$. What does the result mean?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$R_2 \to R_2 - 2R_1$: $0\,x + 0\,y = 1$.

Contradiction: $0 = 1$. **No solution**.

Geometrically: the two equations describe **parallel lines** (same slope, different intercepts) — never intersect.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4.** Solve $\begin{cases}x + y + z = 6\\ 2x + 2y + 2z = 12\\ x - y + z = 2\end{cases}$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$R_2 \to R_2 - 2R_1$: all zeros (the second equation was redundant).

$R_3 \to R_3 - R_1$: $0, -2, 0, -4$ ⟹ $y = 2$.

Now: $x + z = 4$ — one equation, two unknowns. $z$ is **free**.

General solution: $\mathbf{x} = (4 - t,\ 2,\ t)$ for any $t$.

**Infinite solutions** — the system has fewer independent equations than unknowns.
</ExA>

### Conceptual

<ExQ>
**5. True or False.** Adding rows of a matrix to each other can produce a matrix with the same row space. Justify.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**True.** The row space is the span of the rows. Adding row $i$ to row $j$ replaces row $j$ with a linear combination of the existing rows — this doesn't add any new "direction" to the span, and the original row $i$ is still there, so the span is unchanged.

This is exactly why elementary row operations don't change the *row space* — which is why elimination preserves solutions to $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**6.** A system has reduced augmented matrix $\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c}1 & 0 & 2 & 3\\ 0 & 1 & -1 & 4\\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0\end{array}\right]$. Describe all solutions.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Pivots in columns 1, 2. Column 3 has no pivot → $z$ is free.

$x = 3 - 2z$, $y = 4 + z$.

Parametric: $\mathbf{x} = (3, 4, 0) + t(-2, 1, 1)$ for any $t \in \mathbb{R}$.

A line in $\mathbb{R}^3$.
</ExA>
