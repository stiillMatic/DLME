# Rank, Nullspace, and Column Space

## The Big Picture

Every matrix $A$ defines four fundamental subspaces: the **column space** (image), **null space** (kernel), **row space**, and **left null space**. These tell you what the matrix can do and what it cannot. The **rank** is a single number capturing how "much" the matrix actually transforms. Understanding rank and these subspaces lets you immediately read off whether a system has solutions, how many, and what the structure looks like.

## Intuition First — A Matrix as a Funnel

A matrix $A$ takes vectors from the *input* space $\mathbb{R}^n$ to the *output* space $\mathbb{R}^m$. Two questions:
- **Which outputs are reachable?** That's the column space (image).
- **Which inputs map to zero?** That's the null space (kernel).

Some matrices "flatten" their inputs — multiple inputs collapse to one output, losing information. Rank measures how much information survives.

## Column Space (Image)

$$\text{Col}(A) = \{A\mathbf{x} : \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n\} = \text{span of the columns of } A$$

It's a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^m$. The **dimension of Col($A$)** is the rank.

## Null Space (Kernel)

$$\text{Null}(A) = \{\mathbf{x} : A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}\}$$

A subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$. Vectors that the matrix "kills." Its dimension is called the **nullity**.

## The Rank-Nullity Theorem

For an $m\times n$ matrix:

$$\text{rank}(A) + \text{nullity}(A) = n$$

In words: the number of input dimensions = "dimensions that survive" + "dimensions that get killed."

This conservation principle is the most important equation in linear algebra after the matrix product.

## How to Find Them

**Row reduce** $A$ to RREF. Then:
- **Rank** = number of pivots.
- **Column space basis** = original columns corresponding to pivot positions.
- **Null space basis** = solve $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ via free variables, write parametric form.
- **Row space** = nonzero rows of the RREF.

## Worked Example

$$A = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 2 & 3\\ 2 & 4 & 6\\ 1 & 1 & 2\end{pmatrix}$$

Row reduce: $R_2 \to R_2 - 2R_1$ gives all zeros. $R_3 \to R_3 - R_1$ gives $(0, -1, -1)$.

RREF: $\begin{pmatrix}1 & 0 & 1\\ 0 & 1 & 1\\ 0 & 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}$ (after further reduction).

**Rank = 2** (two pivots), **Nullity = 1** (third column has no pivot — free variable).

**Column space basis:** columns 1 and 2 of original $A$: $(1, 2, 1)^T$ and $(2, 4, 1)^T$.

**Null space:** solve $\begin{pmatrix}1 & 0 & 1\\ 0 & 1 & 1\\ 0 & 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$. With $z = t$ free: $x = -t, y = -t$. Basis: $(-1, -1, 1)^T$.

## What Rank Tells You

| Rank $r$ for $m\times n$ matrix | Meaning |
|---|---|
| $r = n$ | columns linearly independent |
| $r = m$ | rows linearly independent |
| $r = m = n$ | invertible |
| $r < \min(m, n)$ | rank-deficient |

For $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$:
- Solution exists iff $\mathbf{b} \in $ Col$(A)$ iff rank$([A | \mathbf{b}])$ = rank$(A)$.
- Solution unique iff rank = $n$ (no free variables) iff Null$(A) = \{0\}$.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "rank = number of nonzero rows."**

Only after row reduction. The *original* matrix might have all-nonzero rows but be rank 1 (all rows are multiples of one).

**Misconception 2: "Null space contains the zero vector, so it's always 1-dimensional."**

Null space *always contains* $\mathbf{0}$ (that's what makes it a subspace). But if the only solution to $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ is $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$, the null space is $\{\mathbf{0}\}$ — a **zero-dimensional** subspace (just the origin). If there are nontrivial solutions, dimension ≥ 1.

**Misconception 3: "Column space basis = columns of RREF."**

No! Use **original** columns at pivot positions. The RREF columns are different vectors (in a different basis).

**Misconception 4: "Full rank means invertible."**

Full rank just means rank = min(m, n). For invertibility, need square AND full rank.

## Engineering Connection

- **Truss / structural rigidity:** kinematic indeterminacy = nullity of the equilibrium matrix. Zero nullity = stable structure.
- **Static indeterminacy:** when the equilibrium matrix has rank < number of unknowns → can't solve uniquely from statics alone.
- **System identifiability:** in dynamical systems, the rank of the observability matrix tells you whether the state can be inferred from outputs.
- **Compressed sensing:** signals that are sparse have effective rank lower than ambient dimension.
- **PCA / dimensionality reduction:** "effective rank" of a data matrix tells you how many features really matter.
- **Neural network expressiveness:** layers with low-rank weight matrices effectively reduce information bandwidth.

## Practice Problems

### Warm-up

<ExQ>
**1.** Find the rank of $A = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 2\\ 3 & 6\end{pmatrix}$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$R_2 - 3R_1$ gives row of zeros. **Rank = 1.** Second row is a multiple of the first.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Find a basis for the null space of $A = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 2 & 1\\ 2 & 4 & 2\end{pmatrix}$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$R_2 - 2R_1$ gives zero row. RREF: $\begin{pmatrix}1 & 2 & 1\\ 0 & 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}$.

One pivot in column 1; columns 2 and 3 free: $y = s$, $z = t$ free. $x = -2s - t$.

Null space basis: $\{(-2, 1, 0), (-1, 0, 1)\}$. Dimension 2.

Verify rank-nullity: $\text{rank} = 1$, $n = 3$, $1 + 2 = 3$ ✓.
</ExA>

### Application

<ExQ>
**3. Engineering: Truss.** A 2D truss has 8 unknowns (member forces) and 10 equilibrium equations from 5 joints. If the equilibrium matrix has rank 8, what does this tell you?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Rank = number of unknowns = 8 → unique solution exists for member forces if the loading is statically admissible.

The 10 equations have rank 8, meaning 2 equations are redundant (or contradictory if loading is inadmissible). Equilibrium imposes only 8 independent constraints.

This is the standard **statically determinate** truss analysis. Rank-deficient cases (rank < 8) correspond to mechanisms — the truss can deform without applying force.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4.** For $A = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 1 & 0\\ 0 & 1 & 1\\ 1 & 0 & -1\end{pmatrix}$, find rank and determine if $\mathbf{b} = (2, 1, 1)^T$ is in Col$(A)$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$R_3 \to R_3 - R_1$: $(0, -1, -1)$. $R_3 \to R_3 + R_2$: $(0, 0, 0)$.

RREF: $\begin{pmatrix}1 & 0 & -1\\ 0 & 1 & 1\\ 0 & 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}$.

**Rank = 2.**

Augment with $\mathbf{b}$ and reduce: $\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c}1 & 1 & 0 & 2\\ 0 & 1 & 1 & 1\\ 1 & 0 & -1 & 1\end{array}\right]$.

Apply same operations: third row becomes $(0, 0, 0, 0)$. Rank of augmented matrix = 2 = rank of $A$.

So **yes**, $\mathbf{b} \in $ Col$(A)$, system is consistent (infinite solutions due to nullity 1).
</ExA>

### Conceptual

<ExQ>
**5. True or False.** If $A$ is invertible, then $A^TA$ is also invertible. Justify.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**True.** $A^TA$ is invertible iff $A^TA$ has trivial null space.

Suppose $A^TA\mathbf{x} = 0$. Multiply on left by $\mathbf{x}^T$: $\mathbf{x}^T A^T A\mathbf{x} = \|A\mathbf{x}\|^2 = 0$, so $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$.

If $A$ is invertible, $A\mathbf{x} = 0$ implies $\mathbf{x} = 0$. So Null$(A^TA) = \{0\}$, meaning $A^TA$ is invertible. ✓

**General fact:** $A^TA$ is invertible iff $A$ has full column rank. Doesn't require $A$ square.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**6.** Explain why the **rows** of $A$ and the **columns** of $A$ always have the same dimension of span.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
This is the *row rank = column rank* theorem.

After row-reducing $A$ to RREF, the number of nonzero rows (= row rank) equals the number of pivot columns (= column rank). Row operations don't change the **row space** dimension, and pivot columns correspond to a maximal linearly independent set of original columns.

So both row rank and column rank equal the rank of the matrix.

**Practical:** you can find rank by looking at rows OR columns — whichever is convenient.
</ExA>
