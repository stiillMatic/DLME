# Diagonalization and Matrix Powers

## The Big Picture

A matrix is hard to work with when its action mixes the coordinates. But if you change to the **eigenvector basis**, the same transformation becomes **diagonal** — every component just scales independently. This is **diagonalization**: rewriting $A$ as $PDP^{-1}$ where $D$ is diagonal. Once you have this form, matrix powers, exponentials, and dynamical systems become trivial. Diagonalization is the single most useful structural decomposition in applied math.

## Intuition First — Coordinate Choice Matters

Imagine you're stretching a piece of rubber. In some coordinate system, the stretch mixes $x$ and $y$ — confusing. But if you align coordinates with the principal stretch directions, the same operation looks like:
- stretch $\hat{e}_1$ direction by factor $\lambda_1$
- stretch $\hat{e}_2$ direction by factor $\lambda_2$

These principal directions ARE the eigenvectors. The factors ARE the eigenvalues.

Diagonalization is just **putting the matrix in its natural coordinates**.

## The Diagonalization

If $A$ is $n\times n$ with $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_n$ and eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n$, then:

$$A = PDP^{-1}$$

where:
- $P$ has the eigenvectors as columns
- $D$ is diagonal with eigenvalues on the diagonal

**Equivalent:** $AP = PD$, i.e., $A\mathbf{v}_i = \lambda_i\mathbf{v}_i$ for each column.

## When Is a Matrix Diagonalizable?

$A$ is diagonalizable **iff** it has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors. This happens when:
- All eigenvalues are **distinct**, OR
- For each repeated eigenvalue, its geometric multiplicity equals its algebraic multiplicity.

**Always diagonalizable:**
- Real symmetric matrices (with orthogonal eigenvectors!) — Spectral theorem.

**Not always diagonalizable:**
- Matrices with repeated eigenvalues and "defective" eigenvectors (Jordan blocks).

## Power of Diagonalization — Computing $A^k$

If $A = PDP^{-1}$:

$$A^k = (PDP^{-1})(PDP^{-1})\cdots(PDP^{-1}) = PD^kP^{-1}$$

Powers of $A$ require $n^3$ operations each. Powers of $D$ are trivial: $D^k = \text{diag}(\lambda_1^k, \ldots, \lambda_n^k)$.

This transforms $A^{1000}$ from "expensive" to "trivial."

## Matrix Exponential

For dynamics $\dot{\mathbf{x}} = A\mathbf{x}$, the solution is $\mathbf{x}(t) = e^{At}\mathbf{x}_0$.

Using diagonalization:
$$e^{At} = Pe^{Dt}P^{-1}, \quad e^{Dt} = \text{diag}(e^{\lambda_1 t}, \ldots, e^{\lambda_n t})$$

So the dynamics decouple: each eigenmode evolves independently as $e^{\lambda_i t}$.

## Worked Example

$$A = \begin{pmatrix}4 & 1\\ 2 & 3\end{pmatrix}$$

Eigenvalues: $\lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 = 0 \Rightarrow \lambda = 2, 5$.

Eigenvectors:
- $\lambda = 5$: $(A - 5I)\mathbf{v} = 0 \Rightarrow \mathbf{v}_1 = (1, 1)$.
- $\lambda = 2$: $(A - 2I)\mathbf{v} = 0 \Rightarrow \mathbf{v}_2 = (1, -2)$.

$P = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\\ 1 & -2\end{pmatrix}$, $D = \begin{pmatrix}5 & 0\\ 0 & 2\end{pmatrix}$.

$P^{-1} = \frac{1}{-3}\begin{pmatrix}-2 & -1\\ -1 & 1\end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{3}\begin{pmatrix}2 & 1\\ 1 & -1\end{pmatrix}$.

Verify: $A = PDP^{-1}$ — check by direct computation if desired.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "All matrices can be diagonalized."**

False. Defective matrices like $\begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}$ have one eigenvalue ($\lambda = 1$) with algebraic multiplicity 2 but only one eigenvector — not diagonalizable. They need Jordan canonical form instead.

**Misconception 2: "Eigenvectors are unique."**

Eigenvectors are unique up to scaling. Any nonzero multiple of an eigenvector is also an eigenvector. By convention, we often normalize them.

**Misconception 3: "Order of eigenvalues doesn't matter."**

Order matters: column $i$ of $P$ must match $D_{ii}$. Permuting eigenvalues requires permuting eigenvectors correspondingly.

**Misconception 4: "Diagonalization makes everything diagonal."**

It makes $A$ diagonal **in the eigenvector basis**, not in the standard basis. The transformation $P$ converts between bases.

## Engineering Connection

- **Vibration analysis:** the modal decomposition of mass-stiffness system $M\ddot{\mathbf{x}} + K\mathbf{x} = 0$ is essentially diagonalization. Each "mode" is an eigenvector; "natural frequency" is $\sqrt{\lambda}$.
- **Control theory:** state-space system $\dot{\mathbf{x}} = A\mathbf{x}$ is stable iff eigenvalues of $A$ have negative real parts.
- **PageRank / Markov chains:** long-run behavior dominated by largest-eigenvalue eigenvector.
- **Principal stresses:** diagonalizing the stress tensor gives principal stresses (eigenvalues) along principal directions (eigenvectors).
- **Quantum mechanics:** eigenvalues = energy levels; eigenvectors = stationary states.

## Practice Problems

### Warm-up

<ExQ>
**1.** Diagonalize $A = \begin{pmatrix}3 & 0\\ 0 & 5\end{pmatrix}$. Find $A^{10}$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Already diagonal! $A^{10} = \begin{pmatrix}3^{10} & 0\\ 0 & 5^{10}\end{pmatrix}$.

(Eigenvalues 3, 5; eigenvectors $(1, 0), (0, 1)$.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Find eigenvalues and eigenvectors of $A = \begin{pmatrix}2 & 1\\ 0 & 3\end{pmatrix}$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Eigenvalues from $(2-\lambda)(3-\lambda) = 0$: $\lambda = 2, 3$.

$\lambda = 2$: $(A - 2I)\mathbf{v} = 0$ gives $\mathbf{v}_1 = (1, 0)$.
$\lambda = 3$: $(A - 3I)\mathbf{v} = 0$ gives $\mathbf{v}_2 = (1, 1)$.
</ExA>

### Application

<ExQ>
**3.** Compute $A^{100}$ symbolically for $A = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1\\ 1 & 1\end{pmatrix}$ (Fibonacci matrix).
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Characteristic: $\lambda^2 - \lambda - 1 = 0 \Rightarrow \lambda = (1\pm\sqrt 5)/2 = \phi, -1/\phi$ where $\phi$ is the golden ratio.

After diagonalization, $A^{100}$ has entries $\sim \phi^{100}$. The $(1,2)$ entry of $A^n$ is exactly the $n$-th Fibonacci number $F_n$.

So $F_{100} = $ entry $(1,2)$ of $A^{100}$. Compute via $A^{100} = PD^{100}P^{-1}$ in closed form.

This is **Binet's formula**: $F_n = (\phi^n - (-1/\phi)^n)/\sqrt 5$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: 2-DOF vibration.** A mass-spring system has $M\ddot{\mathbf{x}} = -K\mathbf{x}$ with $M = I$, $K = \begin{pmatrix}3 & -1\\ -1 & 3\end{pmatrix}$. Find natural frequencies and mode shapes.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Need $\det(K - \omega^2 I) = 0$: $(3-\omega^2)^2 - 1 = 0 \Rightarrow (3-\omega^2) = \pm 1$, so $\omega^2 = 2, 4$.

Frequencies: $\omega_1 = \sqrt 2 \approx 1.41$ rad/s, $\omega_2 = 2$ rad/s.

Mode shapes:
- $\omega^2 = 2$: $(K - 2I)\mathbf{v} = 0$ gives $\mathbf{v}_1 = (1, 1)$ — **in-phase** mode.
- $\omega^2 = 4$: $(K - 4I)\mathbf{v} = 0$ gives $\mathbf{v}_2 = (1, -1)$ — **out-of-phase** mode.

Diagonalization gives the modal coordinates that decouple the system.
</ExA>

### Conceptual

<ExQ>
**5. True or False.** If $A$ and $B$ have the same eigenvalues, then $A = B$. Justify.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**False.** Eigenvalues alone don't determine a matrix — eigenvectors also matter.

Counterexample: $A = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0\\ 0 & 2\end{pmatrix}$ and $B = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 5\\ 0 & 2\end{pmatrix}$ both have eigenvalues 1, 2 but $A \neq B$.

Eigenvalues capture *how much* but eigenvectors capture *in which directions*. Both are needed.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**6.** Why is a symmetric matrix always diagonalizable?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**Spectral Theorem:** every real symmetric matrix has real eigenvalues and a complete set of orthogonal eigenvectors.

This is a deep result with several proofs. The key consequence: $A = PDP^T$ where $P$ is **orthogonal** ($P^T = P^{-1}$). The change of basis is a rotation (or reflection) — particularly easy to invert.

This is why so many engineering problems with symmetric matrices (stress, stiffness, covariance) have clean diagonalization-based analyses.
</ExA>
