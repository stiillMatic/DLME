# Chapter 4 Practice — Reaction Kinetics

<ExQ>
**1.** Reaction is first-order with $k = 5\times 10^{-3}$ /s. Half-life?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$t_{1/2} = \ln 2/k = 0.693/5\times 10^{-3} = 139$ s ≈ 2.3 min.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Second-order, $k = 0.05$ M⁻¹s⁻¹, $[A]_0 = 0.2$ M. Time to reach 0.05 M?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$1/[A] - 1/[A]_0 = kt \Rightarrow 20 - 5 = 0.05 t \Rightarrow t = 300$ s = 5 min.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** $k(25°C) = 10^{-4}$, $k(50°C) = 10^{-3}$. $E_a$?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\ln 10 = (E_a/8.314)(1/298 - 1/323)$.

$2.303 = E_a/8.314\cdot 2.60\times 10^{-4}$.

$E_a = 2.303\cdot 8.314/2.60\times 10^{-4} \approx 73.6$ kJ/mol.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Food spoilage.** Bacteria growth doubles per 10°C (Q₁₀ rule). Why does refrigeration extend shelf life?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Refrigerator at 4°C vs room 22°C: ΔT = 18°C → rate slowed by ~$2^{1.8} \approx 3.5$× → shelf life extended ~3.5×.

Freezer at -18°C vs room 22°C: ΔT = 40°C → ~$2^4 = 16$× slower → ~16× shelf life.

This is why refrigeration is so effective despite seemingly small temperature change — exponential kinetics is unforgiving.

Same physics underlies cryopreservation, drug stability testing, ice cores preserving ancient samples.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why doesn't a catalyst affect equilibrium?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Catalyst lowers $E_a$ for forward AND reverse reactions equally (same TS).

So both directions speed up by same factor → equilibrium reached faster but at same position.

If catalysts could shift equilibrium, second law would be violated: you could extract work from a system at equilibrium by adding catalyst.

This is why catalysts are valuable: they don't change *what* you can make, but **how fast** you can make it. Combined with cleverness (e.g., removing product as it forms — Le Chatelier), this is the basis of industrial chemistry.
</ExA>
