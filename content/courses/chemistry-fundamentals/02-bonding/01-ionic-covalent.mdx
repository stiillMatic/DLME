# Ionic and Covalent Bonds

## The Big Picture

Atoms bond to lower their energy — typically by achieving noble-gas-like electron configurations. **Ionic bonds** transfer electrons (one atom gives, one takes), forming oppositely charged ions held by electrostatic attraction. **Covalent bonds** share electrons. Most real bonds are intermediate ("polar covalent"). Understanding bond type predicts properties: melting point, conductivity, solubility, mechanical behavior.

## Ionic Bonds

When **electronegativity difference is large** (typically > ~1.7), one atom captures electrons from the other.

Example: NaCl. Na (low electronegativity, 0.93) gives up electron → Na⁺. Cl (high, 3.16) takes it → Cl⁻. Coulomb attraction holds them in crystal lattice.

**Properties of ionic compounds:**
- High melting points (strong electrostatic forces).
- Brittle (sliding layers brings like charges adjacent → repulsion).
- Conduct when molten or dissolved (free ions), not as solids.
- Often water-soluble (water solvates ions).

## Covalent Bonds

When **electronegativities are similar** (small difference), atoms share electrons.

Example: H₂. Each H needs 1 more electron for stable configuration; share 1 each, both effectively have 2.

**Properties of covalent compounds:**
- Lower melting points (intermolecular forces weak compared to intramolecular bonds).
- Don't conduct (no free charges).
- Often gases or low-boiling liquids.
- Network covalent (diamond, SiO₂): extremely high melting due to entire crystal being one molecule.

## Polar Covalent

Most real bonds aren't pure ionic or pure covalent — intermediate.

| Δχ (electronegativity diff) | Bond type |
|---|---|
| 0 – 0.4 | Nonpolar covalent |
| 0.4 – 1.7 | Polar covalent |
| > 1.7 | Ionic |

H-Cl: $\Delta\chi = 0.96$ → polar covalent. Cl pulls electron density toward itself; H end is partially positive ($\delta^+$), Cl end partially negative ($\delta^-$).

## Bond Strength

| Bond | Strength (kJ/mol) |
|---|---|
| H-H | 436 |
| C-C | 348 |
| C=C | 614 |
| C≡C | 839 |
| C-H | 414 |
| O=O | 498 |
| N≡N | 945 |

Multiple bonds stronger and shorter than single. Inert N₂ has the strongest common bond.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Ionic = always 100% transfer."**

Even in NaCl, electron isn't 100% on Cl. Real bonds have shared character. The ionic/covalent distinction is a spectrum.

**Misconception 2: "Strong bonds = high melting point."**

For molecular substances, melting point depends on **intermolecular** forces, not bond strength inside molecules. Water has weak H-bonds but boils at 100°C; methane has similar molecular weight but boils at -161°C.

**Misconception 3: "Covalent bonds are weak."**

Individual covalent bonds are stronger than ionic bonds. But ionic substances form 3D lattices where every cation interacts with many anions → high lattice energy.

## Engineering Connection

- **Materials selection:** ionic (ceramic) vs covalent (polymer) vs metallic — different properties for different needs.
- **Solubility design:** "like dissolves like" — polar dissolves polar, nonpolar dissolves nonpolar.
- **Polymer engineering:** covalent backbones, intermolecular forces dictate mechanical properties.
- **Battery design:** ion mobility in electrolytes (ionic) vs electron flow in electrodes (covalent/metallic).
- **Semiconductor manufacturing:** silicon's covalent bonding + dopants control electron mobility.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Bond type in NaF, HBr, O₂?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
NaF: large Δχ (~3.0) → **ionic**.

HBr: Δχ ~0.8 → **polar covalent**.

O₂: same atoms → **nonpolar covalent**.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Why is NaCl high-melting (801°C) but H₂O melts at 0°C?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
NaCl: ionic crystal — millions of Na⁺-Cl⁻ attractions per cm³. Each ion bonded to 6 neighbors. Lattice energy huge.

H₂O: covalent molecules held by relatively weak intermolecular H-bonds. To melt, only the weak H-bonds need to break, not the covalent O-H bonds.

Crystal type (ionic, molecular, network) dominates melting behavior.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Diamond melts at 3550°C. Why so high?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Diamond is **network covalent**: each C bonded to 4 others covalently throughout entire crystal. To melt, must break the covalent bonds — energy comparable to vaporization.

Compare with graphite: layers of covalent bonds, weak intermolecular forces between layers → graphite is layered, soft, conductive in plane.

Same atoms, different bonding structure → vastly different properties. Allotropy.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Battery electrolyte.** Why does NaCl in water conduct electricity but solid NaCl doesn't?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Solid NaCl: ions locked in lattice — can't move.

Dissolved (or molten) NaCl: ions free to move → carry current.

Same compound, different state. This mobility distinction is crucial for batteries (need ionic conduction in electrolyte, electronic in electrodes).

Lithium-ion batteries use Li-conducting solid or liquid electrolytes for this reason.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why is water a good solvent for ionic compounds but oil isn't?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Water is **polar** — H end $\delta^+$, O end $\delta^-$. Polar molecules can surround ions, with appropriate end pointing toward each ion → **solvation** lowers energy enough to overcome lattice energy.

Oil is **nonpolar** — no charged regions. Can't stabilize ions → ionic compounds don't dissolve.

"Like dissolves like": polar solvents dissolve polar/ionic; nonpolar dissolve nonpolar (oils, fats).

This rule governs separation chemistry, drug formulation, and biological membrane transport.
</ExA>
