# Metallic Bonding

In metals, valence electrons are not bound to individual atoms but instead form a **sea of delocalized electrons** — sometimes called the "electron gas" — surrounding a lattice of positive metal ions.

## Drude Model

The positive ion cores are held in place by their mutual attraction to the shared electron cloud. This non-directional bonding is what gives metals their characteristic properties:

| Property | Origin |
|---|---|
| High electrical conductivity | Free electrons carry current |
| High thermal conductivity | Free electrons transfer kinetic energy |
| Malleability / ductility | Ion layers can slide without breaking bonds |
| Metallic luster | Free electrons absorb and re-emit photons |
| High melting point (refractory metals) | Strong ion-electron attraction |

## Comparison with Other Bond Types

| Bond type | Example | Directionality | Conductivity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ionic | NaCl | Non-directional | Poor (solid), good (molten) |
| Covalent | Diamond | Highly directional | Poor |
| Metallic | Fe, Al, Cu | Non-directional | Excellent |

## Alloying Effects

Pure metals are often soft. **Alloying** — dissolving one metal into another — increases strength through:

- **Solid solution strengthening**: foreign atoms distort the lattice, impeding dislocation motion
- **Precipitation hardening**: fine precipitate particles block dislocations (e.g., Al 7075 in aerospace)

## Bond Energy and Young's Modulus

The curvature of the interatomic potential well at equilibrium determines Young's modulus $E$. Metals with deeper, narrower wells (e.g., W, Mo) have higher $E$.

$$E \propto \frac{d^2 U}{dr^2}\bigg|_{r=r_0}$$

This is why tungsten ($E \approx 400$ GPa) is used in high-temperature applications.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
Rank the following in order of increasing electrical conductivity and explain: diamond, copper, NaCl (solid), aluminum.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**Increasing conductivity:** Diamond < NaCl (solid) < Aluminum < Copper

- **Diamond**: covalent bonds, no free electrons — excellent insulator
- **NaCl (solid)**: ionic, ions locked in lattice — very poor conductor (ions mobile only when molten/dissolved)
- **Aluminum**: metallic bonding, free electrons — good conductor ($\sigma \approx 3.5 \times 10^7$ S/m)
- **Copper**: metallic bonding, more free electrons per volume — better conductor ($\sigma \approx 5.8 \times 10^7$ S/m)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
Why can metals be hammered into thin sheets (malleable) while ionic crystals like NaCl shatter when struck?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
In metals, the **non-directional electron sea** allows ion layers to slide past each other without breaking bonds — the electrons simply redistribute. This is malleability.

In ionic crystals, layers are held by **directional electrostatic forces** between alternating + and − ions. Sliding shifts like charges adjacent to each other, creating strong repulsion that causes fracture rather than deformation.
</ExA>
