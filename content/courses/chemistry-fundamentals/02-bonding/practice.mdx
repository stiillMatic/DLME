# Chapter 2 Practice — Chemical Bonding

<ExQ>
**1.** Predict bonding type: NaCl, Cu, SiO₂, polymer.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
NaCl: ionic. Cu: metallic. SiO₂: covalent (and ionic mix). Polymer: covalent + secondary (H-bonds, van der Waals).
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Why does diamond have higher melting point than graphite?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Diamond has 3D covalent network — every atom bonded in 3D. Graphite has 2D covalent layers + weak van der Waals between layers.

Melting requires breaking 3D bonds in diamond, only 2D + weak forces in graphite.

Diamond melts above 4000°C, graphite sublimes around 3650°C.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3. Engineering: Why metals conduct electricity.**
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Metallic bonding has delocalized electrons (electron sea) free to move under applied field → high electrical conductivity.

Insulators (covalent, ionic) have electrons localized in bonds — no free movement.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Conceptual.** Why are ionic compounds typically brittle?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Layers of alternating + and − ions. When sheared, like charges align next to each other → strong electrostatic repulsion → fracture.

Metals don't have this issue: free electrons reorganize as planes slide. Metallic bonding allows ductility.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5.** Bond energy of NaCl is 411 kJ/mol. Energy to break 1 mole?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Just 411 kJ. Per bond: $411\times 10^3/6.022\times 10^{23} = 6.82\times 10^{-19}$ J = 4.26 eV.

Compared to typical thermal energy at 300 K ($kT \approx 0.025$ eV), bonds are ~170× stronger — explains why salt is solid at room temperature.
</ExA>
