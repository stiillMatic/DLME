# Enthalpy and Hess's Law

## The Big Picture

Chemical reactions release or absorb heat. **Enthalpy** ($H$) is the thermodynamic quantity that captures this — and a key insight: enthalpy is a *state function*, depending only on initial and final states, not on path. **Hess's law** lets us compute reaction enthalpies indirectly by combining easier-to-measure reactions. This is the foundation of thermochemistry — sizing chemical reactors, designing fuel cells, predicting feasibility of combustion.

## Definitions

**Enthalpy:** $H = U + PV$.

For reactions at constant pressure: $\Delta H = Q_{\text{rxn}}$.

- $\Delta H < 0$: exothermic (releases heat)
- $\Delta H > 0$: endothermic (absorbs heat)

## Standard Enthalpy of Formation

$\Delta H_f^\circ$: enthalpy change when 1 mol of compound forms from elements in standard states.

By convention, $\Delta H_f^\circ = 0$ for elements (e.g., O₂, N₂, C(graphite)).

## Hess's Law

$$\Delta H_{\text{rxn}} = \sum\Delta H_f^\circ(\text{products}) - \sum\Delta H_f^\circ(\text{reactants})$$

Adding two reactions: enthalpies add. Reversing a reaction: sign of $\Delta H$ flips. Multiplying by stoichiometric factor: scales $\Delta H$.

## Worked Example

Combustion of methane: $\text{CH}_4 + 2\text{O}_2 \to \text{CO}_2 + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}$.

$\Delta H_f^\circ$: CH₄ = -75, CO₂ = -394, H₂O = -286 kJ/mol; O₂ = 0.

$\Delta H_{\text{rxn}} = (-394) + 2(-286) - (-75) - 0 = -891$ kJ/mol.

So 891 kJ released per mole CH₄ — basis of natural-gas heating energy.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Enthalpy is the same as energy."**

$H = U + PV$. At constant pressure, $\Delta H$ equals heat. But strictly, $H$ includes the $PV$ work term — different from $U$.

**Misconception 2: "Reaction with $\Delta H < 0$ always happens."**

Spontaneity depends on Gibbs free energy: $\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$. Some exothermic reactions are slow (kinetically blocked); some endothermic are spontaneous (entropy-driven, e.g., dissolution).

**Misconception 3: "Standard enthalpy of formation = bond energy."**

$\Delta H_f$ accounts for *whole molecule formation* from elements. Bond energy is per-bond. Related but distinct.

## Engineering Connection

- **Combustion engine design:** heat of combustion sets the energy available per cycle.
- **Heat exchangers in chemical plants:** balance reaction heat with cooling capacity.
- **Battery energy density:** related to enthalpy of electrochemical reactions.
- **Industrial furnaces:** size to deliver $\Delta H_{\text{rxn}}$ per unit time.
- **Process safety:** runaway reactions release $\Delta H$ rapidly — sizing relief systems requires knowing this.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Find $\Delta H$ for $2\text{H}_2 + \text{O}_2 \to 2\text{H}_2\text{O}$ (liquid). $\Delta H_f^\circ$(H₂O,l) = -286 kJ/mol.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\Delta H = 2(-286) - 2(0) - 0 = -572$ kJ.

286 kJ per mole of water formed. Energy from hydrogen fuel cells.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** $\text{C} + \text{O}_2 \to \text{CO}_2$, $\Delta H_1 = -394$ kJ/mol.
$\text{CO} + \tfrac{1}{2}\text{O}_2 \to \text{CO}_2$, $\Delta H_2 = -283$ kJ/mol.

Find $\Delta H$ for $\text{C} + \tfrac{1}{2}\text{O}_2 \to \text{CO}$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Subtract: $\Delta H_1 - \Delta H_2 = -394 - (-283) = -111$ kJ/mol.

Hess's law: combine known reactions to find unknown.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3. Engineering: Heating value.** Methane $\Delta H_{\text{combustion}} = -891$ kJ/mol. How much heat from 1 kg CH₄?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
1 kg CH₄ = 1000/16 = 62.5 mol.

Heat = $62.5\cdot 891 = 55.7$ MJ.

Compare gasoline (~44 MJ/kg) — methane has higher specific energy.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Conceptual.** Why is Hess's law useful for reactions you cannot directly measure?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Many reactions are too slow, dangerous, or impossible at standard conditions. But you can often *measure* combustion (with O₂) easily.

Combine measurable reactions algebraically to compute the inaccessible one. This is how most modern thermochemical tables are constructed.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5.** Reaction at constant pressure: $\Delta H = -200$ kJ. If volume increases by 0.5 L at 1 atm, find $\Delta U$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\Delta H = \Delta U + \Delta(PV)$. Const $P$: $\Delta(PV) = P\Delta V = 101325\cdot 5\times 10^{-4} = 50.7$ J ≈ 0.05 kJ.

$\Delta U = \Delta H - P\Delta V = -200 - 0.05 = -200.05$ kJ ≈ $-200$ kJ.

For most chemical reactions in solution, $\Delta H \approx \Delta U$ (PV term small). For gas-phase reactions, the PV term can be significant.
</ExA>
