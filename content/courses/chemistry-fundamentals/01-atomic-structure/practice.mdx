# Chapter 1 Practice — Atomic Structure

<ExQ>
**1.** How many electrons in a neutral chlorine atom?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Z = 17 → 17 electrons in neutral atom.

Configuration: $[\text{Ne}]\,3s^2 3p^5$. 7 valence (group 17, halogen).
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Predict electron configuration of Mn²⁺ (manganese ion).
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Mn (Z=25): $[\text{Ar}]\,4s^2 3d^5$.

Form Mn²⁺ by removing 2 electrons. Remove **4s first** (4s is outer, easier to ionize):

Mn²⁺: $[\text{Ar}]\,3d^5$ — half-filled 3d (extra stable).

Counter-intuitive but correct: transition metals lose s-electrons before d when ionizing.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Rank by atomic radius: Na, Mg, Cl, Cs.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Cs > Na > Mg > Cl.

Cs: 6th period, alkali metal — largest.
Na: 3rd period, alkali.
Mg: same period as Na but right of it (smaller, higher Z_eff).
Cl: same period, far right (smallest).
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Phosphor for lighting.** Eu³⁺ ($f^6$) glows red in some materials. Why does the f electron configuration matter?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
f electrons are deep inside (shielded by 5s, 5p) — transitions among them give sharp, narrow emission lines.

Specific f-f transitions in Eu³⁺ produce intense red around 615 nm.

Used in: TV phosphors (now LEDs use similar physics), fluorescent lamps, security inks. Each rare earth has characteristic emission used for color tuning.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why is the first ionization energy of N higher than O?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
N (Z=7): $1s^2 2s^2 2p^3$ — exactly half-filled 2p (extra stable by Hund's rule + symmetry).

O (Z=8): $1s^2 2s^2 2p^4$ — one paired electron in 2p, which experiences repulsion.

Removing that paired electron releases the repulsion energy, making ionization easier.

So O has slightly **lower** first ionization energy than N — an exception to the general "increase across period" trend.

Other exceptions: similar half-filled stability at d⁵, f⁷ configurations.
</ExA>
