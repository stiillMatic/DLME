# The Atomic Model and Periodic Table

## The Big Picture

The atom is the basic unit of chemistry: a nucleus of protons (+) and neutrons (neutral) surrounded by electrons (−). The atomic number $Z$ — number of protons — defines the element. The **periodic table** organizes elements by Z and groups them by similar chemical behavior, reflecting their electron arrangements. Understanding the table's layout is the first key to predicting chemistry.

## Structure of the Atom

| Particle | Charge | Mass (amu) | Location |
|---|---|---|---|
| Proton | +1 | 1.0073 | Nucleus |
| Neutron | 0 | 1.0087 | Nucleus |
| Electron | −1 | 0.000549 | Around nucleus |

Atomic number $Z$ = number of protons.
Mass number $A$ = protons + neutrons.
Isotopes: same Z, different A.

The mass is concentrated in the nucleus; electrons occupy nearly all the volume.

## Historical Models

- **Dalton (1808):** atoms are indivisible spheres.
- **Thomson (1897):** "plum pudding" — electrons in positive sphere.
- **Rutherford (1911):** dense nucleus, electrons orbiting (after gold foil experiment).
- **Bohr (1913):** quantized orbits explained hydrogen spectra.
- **Quantum mechanics (1925+):** electrons described by probabilistic wavefunctions.

Modern picture: electrons in **orbitals** (3D regions of probability), not orbits.

## The Periodic Table

Arranged by increasing $Z$. Two key dimensions:

**Periods (rows):** principal quantum number $n$.
**Groups (columns):** number of valence electrons.

Major regions:
- **s-block** (groups 1-2): alkali, alkaline earth.
- **p-block** (groups 13-18): main group, includes noble gases.
- **d-block** (transition metals).
- **f-block** (lanthanides, actinides).

## Periodic Trends

| Property | Trend across period | Trend down group |
|---|---|---|
| Atomic radius | Decreases | Increases |
| Ionization energy | Increases | Decreases |
| Electronegativity | Increases | Decreases |
| Metallic character | Decreases | Increases |

These trends emerge from effective nuclear charge and shell structure.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Electrons orbit like planets."**

Old Bohr model. Modern: electrons exist in probability clouds (orbitals). Specific orbit unknowable (uncertainty principle).

**Misconception 2: "Atomic mass = sum of particles' masses."**

Less! Mass defect ($E = mc^2$) — some mass converted to binding energy when nucleus forms. About 0.7% for iron-56.

**Misconception 3: "Periodic table is alphabetical or by abundance."**

By atomic number Z, which reveals chemistry. Mendeleev's genius: organizing by then-unknown principle (electronic structure).

## Engineering Connection

- **Materials selection:** properties (conductivity, strength) follow periodic trends.
- **Semiconductors:** Si (group 14) doped with group 13 (B) or 15 (P) for p/n type.
- **Alloying:** mixing metals from same or adjacent groups for tailored properties.
- **Catalysis:** transition metals (d-block) provide variable oxidation states for catalytic cycles.
- **Drug design:** atomic radii and electronegativity guide molecular interactions.
- **Nuclear engineering:** mass differences in fission/fusion drive nuclear power.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Sodium (Na). How many protons, electrons (neutral), valence electrons?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Z = 11. So **11 protons**, **11 electrons** (neutral atom).

Electron configuration: $1s^2 2s^2 2p^6 3s^1$. Outermost shell has 1 electron → **1 valence electron**.

Group 1 element → loses 1 electron easily → forms Na⁺.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Isotope $^{14}_{6}C$. Protons, neutrons, electrons?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Z = 6 → 6 protons.

A = 14 → 14 - 6 = 8 neutrons.

Neutral atom: 6 electrons.

(Carbon-14: radioactive, half-life 5730 years, used for dating organic material.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Which has higher ionization energy: Na or Cl? Why?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**Chlorine**, by far.

Across period: Z increases, effective nuclear charge increases, electrons pulled tighter → harder to ionize.

Na: 496 kJ/mol. Cl: 1251 kJ/mol. Difference of ~750 kJ/mol explains why Na readily forms Na⁺ but Cl prefers to gain electron (forms Cl⁻).
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Semiconductor doping.** To make n-type silicon, dope with phosphorus. Why?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Si (group 14): 4 valence electrons. P (group 15): 5 valence electrons.

Replacing one Si atom with P: P uses 4 electrons for bonding, leaves 1 extra ("donor electron"). Easily promoted to conduction band.

P doping creates n-type semiconductor — extra mobile electrons.

Boron (group 13, 3 valence) creates p-type — missing electron is a mobile "hole."

The chemistry of doping is straight periodic table.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why are noble gases unreactive?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Noble gases have **complete valence shells**: 2 electrons (He) or 8 (Ne, Ar, etc.).

This stable electron configuration is the lowest energy state — no driver to form bonds.

Other elements gain/lose/share electrons to achieve noble gas configuration (octet rule). This explains why Na⁺ (like Ne) and Cl⁻ (like Ar) are so stable.

Modern note: heavier noble gases (Kr, Xe) can form compounds with very electronegative atoms, but require extreme conditions.
</ExA>
