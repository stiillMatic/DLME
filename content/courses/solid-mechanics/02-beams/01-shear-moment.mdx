# Shear Force and Bending Moment Diagrams

## The Big Picture

A beam under load develops internal **shear forces** ($V$) and **bending moments** ($M$) that vary along its length. To design the beam — pick its cross-section, check stress, predict deflection — we need to know $V(x)$ and $M(x)$. Drawing **shear and moment diagrams** is the universal first step in structural analysis. Once you can read these diagrams, you can locate critical points (max shear, max moment) and design accordingly.

## Sign Conventions

Cut the beam at section $x$. Internal forces on the left part of the cut:

- **Shear $V$:** positive if it acts downward on the *left* face (and upward on the right).
- **Moment $M$:** positive if it tends to bend the beam concave-up (sagging).

(Conventions vary by textbook; consistency matters more than absolute sign.)

## Relationships (FUNDAMENTAL)

For distributed load $w(x)$ (downward positive):

$$\frac{dV}{dx} = -w(x), \qquad \frac{dM}{dx} = V(x)$$

In words: the slope of $V$ equals the negative load; the slope of $M$ equals the shear.

These come from equilibrium of an infinitesimal beam element.

**Consequences:**
- Concentrated loads cause **jumps** in $V$.
- Concentrated moments cause **jumps** in $M$.
- $V = 0$ at a maximum or minimum of $M$.

## Procedure

1. Find reactions at supports (statics).
2. Cut the beam at convenient sections.
3. Apply equilibrium to find $V$, $M$ at each section.
4. Plot $V(x)$ and $M(x)$.

## Worked Example — Simply Supported Beam, Point Load at Center

Length $L$, point load $P$ at $x = L/2$. Reactions: $R_A = R_B = P/2$.

**For $0 < x < L/2$:** Cut at $x$. Sum of forces on left = $R_A - V = 0 \Rightarrow V = P/2$.

Moment about cut: $M = R_A x = Px/2$ (linear, increasing).

**For $L/2 < x < L$:** $V = R_A - P = -P/2$ (constant).

$M = R_A x - P(x - L/2) = Px/2 - P(x - L/2) = -Px/2 + PL/2$ (linear, decreasing).

**Maximum $M = PL/4$ at midspan.** Shear has a sign change at midspan.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Bending moment is at supports."**

Actually max moment usually occurs *between* supports — where shear changes sign.

**Misconception 2: "Distributed load = constant shear."**

Distributed load gives **linearly varying** shear ($V(x) = R - wx$). Constant shear means *no* distributed load.

**Misconception 3: "Cantilever has zero moment at fixed end."**

Cantilever has *maximum* reaction moment at the fixed end. The free end has zero moment (no load beyond it).

## Engineering Connection

- **Beam design:** size cross-section to handle max moment without yielding.
- **Bridge design:** moment diagrams under different live load patterns determine safety.
- **Truss vs. beam:** trusses carry only axial loads; beams carry shear and moment too.
- **Crane booms:** the bending moment along the boom dictates structural design.
- **Aircraft wings:** bending moment due to aerodynamic loads sizes the spars.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Simply supported beam, length 6 m, uniform load $w = 2$ kN/m. Find max shear and max moment.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Reactions: $R = wL/2 = 6$ kN at each end.

$V(x) = 6 - 2x$. Max at supports: $|V_{\max}| = 6$ kN.

$M(x) = 6x - x^2$. Maximum at midspan: $M_{\max} = 18 - 9 = 9$ kN·m.

(Or directly: $M_{\max} = wL^2/8 = 9$ ✓.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Cantilever beam, length 4 m, fixed at left, point load 5 kN at right tip. Find $V$ and $M$ at the fixed end.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Free body: at fixed end, support exerts $V = 5$ kN (upward) and $M = 5\cdot 4 = 20$ kN·m (counterclockwise).

Throughout beam: $V = 5$ kN constant. $M$ varies linearly from 20 (fixed end) to 0 (tip).
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3. Conceptual.** Why does $V = 0$ at the location of maximum bending moment?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$dM/dx = V$. At a maximum (or minimum) of $M$, the derivative is zero.

So locating points where $V = 0$ pinpoints where $M$ is at extrema — the most critical sections for stress.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering.** A simply supported beam (length 8 m) carries a triangular load: zero at left, $w_0 = 4$ kN/m at right. Find the location and value of max moment.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Total load: $W = w_0 L/2 = 16$ kN, centroid at $2L/3 = 5.33$ m from left.

Reactions: $R_A + R_B = 16$. Sum moments about A: $R_B\cdot 8 = 16\cdot 5.33 \Rightarrow R_B = 10.67$, $R_A = 5.33$.

$w(x) = w_0 x/L = x/2$ kN/m. $V(x) = R_A - \int_0^x w\,dx' = 5.33 - x^2/4$.

$V = 0$: $x^2 = 21.33 \Rightarrow x = 4.62$ m.

$M(4.62) = R_A\cdot 4.62 - \int_0^{4.62}(x'/2)(4.62 - x')\,dx'$. Computing: $M_{\max} \approx 16.4$ kN·m.

(Triangular loads have asymmetric reactions and offset max-moment points.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Multi-step.** Two point loads of 10 kN at 2 m and 4 m from left of a 6 m simply supported beam. Find max moment.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Reactions by symmetry: $R_A = R_B = 10$ kN.

For $0 < x < 2$: $V = 10$, $M = 10x$.
At $x = 2$: $M = 20$.
For $2 < x < 4$: $V = 0$, $M = 20$ (constant).
For $4 < x < 6$: $V = -10$, $M = 20 - 10(x-4)$, ending at 0.

Max $M = 20$ kN·m, between the two point loads.
</ExA>
