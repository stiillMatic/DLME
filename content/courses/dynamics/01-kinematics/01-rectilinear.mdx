# Rectilinear and Curvilinear Motion

## The Big Picture

Dynamics describes the motion of bodies under forces. Before getting to forces, we need a complete language for describing motion itself: position, velocity, acceleration in 1D (rectilinear) and 2D/3D (curvilinear). The most useful insight: in curved motion, acceleration has both a *tangential* (changing speed) and *normal* (changing direction) component. Mastering this decomposition unlocks the dynamics of cars on roads, satellites in orbit, and pendulums.

## Rectilinear (1D) Motion

Position $s(t)$, velocity $v = ds/dt$, acceleration $a = dv/dt = d^2s/dt^2$.

Useful relation: $a\,ds = v\,dv$ (eliminate $t$). Used when $a$ depends on $s$ rather than $t$.

**Example.** Spring force gives $a = -ks/m$. Then:
$\int v\,dv = -k/m\int s\,ds \Rightarrow v^2/2 = -(k/m)s^2/2 + C$ — energy-like result.

## Curvilinear Motion: Tangent-Normal (TN) Coordinates

Along a curved path, decompose velocity and acceleration along:

- $\hat{\mathbf{t}}$: unit tangent (direction of motion)
- $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$: unit normal (perpendicular, toward center of curvature)

**Velocity:** $\mathbf{v} = v\hat{\mathbf{t}}$ (always along path).

**Acceleration:**
$$\mathbf{a} = \frac{dv}{dt}\hat{\mathbf{t}} + \frac{v^2}{\rho}\hat{\mathbf{n}}$$

- Tangential: $a_t = dv/dt$ (changes speed)
- Normal: $a_n = v^2/\rho$ (changes direction; $\rho$ = radius of curvature)

For uniform circular motion: $a_t = 0$, $a_n = v^2/r$ — constant centripetal.

## Curvilinear Motion: Polar Coordinates

In 2D with origin and angle $\theta$:

**Position:** $\mathbf{r} = r\hat{\mathbf{r}}$.

**Velocity:** $\mathbf{v} = \dot r\hat{\mathbf{r}} + r\dot\theta\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$.

**Acceleration:** $\mathbf{a} = (\ddot r - r\dot\theta^2)\hat{\mathbf{r}} + (r\ddot\theta + 2\dot r\dot\theta)\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$.

The "$2\dot r\dot\theta$" is the **Coriolis-like** term — appears when both radial and angular motion change.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Acceleration always points the way velocity points."**

In curvilinear motion, $\mathbf{a}$ has both tangential AND normal components. Even at constant speed (turning), acceleration is nonzero — pointing toward the center.

**Misconception 2: "Centripetal force is a separate force."**

It's not — it's the *resultant* of real forces (tension, gravity, friction) directed toward center. The "centripetal" label describes its function, not its origin.

**Misconception 3: "Polar coordinate acceleration is just $\ddot r$ and $r\ddot\theta$."**

The complete radial acceleration includes $-r\dot\theta^2$ (centripetal); the angular includes $2\dot r\dot\theta$ (Coriolis-like). Forgetting these gives wrong dynamics.

## Engineering Connection

- **Vehicle dynamics:** lateral acceleration $v^2/r$ in turns determines tire force needed and roll-over risk.
- **Roller coaster design:** $a_n$ at loops sets the gravity multiples ("g-forces") felt by riders.
- **Satellite orbits:** $v_{\text{orbit}}^2/r = g_{\text{eff}}$ at orbital altitude — circular orbit speed.
- **Robotic arm trajectory planning:** specify desired path and speed profile, compute joint accelerations via TN decomposition.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** A car accelerates at constant 2 m/s² along a circular track of radius 100 m, starting from rest. Find the magnitude of acceleration after 5 s.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
At $t=5$: $v = 10$ m/s.
$a_t = 2$ m/s², $a_n = v^2/r = 100/100 = 1$ m/s².
$|a| = \sqrt{4 + 1} = \sqrt 5 \approx 2.24$ m/s².
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** A particle moves along $y = x^2$ at constant speed 5 m/s. Find $a_n$ at $x = 1$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Curvature $\kappa = |y''|/(1 + y'^2)^{3/2}$. At $x = 1$: $y' = 2$, $y'' = 2$, $\kappa = 2/(5)^{3/2} = 0.179$.

$a_n = v^2 \kappa = 25\cdot 0.179 = 4.47$ m/s².
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3. Engineering: Race car.** Track radius 50 m, speed 30 m/s. Find $a_n$. If $\mu = 0.8$, can the tires sustain this turn?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$a_n = 900/50 = 18$ m/s².

Max friction acceleration = $\mu g = 0.8\cdot 9.81 = 7.85$ m/s². 

$18 > 7.85$ — tires CANNOT sustain the turn. Car would skid. Maximum safe speed: $v = \sqrt{\mu g r} = \sqrt{392.4} \approx 19.8$ m/s ≈ 71 km/h.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Conceptual.** Why is the Coriolis-like term $2\dot r\dot\theta$ in polar coordinates not zero, even at constant $r$?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
At constant $r$, $\dot r = 0$, so the term IS zero. The Coriolis term arises only when both radial and angular motion are nonzero — i.e., a particle moving outward in a rotating frame.

Famously: water spinning down a drain (in idealized Coriolis-only context) curves due to this term in rotating coordinates.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Multi-step.** A satellite in circular orbit at altitude 400 km. Earth radius 6378 km, $g$ at orbit ≈ 8.66 m/s². Find orbital speed and period.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$g = v^2/r \Rightarrow v = \sqrt{g r} = \sqrt{8.66\cdot 6{,}778{,}000} = 7659$ m/s ≈ 27,600 km/h.

Period: $T = 2\pi r/v = 5559$ s ≈ 92.6 min.

This matches LEO satellite values (ISS, ~93 min orbit).
</ExA>
