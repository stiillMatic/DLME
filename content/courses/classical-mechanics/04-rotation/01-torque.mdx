# Torque and Rotational Inertia

## The Big Picture

Newton's second law for rotation: **torque equals moment of inertia times angular acceleration**. Torque is the "twisting force" â€” depends on both force magnitude and where it's applied (lever arm). Moment of inertia is rotational mass â€” depends on how mass is distributed relative to the rotation axis. These two quantities govern every spinning system: gears, wheels, robotic arms, planets, atoms.

## Torque

$$\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{F}$$

Magnitude: $\tau = rF\sin\theta = r_\perp F$, where $r_\perp$ is the **lever arm** (perpendicular distance from axis to line of force).

**Key insight:** apply force far from axis (large $r$) or perpendicular ($\sin\theta = 1$) for max torque.

Units: NÂ·m.

## Moment of Inertia

$$I = \int r^2\,dm$$

Resistance to angular acceleration. Depends on:
- Mass distribution
- Choice of axis

For point mass: $I = mr^2$.

Common solids (about symmetry axis):

| Object | $I$ |
|---|---|
| Solid disk/cylinder | $\tfrac{1}{2}MR^2$ |
| Hollow cylinder (thin shell) | $MR^2$ |
| Solid sphere | $\tfrac{2}{5}MR^2$ |
| Hollow sphere | $\tfrac{2}{3}MR^2$ |
| Thin rod (about center) | $\tfrac{1}{12}ML^2$ |
| Thin rod (about end) | $\tfrac{1}{3}ML^2$ |

**Parallel axis theorem:** $I = I_{\text{cm}} + Md^2$ where $d$ is distance from center-of-mass axis to parallel axis.

## Newton's Second Law for Rotation

$$\tau = I\alpha$$

Direct analog: $\tau \leftrightarrow F$, $I \leftrightarrow m$, $\alpha \leftrightarrow a$.

For multiple torques: $\sum\tau = I\alpha$.

## Rotational Kinematics

Mirror linear: with $\theta$ replacing $x$, $\omega$ replacing $v$, $\alpha$ replacing $a$:

$\omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t$
$\theta = \omega_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}\alpha t^2$
$\omega^2 = \omega_0^2 + 2\alpha\theta$

For constant angular acceleration.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Heavier objects always have larger $I$."**

Depends on distribution. A thin hoop of mass $M$ has $I = MR^2$, while a solid disk has $I = \tfrac{1}{2}MR^2$ â€” half. Mass at large $r$ contributes more.

**Misconception 2: "Torque is force Ă— distance."**

It's force Ă— *perpendicular* distance. Force along the radius (radial) has zero torque.

**Misconception 3: "$I$ is intrinsic to an object."**

Depends on the **axis** of rotation. A rod has different $I$ about its center vs. its end.

## Engineering Connection

- **Engine torque:** rotational analog of force â€” what an engine actually delivers.
- **Wrenches with longer handles:** larger lever arm = same torque with less force.
- **Flywheel energy storage:** large $I$ + high $\omega$ stores rotational KE.
- **Gyroscopes:** $I\omega$ = angular momentum, resists changes in orientation.
- **Robot arms:** $I$ varies with arm configuration; controllers must compensate.
- **Ship stability:** moment of inertia about vertical axis affects rolling response.
