# Angular Momentum

## The Big Picture

**Angular momentum** is the rotational equivalent of linear momentum. Like linear momentum, it's conserved when no external torque acts. This conservation is the deep reason behind a host of phenomena: spinning skaters, orbiting planets, gyroscope stability, neutron stars. Understanding angular momentum is essential for any rotating system.

## Definition

For a particle: $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{p}$.

For a rigid body rotating about an axis: $L = I\omega$.

Units: kg·m²/s = J·s.

## Conservation of Angular Momentum

For isolated system (no external torque):

$$\mathbf{L} = \text{const}$$

When mass distribution changes (changing $I$), $\omega$ adjusts: $I_1\omega_1 = I_2\omega_2$.

This is why a skater pulling arms in spins faster.

## Newton's Second Law for Rotation (General Form)

$$\boldsymbol{\tau} = \frac{d\mathbf{L}}{dt}$$

For constant $I$: $\tau = I\alpha$ (as before).
For changing $I$: more general form needed.

## Rotational Kinetic Energy

$$KE_{\text{rot}} = \tfrac{1}{2}I\omega^2 = \tfrac{L^2}{2I}$$

When $L$ conserved but $I$ decreases (skater pulls in arms): KE **increases**. Where does the energy come from? The skater's muscles — work done pulling arms inward against centrifugal effect.

## Gyroscopic Precession

A spinning gyroscope under torque doesn't fall — it **precesses**. Direction of $L$ rotates around vertical axis.

Precession rate: $\Omega = \tau/L = mgr/(I\omega)$.

The faster $\omega$ (larger $L$), the slower the precession. This stability is why bicycles can balance, why planets maintain their axes, why guidance gyroscopes work.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Angular momentum applies only to spinning objects."**

A particle moving in a straight line *also* has angular momentum about any axis not on its path. $L = mvr_\perp$.

**Misconception 2: "Rotational KE is conserved when $L$ is."**

No — these are independent conservation laws. KE conserved only without dissipation. $L$ conserved only without external torque. They can both hold or one can hold without the other.

**Misconception 3: "Gravity provides torque on orbital motion."**

For circular orbit, gravity points toward center (parallel to $\mathbf{r}$). Torque $\mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{F} = 0$ — central forces produce no torque. Hence orbital angular momentum is conserved (Kepler's second law).

## Engineering Connection

- **Gyroscopes:** navigation in aircraft, spacecraft, missiles. Heading inferred from $L$ direction.
- **Reaction wheels:** spacecraft attitude control via internal momentum transfer.
- **Helicopter rotors:** transfer angular momentum to air; tail rotor compensates.
- **Pulsars / neutron stars:** collapsed stars spin extremely fast (conservation as $I$ shrinks dramatically).
- **Power generation:** turbine rotors carry massive angular momentum, providing inertial stability to grids.
- **Bicycle stability:** wheel angular momentum aids balance.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Disk $I = 2$ kg·m² at $\omega = 10$ rad/s. Angular momentum?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$L = I\omega = 20$ kg·m²/s.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Skater $I_1 = 5$ kg·m² spinning at 2 rev/s. Pulls in arms, $I_2 = 1$ kg·m². New rate?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$L$ conserved: $I_1\omega_1 = I_2\omega_2$.

$\omega_2 = (5/1)\cdot 2 = 10$ rev/s. 5× faster.

Skater works against pseudo-centrifugal force when pulling in — energy comes from muscles.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Particle 0.5 kg moving at 4 m/s, perpendicular distance 3 m from axis. Angular momentum?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$L = mvr_\perp = 0.5\cdot 4\cdot 3 = 6$ kg·m²/s.

Linear motion has angular momentum about any external axis.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Reaction wheel.** Satellite has $I = 100$ kg·m². Wheel inside spins up by 50 rad/s, $I_{wheel} = 0.5$ kg·m². Satellite angular speed change?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Total $L$ conserved (no external torque). $\Delta L_{wheel} = 0.5\cdot 50 = 25$ kg·m²/s.

Satellite must absorb opposite: $\Delta\omega_{sat} = -25/100 = -0.25$ rad/s.

Satellite rotates the opposite way. This is **reaction wheel attitude control** — change orientation by spinning internal wheels. No fuel needed. Used in Hubble, ISS, most modern satellites.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why does a neutron star spin so fast (up to 700 Hz)?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Original star had angular momentum $L = I\omega$. Upon collapse to neutron star, radius shrinks from $\sim 10^9$ m to $\sim 10^4$ m → $I \propto R^2$ decreases by $\sim 10^{10}$.

Conservation: $\omega$ must increase by same factor. Original $\omega$ (~1/month) becomes ~700 Hz.

This is exactly how figure skaters work, just at cosmic scale. Pulsars are stars that "tucked their arms in."

The same physics ties everything from atoms to galaxies.
</ExA>
