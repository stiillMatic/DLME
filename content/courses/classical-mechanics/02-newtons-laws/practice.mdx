# Chapter 2 Practice — Newton's Laws

<ExQ>
**1.** A 5 kg box on horizontal frictionless floor pulled with 20 N. Find acceleration.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$F = ma \Rightarrow a = 20/5 = 4$ m/s².
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Block on incline angle 30°, mass 8 kg, friction coefficient 0.2. Find net acceleration down the incline.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Component of weight along incline: $mg\sin 30° = 39.24$ N.

Normal force: $mg\cos 30° = 67.95$ N. Friction: $0.2\cdot 67.95 = 13.59$ N (up incline).

Net force: $39.24 - 13.59 = 25.65$ N. $a = 25.65/8 = 3.21$ m/s².
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Atwood machine: $m_1 = 4$ kg, $m_2 = 6$ kg, frictionless pulley. Find acceleration and tension.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$a = (m_2 - m_1)g/(m_1 + m_2) = 2\cdot 9.81/10 = 1.96$ m/s².

$T = m_1(g + a) = 4(11.77) = 47.08$ N.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Conceptual.** Why don't action-reaction force pairs cancel?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
They act on **different objects**. Earth pulls you down ($-mg$); you pull Earth up ($+mg$). Each force changes a different object's motion.

Forces on a single object can cancel (equilibrium), but action-reaction pairs are on different objects — so neither cancels for either object's analysis.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Engineering.** A car of mass 1200 kg accelerates at 3 m/s². Find required net engine force; if $\mu = 0.6$ on dry pavement, can the tires deliver this force?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$F = ma = 3600$ N.

Max friction = $\mu mg = 0.6\cdot 1200\cdot 9.81 = 7063$ N.

$3600 < 7063$ ✓. Tires can deliver. (Above this, wheels would slip.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**6. Multi-step.** Two blocks (4 kg and 6 kg) on frictionless table, in contact. Force 30 N pushes from left. Find the contact force between them.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Total mass = 10 kg, $a = 3$ m/s².

For the 6 kg block alone (only contact force pushes it): $F_c = 6\cdot 3 = 18$ N.

Or check 4 kg block: $30 - F_c = 4\cdot 3 = 12 \Rightarrow F_c = 18$ N ✓.
</ExA>
