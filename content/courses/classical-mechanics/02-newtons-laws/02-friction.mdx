# Friction and Normal Forces

## The Big Picture

When two surfaces touch, two contact forces arise: **normal force** (perpendicular to surface) and **friction** (parallel). Friction is what makes walking, gripping, braking possible — and what makes things slow down. Despite being ubiquitous, friction is a complex microscopic phenomenon idealized as a simple coefficient. Understanding the standard model is essential for every mechanical design.

## Normal Force

When an object presses on a surface, the surface pushes back **perpendicular** to itself. This balances gravity (or other components) and prevents penetration.

For object on horizontal surface: $N = mg$.

For object on incline of angle $\theta$: $N = mg\cos\theta$.

For object with applied force component perpendicular: adjust accordingly.

## Friction

Friction opposes relative motion (or tendency thereof) and is proportional to normal force.

**Kinetic friction** (sliding):
$$f_k = \mu_k N$$

**Static friction** (not yet sliding):
$$f_s \leq \mu_s N$$

Static is *up to* $\mu_s N$ — adjusts to balance applied force until exceeded, then sliding begins.

Generally $\mu_s > \mu_k$. (Why "static gives way" feels like a sudden release.)

## Coefficients of Friction

| Surfaces | $\mu_s$ | $\mu_k$ |
|---|---|---|
| Steel on steel (dry) | 0.6 | 0.5 |
| Rubber on dry pavement | 0.9 | 0.7 |
| Rubber on wet pavement | 0.6 | 0.5 |
| Ice on ice | 0.1 | 0.03 |
| Teflon on teflon | 0.04 | 0.04 |

Note: ranges widely with surface conditions, temperature, lubrication.

## Worked Example: Block on Incline

Mass $m$ on incline angle $\theta$. Will it slide?

Forces along incline: gravity component $mg\sin\theta$ (down) vs friction $f_s$ (up).

Threshold: $mg\sin\theta = \mu_s mg\cos\theta \Rightarrow \tan\theta = \mu_s$.

So **critical angle** $\theta_c = \arctan(\mu_s)$. Above this, block slides.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Friction depends on area of contact."**

In the standard model, friction depends only on $N$, not area. Microscopically, surfaces touch only at "asperities" — true contact area is much smaller than apparent area and scales with $N$.

**Misconception 2: "Friction goes to zero at zero velocity."**

Kinetic friction continues until motion stops. Then static friction takes over.

**Misconception 3: "Heavier objects need more force to move."**

In one sense yes: $f_s = \mu_s mg$ scales with $m$. But also $F = ma$ — so acceleration $a = F/m$. If you maintain the same applied force, heavier objects accelerate slower; if you maintain the same acceleration, you need more force.

## Engineering Connection

- **Vehicle braking:** kinetic friction sets stopping distance. Anti-lock brakes (ABS) maintain wheels on the verge of slipping → max friction.
- **Tire design:** rubber compound, tread pattern optimize $\mu$ for various conditions.
- **Bearings and lubrication:** reduce friction in machinery → less wear, energy loss.
- **Grip and assembly:** bolts, nails rely on friction.
- **Friction welding:** intentional high-friction heating to fuse metals.
- **Rocket launches:** static friction limits how heavy a rocket can sit on its pad.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Block of mass 10 kg on horizontal surface, $\mu_s = 0.4$. Minimum force to start it moving?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$F_{\min} = \mu_s mg = 0.4\cdot 10\cdot 9.81 = 39.2$ N.

Less than this, static friction adjusts to match. Above, block accelerates.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Same block, sliding. Applied force 50 N, $\mu_k = 0.3$. Acceleration?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$f_k = \mu_k mg = 0.3\cdot 98.1 = 29.4$ N.

Net force: $50 - 29.4 = 20.6$ N. $a = 20.6/10 = 2.06$ m/s².
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Incline with $\mu_s = 0.5$. Critical angle?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\theta_c = \arctan(0.5) \approx 26.6°$. Steeper inclines: block slides.

This is the "angle of repose" — characteristic for granular materials (sand, grain).
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Car braking.** Car at 30 m/s, $\mu_k = 0.8$ (dry pavement). Stopping distance with locked wheels?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Deceleration: $a = \mu_k g = 7.85$ m/s².

Using $v^2 = u^2 - 2as$: $0 = 900 - 2(7.85)s$, $s = 57.3$ m.

With ABS keeping near static friction (higher $\mu$): shorter distance ~50 m.

Wet pavement (µ = 0.4): doubles to ~115 m. Why braking distance changes so much with conditions.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** A rope wrapped around a pole creates capstan friction. Why can wrapping 3 times let one person hold a much heavier load?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Capstan equation: $T_1/T_2 = e^{\mu\theta}$ where $\theta$ is wrap angle in radians.

For 3 wraps: $\theta = 6\pi \approx 18.8$. With $\mu = 0.3$: $e^{5.65} \approx 285$.

So 1 N of holding force can resist 285 N pulling force. Doubles per ~2 radians of wrap.

Boats are moored with just a few wraps around a cleat. Capstan winches use this principle to manage huge loads safely.
</ExA>
