# Free Body Diagrams

## The Big Picture

A **free body diagram (FBD)** is a sketch showing all forces acting on a single isolated body. It's the gateway to every dynamics problem. Done right, the FBD reduces a complex situation (multiple objects, pulleys, inclines) to a clean vector sum that Newton's second law can crunch. Done sloppily, it's the source of every wrong answer in mechanics.

## The 5-Step Procedure

1. **Choose** the body of interest (one object at a time).
2. **Sketch** it as a simple shape (often just a box or point).
3. **Identify** all external forces acting on it:
   - Weight $mg$ (downward, at center)
   - Normal $N$ (perpendicular to contact surface)
   - Tension $T$ (along rope, away from object)
   - Friction $f$ (parallel to surface, opposing motion or tendency)
   - Applied forces (pushes, pulls)
4. **Choose axes** (often along motion direction for convenience).
5. **Apply** $\sum\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$ in each direction.

## What Forces to Include — and Exclude

**Include only**: forces from *other bodies* acting on the chosen one.
**Exclude**: forces the chosen body exerts on others (those go on *their* FBDs).

Example: Block on table.
- ON block: weight (Earth pulls), normal (table pushes).
- BY block on table: weight (block pushes down on table) — not on block's FBD!

## Worked Example: Two Blocks on a Pulley (Atwood)

Two masses $m_1, m_2$ connected by rope over frictionless pulley.

**FBD of $m_1$:** weight $m_1 g$ down, tension $T$ up.
**FBD of $m_2$:** weight $m_2 g$ down, tension $T$ up.

Newton's 2nd law on each (taking $m_2 > m_1$, so $m_2$ accelerates down, $m_1$ up; same acceleration magnitude $a$):
- $m_1$: $T - m_1 g = m_1 a$
- $m_2$: $m_2 g - T = m_2 a$

Adding: $(m_2 - m_1)g = (m_1 + m_2)a \Rightarrow a = \dfrac{(m_2 - m_1)g}{m_1 + m_2}$.

Then $T = m_1(g + a)$.

## Coupled Systems

For systems of multiple objects: separate FBDs, separate equations. Internal forces (like tensions) appear with opposite signs in different equations and often cancel when added.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Include all forces around the object."**

Include only forces ON the object, not from it.

**Misconception 2: "Centripetal force is a force you draw."**

Centripetal force is the *net* of real forces (tension, gravity, etc.). It's the *role*, not a separate arrow. In an FBD, draw the real forces; their sum equals $ma_c$ if circular motion.

**Misconception 3: "Forces are independent of the choice of body."**

Forces always come in pairs (Newton's third law). Switching to a different body changes which forces are external.

**Misconception 4: "Diagonal forces should be drawn diagonally then 'projected later'."**

Always **decompose** into chosen axes immediately. Easier to track signs and balance equations.

## Engineering Connection

- **Structural analysis:** truss members analyzed via FBDs at joints (method of joints).
- **Robotics:** every link of a robot arm has its FBD; assemble for inverse dynamics.
- **Vehicle dynamics:** FBD of vehicle, tires, suspension — used for handling/stability design.
- **Aircraft:** FBD shows lift, drag, weight, thrust — the four fundamental aerodynamic forces.
- **Failure analysis:** complex assemblies broken down via FBDs to find the over-stressed component.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Block on frictionless incline angle 30°, $m = 5$ kg. Acceleration down the slope?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
FBD: weight $mg$ down, normal $N$ perpendicular to slope.

Along slope: $mg\sin 30° = ma$, so $a = g\sin 30° = 4.9$ m/s².

Perpendicular: $N = mg\cos 30° = 42.4$ N (used if friction present).
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Block 10 kg on horizontal surface, applied force 50 N at 30° above horizontal. $\mu_k = 0.2$. Acceleration?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
FBD: weight, normal, applied force at 30°, friction opposing motion.

Vertical: $N + 50\sin 30° = mg \Rightarrow N = 98.1 - 25 = 73.1$ N.

Friction: $f_k = 0.2\cdot 73.1 = 14.6$ N.

Horizontal: $50\cos 30° - 14.6 = ma \Rightarrow a = (43.3 - 14.6)/10 = 2.87$ m/s².

Lifting reduces normal force, reducing friction — useful trick.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Two blocks ($m_1 = 3$ kg, $m_2 = 5$ kg) connected on frictionless pulley. Acceleration and tension?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$a = (m_2 - m_1)g/(m_1 + m_2) = 2\cdot 9.81/8 = 2.45$ m/s².

$T = m_1(g + a) = 3\cdot 12.26 = 36.8$ N.

Always: $m_1 g < T < m_2 g$ in this Atwood setup.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Crane.** Box of 200 kg lifted by 2 cables, each at 45° from vertical. Tension in each?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
FBD on box: weight $mg = 1962$ N down, two cable tensions $T$ each at 45° from vertical.

Vertical: $2T\cos 45° = mg \Rightarrow T = mg/(2\cos 45°) = 1962/1.414 \approx 1387$ N.

Each cable carries 1387 N — higher than half the weight! Acute angle increases tension. Vertical lift would have $T = mg/2 = 981$ N each.

This is why lifting slings should hang close to vertical — angled slings amplify tension dangerously.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why isn't normal force always equal to weight?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$N = mg$ only when:
- Object on horizontal surface AND
- Vertical equilibrium AND
- No other vertical forces.

In other cases:
- Incline: $N = mg\cos\theta$.
- Accelerating elevator: $N = m(g \pm a)$.
- Pushed down or pulled up: $N$ adjusts.
- In free fall: $N = 0$ (apparent weightlessness).

Normal force is just the surface's response to keep object from penetrating — it adjusts as needed.
</ExA>
