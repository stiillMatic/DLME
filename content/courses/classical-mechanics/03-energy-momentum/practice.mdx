# Chapter 3 Practice — Energy and Momentum

<ExQ>
**1.** A 2 kg object falls from 10 m. Use energy conservation to find speed at the bottom.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$mgh = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 \Rightarrow v = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{196.2} \approx 14$ m/s.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Two carts on a frictionless track collide elastically. $m_1 = 2$ kg, $v_1 = 4$ m/s. $m_2 = 3$ kg at rest. Find post-collision velocities.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$v'_1 = (m_1 - m_2)v_1/(m_1 + m_2) = -1\cdot 4/5 = -0.8$ m/s (bounces back).

$v'_2 = 2m_1 v_1/(m_1 + m_2) = 4\cdot 4/5 = 3.2$ m/s.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Spring with $k = 500$ N/m compressed 5 cm. Energy stored?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$U = \tfrac{1}{2}(500)(0.0025) = 0.625$ J.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering.** Car (1500 kg) at 30 m/s impacts wall, comes to rest in 0.1 s. Find average force.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Impulse: $J = \Delta p = 1500\cdot(-30) = -45{,}000$ N·s.

$F = J/\Delta t = -450{,}000$ N (450 kN). Severe — typical crash.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** When is mechanical energy conserved?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
When only **conservative forces** (gravity, springs, electrostatic) do work. Friction, drag, and other non-conservative forces dissipate energy → not conserved.

In real systems, "conserved enough" requires that dissipation is negligible over the timescale of interest.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**6. Multi-step.** A bullet (50 g) embeds in a 10 kg block at rest on frictionless floor. After collision, the system moves at 2 m/s. Find the bullet's initial speed.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Conservation of momentum (perfectly inelastic):
$0.05 v + 0 = (0.05 + 10)(2) \Rightarrow v = 20.1/0.05 = 402$ m/s.

Energy is **not** conserved here (most kinetic energy lost to deformation/heat) — only momentum.
</ExA>
