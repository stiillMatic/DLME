# Potential Energy and Conservation

## The Big Picture

**Kinetic energy** comes from motion. **Potential energy** comes from configuration — position in a force field. When only *conservative* forces (gravity, spring, electrostatic) act, the sum $E = KE + PE$ is **conserved** — leading to one of the most powerful problem-solving tools in physics. Energy conservation lets you solve problems without ever computing forces or accelerations.

## Conservative vs Non-conservative Forces

**Conservative**: work done is path-independent. Examples: gravity, spring, electrostatic.

**Non-conservative**: work depends on path. Examples: friction, drag.

For conservative forces, we can define a **potential energy** $U$ such that $W = -\Delta U$.

## Common Potential Energies

**Gravity (near Earth):** $U_g = mgh$.

**Gravity (general):** $U_g = -GMm/r$.

**Spring (Hooke):** $U_s = \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2$.

**Electrostatic:** $U_e = qV(\mathbf{r})$.

## Conservation of Mechanical Energy

If only conservative forces act:

$$E = KE + U = \text{const}$$

So $KE_1 + U_1 = KE_2 + U_2$ between any two states.

This single equation solves many problems instantly — without needing to integrate Newton's second law.

## Worked Example: Ball Drop

A 2 kg ball dropped from 10 m. Speed at the bottom?

Initial: $KE = 0$, $U = mgh = 196.2$ J.
Final: $U = 0$, $KE = ?$

$KE_f = 196.2$ J $= \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 \Rightarrow v = \sqrt{2gh} = 14$ m/s.

No need to compute time, acceleration, or force — energy conservation suffices.

## With Non-conservative Forces

When friction or drag does work:

$$E_f - E_i = W_{\text{nc}}$$

$W_{\text{nc}}$ is negative for friction (energy leaves system as heat).

Or use work-energy theorem directly: $\sum W = \Delta KE$.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "PE depends on absolute height."**

PE is defined up to an additive constant (we pick a reference). Only **differences** in PE have physical meaning.

**Misconception 2: "Energy conservation only with no friction."**

Energy is *always* conserved (first law of thermodynamics). Mechanical energy is conserved only without dissipative forces; friction converts KE to heat, but total energy (including thermal) is conserved.

**Misconception 3: "Conservative = preserves total energy."**

Conservative means path-independent work — equivalent to existence of PE function. Total energy is always conserved; mechanical energy conserved iff only conservative forces.

## Engineering Connection

- **Hydroelectric dams:** convert gravitational PE of water to electrical energy.
- **Roller coasters:** designed by energy conservation — first hill must be highest.
- **Springs and shock absorbers:** elastic PE storage.
- **Pumped hydro storage:** stores excess grid electricity as gravitational PE (pumped uphill at night).
- **Mechanical clocks:** spring/weight PE drives oscillation.
- **Bow and arrow:** elastic PE in bow → KE of arrow.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Spring stretched 0.2 m, $k = 500$ N/m. PE stored?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$U = \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2 = 0.5\cdot 500\cdot 0.04 = 10$ J.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Pendulum bob (1 kg) released from height 0.5 m. Speed at bottom?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$mgh = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 \Rightarrow v = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{2\cdot 9.81\cdot 0.5} = 3.13$ m/s.

Independent of mass — heavier object goes same speed.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Block 5 kg on rough incline (30°, $\mu_k = 0.2$), slides down 4 m. KE at bottom?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Vertical drop: $4\sin 30° = 2$ m. PE released: $5\cdot 9.81\cdot 2 = 98.1$ J.

Friction along path: $f_k = \mu_k mg\cos 30° = 0.2\cdot 49.05\cdot 0.866 \approx 8.49$ N.

Heat dissipated: $W_f = 8.49\cdot 4 \approx 34$ J.

KE at bottom = PE released − friction loss = $98.1 - 34 = 64$ J.

Speed: $v = \sqrt{2KE/m} = \sqrt{25.6} \approx 5.06$ m/s.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Pumped hydro.** Reservoir 200 m above turbine, $10^6$ m³ water. Energy stored?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Mass: $\rho V = 1000\cdot 10^6 = 10^9$ kg.

$U = mgh = 10^9\cdot 9.81\cdot 200 = 1.96\times 10^{12}$ J ≈ 545 MWh.

Real recovery: ~75% (turbine + transmission losses) → ~400 MWh.

Pumped hydro is grid-scale energy storage — globally largest battery. Recharged at night with surplus grid power.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why is energy conservation more powerful than force analysis for many problems?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Energy is a **state function** — depends only on initial and final positions.

Forces require integration over the path, with detailed knowledge of trajectory.

For problems where you only care about endpoints (speed at top vs bottom, max height reached), energy conservation skips all the intermediate dynamics.

This is why the "conservation laws" (energy, momentum, angular momentum) are often the first tools applied — they trivialize problems that would require painful integration.
</ExA>
