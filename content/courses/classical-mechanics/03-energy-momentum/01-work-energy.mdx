# Work and Kinetic Energy

## The Big Picture

Newton's laws relate forces and acceleration. The **work-energy theorem** transforms this into a statement about **energy**: the work done on an object equals its change in kinetic energy. This perspective is often more powerful than F=ma directly — energy is a *scalar*, doesn't care about path geometry, and reveals conservation principles that simplify hard problems. Energy methods are the default for analyzing collisions, springs, gravity, and friction.

## Definitions

**Work** by a constant force $\mathbf{F}$ over displacement $\mathbf{d}$:
$$W = \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{d} = Fd\cos\theta$$

**Variable force along a path:**
$$W = \int_C\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$$

**Kinetic energy:**
$$K = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2$$

## Work-Energy Theorem

$$W_{\text{net}} = \Delta K = K_f - K_i$$

The total work by all forces equals the change in kinetic energy. This is the energy-form of Newton's second law.

**Derivation sketch (1D):** $W = \int F\,dx = \int m(dv/dt)dx = \int mv\,dv = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2\big|_i^f$.

## Power

**Average power:** $\bar P = W/\Delta t$.

**Instantaneous power:** $P = \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{v}$.

For a car with constant power: $P = Fv$, so force decreases as speed increases. This is why acceleration drops at higher speeds.

## Conservative vs. Non-Conservative Forces

**Conservative** (gravity, springs): work depends only on endpoints, not path. Have associated potential energies.

**Non-conservative** (friction, drag): work depends on path. Energy is dissipated.

For a closed loop:
- Conservative: $\oint\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0$
- Non-conservative: $\oint\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} \neq 0$ (typically negative for friction)

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Work means physical exertion."**

Holding a heavy box without moving it: zero work (no displacement). Physically tiring, but mathematically zero work.

**Misconception 2: "Force and motion always do positive work."**

If force opposes motion (e.g., braking, friction), work is negative. Negative work removes kinetic energy.

**Misconception 3: "Kinetic energy can be negative."**

$K = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 \geq 0$ always (mass and $v^2$ both non-negative).

## Engineering Connection

- **Vehicle performance:** $P = Fv$ relates engine power to top speed and acceleration.
- **Brake design:** kinetic energy = heat in brakes during stop. $\tfrac{1}{2}mv^2$ must be dissipated.
- **Pump and turbine analysis:** work-energy applied to fluid → Bernoulli's equation derivation.
- **Spring-loaded mechanisms:** energy stored in springs released as kinetic energy.
- **Roller coasters:** kinetic ⇄ potential, with friction losses → design ramp heights and tube lengths.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
A 10 kg object is pushed with 50 N for 4 m on frictionless surface. Find work and final velocity (starting from rest).
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$W = Fd = 200$ J.

$200 = \tfrac{1}{2}(10)v^2 \Rightarrow v = \sqrt{40} \approx 6.32$ m/s.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
A 1500 kg car decelerates from 25 m/s to 10 m/s by friction over 100 m. Find the friction force.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\Delta K = \tfrac{1}{2}(1500)(100 - 625) = -393{,}750$ J.

$W = F\cdot 100\cos 180° = -100 F$.

$-100 F = -393{,}750 \Rightarrow F = 3938$ N $\approx 3.9$ kN.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Spring.** A spring has $k = 200$ N/m, compressed 0.1 m. Find the energy stored, and the speed of a 0.5 kg mass released from rest by the spring.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$U = \tfrac{1}{2}k x^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(200)(0.01) = 1$ J.

Conservation: $1 = \tfrac{1}{2}(0.5)v^2 \Rightarrow v = 2$ m/s.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Conceptual.** Why is the work-energy theorem often easier than $F = ma$ for variable forces?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$F = ma$ requires knowing $a(t)$ pointwise — often difficult for variable forces. Then integrate twice for $x(t)$.

Work-energy gives a single integral $W = \int F\,dx$, directly relating *energy* changes to displacement. For path-independent (conservative) forces, $W$ depends only on endpoints — even easier.

Energy methods *bypass* the time-dependence of motion when only "before vs. after" matters.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Multi-step.** A pendulum (mass $m$, length $L$) is released from rest at angle $\theta_0$. Find its speed at the bottom.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Height drop: $h = L(1 - \cos\theta_0)$.

Energy conservation: $mgh = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 \Rightarrow v = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{2gL(1 - \cos\theta_0)}$.

For small $\theta_0$: $1 - \cos\theta_0 \approx \theta_0^2/2$, so $v \approx \theta_0\sqrt{gL}$.

This is far simpler than solving the pendulum ODE — just energy conservation.
</ExA>
