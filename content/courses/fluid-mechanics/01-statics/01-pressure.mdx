# Pressure and Hydrostatics

## The Big Picture

In a static fluid, pressure varies with depth due to gravity. This single principle — the **hydrostatic equation** — underlies the design of dams, tanks, submarines, and barometers. Understanding it deeply means recognizing that pressure is *isotropic* (same in all directions at a point), that it depends only on depth (not lateral position), and that the resulting forces on surfaces can be computed by integrating over area.

## The Hydrostatic Equation

For a fluid at rest in a gravitational field:

$$\frac{dp}{dz} = -\rho g$$

(Sign: $z$ increases upward, pressure decreases.)

For incompressible fluid (constant $\rho$):

$$p(z) = p_0 - \rho g z$$

Equivalently, **at depth $h$ below the surface**:
$$p = p_{\text{atm}} + \rho g h$$

## Pascal's Principle

Pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted **undiminished** to every part of the fluid.

Mathematical: at any depth, an external pressure increase adds equally to the pressure everywhere at that depth.

This is the basis of **hydraulic systems** — small force on a small piston, large force on a large piston.

## Force on Submerged Surfaces

For a flat surface submerged in fluid:

$$F = p_c\,A$$

where $p_c$ is the pressure at the **centroid** of the surface, $A$ is the area.

The **center of pressure** (where the resultant force acts) is *below* the centroid for a vertical surface.

## Buoyancy (Archimedes' Principle)

A submerged body experiences an upward force equal to the weight of fluid displaced:

$$F_B = \rho_f g V_{\text{disp}}$$

This is a direct consequence of pressure variation with depth — pressure on the bottom of an object exceeds pressure on the top.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Pressure depends on volume of fluid above."**

Pressure depends only on **depth**, not on volume or shape of container. A thin tube and a wide pool of the same height give the same bottom pressure.

**Misconception 2: "Pressure is a vector."**

Pressure is a *scalar* — same in all directions at a point. The *force* it produces is a vector, perpendicular to the surface.

**Misconception 3: "Buoyancy depends on the body's mass."**

It depends on the **fluid displaced**, not the body's weight. A heavy steel rock and a light foam ball of the same volume submerged at the same depth experience the same buoyant force.

## Engineering Connection

- **Dam design:** total horizontal force from water = $\frac{1}{2}\rho g h^2 \cdot \text{width}$, plus moment about base.
- **Submarine ballast:** controlling depth via flooding/blowing tanks adjusts buoyancy.
- **Manometers:** measuring pressure differences via height of liquid columns.
- **Hydraulic jacks:** force amplification through area ratios.
- **Tank wall thickness:** sized to resist hydrostatic pressure at maximum depth.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Pressure at 10 m depth in fresh water? ($\rho = 1000$ kg/m³)
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$p = \rho g h = 1000\cdot 9.81\cdot 10 = 98.1$ kPa (gauge).

Absolute: $p_{\text{atm}} + 98.1 \approx 199$ kPa.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** A submarine at 200 m depth in seawater ($\rho = 1025$). Pressure on hull?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$p = 1025\cdot 9.81\cdot 200 = 2.01$ MPa $\approx 20$ atm.

Plus 1 atm atmospheric → 21 atm total. (Submarines withstand ~30+ atm at deeper depths.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3. Engineering: Dam.** A dam holds water up to height 20 m, width 100 m. Find total force on the dam.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Force per unit width: $\tfrac{1}{2}\rho g h^2 = 0.5\cdot 1000\cdot 9.81\cdot 400 = 1.96$ MN/m.

Total: $100\cdot 1.96 = 196$ MN. (Massive — dams must be enormous.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Conceptual.** A boat carries a stone. Stone is dropped overboard. Does water level rise, fall, or stay the same?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**Falls.** When in the boat: stone displaces water of weight = stone's weight. When sunk: stone displaces water of *volume* = stone's volume.

Since stone is denser than water, weight > volume × $\rho_w g$, so volume displaced decreases when sunk → water level falls.

Classical paradox — counter-intuitive!
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Hydraulic press.** Small piston area 5 cm², large piston 200 cm². Force 10 N on small. Find force on large.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Pressure: $p = 10/(5\times 10^{-4}) = 20$ kPa.

Force on large: $F = pA = 20\times 10^3\cdot 200\times 10^{-4} = 400$ N.

40× amplification — hydraulic jacks, brakes, presses use this principle.
</ExA>
