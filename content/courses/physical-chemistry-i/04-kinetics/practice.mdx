# Chapter 4 Practice — Chemical Kinetics

<ExQ>
**1.** Second-order, $k = 0.1$ M⁻¹s⁻¹, $[A]_0 = 1$ M. Time to drop to 0.1 M?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$1/[A] - 1/[A]_0 = kt \Rightarrow 10 - 1 = 0.1 t \Rightarrow t = 90$ s.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Rate constant at 25°C is $10^{-4}$, at 75°C is $10^{-2}$. $E_a$?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\ln(100) = (E_a/R)(1/298 - 1/348)$.

$1/298 - 1/348 = 4.82\times 10^{-4}$.

$E_a = 4.605\cdot 8.314/4.82\times 10^{-4} = 79.4$ kJ/mol.

Typical for reactions requiring bond breaking.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Find half-life of second-order reaction with $k = 1.5\times 10^{-3}$ M⁻¹s⁻¹, $[A]_0 = 0.5$ M.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$t_{1/2} = 1/(k[A]_0) = 1/(1.5\times 10^{-3}\cdot 0.5) = 1333$ s ≈ 22 min.

Higher initial concentration → faster decay initially. Second-order half-life **depends on $[A]_0$**.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Industrial reactor.** A reaction with $E_a = 75$ kJ/mol runs at 350 K. To halve the residence time (double rate), what T?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\ln 2 = (75000/8.314)(1/350 - 1/T_2)$.

$0.693/9024 = 7.68\times 10^{-5}$.

$1/T_2 = 1/350 - 7.68\times 10^{-5} = 2.78\times 10^{-3}$.

$T_2 \approx 360$ K. Just 10 K higher doubles rate.

But cost: higher T means more energy, possible side reactions, equipment limits. Engineering optimization balances these.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why are enzymes typically much more efficient than industrial catalysts?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Enzymes:
- **Specific binding pocket** orients substrate precisely.
- **Multiple stabilizing interactions** in transition state (H-bonds, electrostatics).
- **Evolved for billions of years** by natural selection.
- Often achieve **rate enhancements of $10^{10}-10^{17}$** vs uncatalyzed.

Industrial catalysts:
- Designed in years/decades, not eons.
- Robust to industrial conditions (high T, P, contamination).
- Achieve $10^6-10^9$ rate enhancement typically.

For specific reactions where conditions allow, **enzymes are now used industrially** (high-fructose corn syrup, detergents). For harsh conditions, mineral or metal catalysts remain dominant.

This is why biocatalysis is a major growth area in green chemistry.
</ExA>
