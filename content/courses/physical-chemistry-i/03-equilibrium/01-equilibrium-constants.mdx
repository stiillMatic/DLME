# Equilibrium Constants and Le Chatelier's Principle

## The Big Picture

Most chemical reactions don't go to completion — they reach **equilibrium**, where forward and reverse rates balance. The **equilibrium constant** $K$ quantifies the position of this balance — large $K$ means products dominate, small $K$ means reactants. **Le Chatelier's principle** predicts how the equilibrium shifts when you perturb the system. Together, these tools let you predict yield, design reactors, and tune chemical processes.

## Equilibrium Constant

For $aA + bB \rightleftharpoons cC + dD$:

$$K = \frac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b}$$

Concentrations or pressures, depending on phase. Pure solids and liquids omitted.

At equilibrium, $Q = K$. If $Q < K$: forward reaction dominates (driving toward equilibrium). $Q > K$: reverse.

## Relation to Gibbs Energy

$$\Delta G^\circ = -RT\ln K$$

So:
- $\Delta G^\circ < 0 \Leftrightarrow K > 1$: products favored
- $\Delta G^\circ > 0 \Leftrightarrow K < 1$: reactants favored

Magnitude of $K$: order-of-magnitude estimate of yield.

## Temperature Dependence: Van't Hoff

$$\ln\frac{K_2}{K_1} = -\frac{\Delta H^\circ}{R}\left(\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}\right)$$

- Exothermic ($\Delta H < 0$): $K$ decreases with temperature.
- Endothermic ($\Delta H > 0$): $K$ increases with temperature.

This is one form of Le Chatelier — heating an endothermic equilibrium shifts forward.

## Le Chatelier's Principle

"A system at equilibrium, when disturbed, shifts to relieve the disturbance."

| Disturbance | Shift |
|---|---|
| Add reactant | Forward |
| Add product | Reverse |
| Increase P (gas; more mol on one side) | Toward fewer mol |
| Increase T (endothermic) | Forward |
| Increase T (exothermic) | Reverse |
| Add catalyst | No shift (just faster equilibration) |
| Add inert gas at constant V | No shift |

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "At equilibrium, all concentrations are equal."**

At equilibrium, **rates** are equal — not concentrations. The ratio of concentrations equals $K$, which can be any value.

**Misconception 2: "Catalysts shift equilibrium."**

No! Catalysts accelerate forward and reverse equally — only the *rate* of approach changes, not the equilibrium position.

**Misconception 3: "Pressure changes shift all equilibria equally."**

Only equilibria with different total moles of gas are affected by pressure. Solid/liquid equilibria essentially independent of P.

## Engineering Connection

- **Ammonia synthesis (Haber):** N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃. High P favors forward (4 → 2 mol gas).
- **Steam reforming:** CH₄ + H₂O ⇌ CO + 3H₂. Endothermic — high T favors H₂ production.
- **Water-gas shift:** CO + H₂O ⇌ CO₂ + H₂. Slightly exothermic; T tuned for kinetics vs equilibrium.
- **Industrial methanol synthesis:** CO + 2H₂ ⇌ CH₃OH. High P, modest T.
- **Buffer design in biology and chemistry** uses acid-base equilibria.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** $K = 4$ for $A + B \rightleftharpoons C$. Initially [A]=[B]=1 M, [C]=0. Equilibrium concentrations?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Let $x$ = amount reacted. At equilibrium: [A] = 1-x, [B] = 1-x, [C] = x.

$K = x/(1-x)^2 = 4 \Rightarrow x = 4(1-x)^2 = 4 - 8x + 4x^2$.

$4x^2 - 9x + 4 = 0$. Quadratic: $x = (9 \pm \sqrt{81-64})/8 = (9 \pm 4.12)/8$.

Physical solution (x < 1): $x = (9-4.12)/8 = 0.61$.

[A] = [B] = 0.39 M, [C] = 0.61 M.

61% yield.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** $K = 10^{-5}$. Reaction goes forward or stays?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Very small $K$ — reactants strongly favored. Reaction barely proceeds.

Equivalent: $\Delta G^\circ = -RT\ln K = +28.6$ kJ/mol at 298 K. Strongly non-spontaneous.

To get product, need to remove product as it forms (Le Chatelier) or couple with another reaction with $\Delta G^\circ \ll 0$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Exothermic reaction at equilibrium. Heat the system. What happens?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Le Chatelier: equilibrium shifts to **absorb the added heat** = endothermic direction = reverse direction.

K decreases. Less product at higher T.

Mathematically: Van't Hoff with $\Delta H^\circ < 0$ gives $dK/dT < 0$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Methanol synthesis.** CO + 2H₂ ⇌ CH₃OH, $\Delta H = -91$ kJ/mol. Exothermic, but industrial process at ~250°C, 100 atm. Why these conditions?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**Pressure:** 3 mol gas → 1 mol gas. High P → forward shift (more methanol). 100 atm is economical and increases yield.

**Temperature:** Trade-off. Cooler → more thermodynamic favor (since exothermic). Hotter → faster (kinetics). 250°C is a compromise where catalyst is active and equilibrium still favors product.

Plus: heat removal is critical (exothermic + high P = explosion risk if uncooled). Reactor design includes heat exchangers.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why does adding an inert gas at constant V not shift gaseous equilibrium?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
At constant V, adding inert gas doesn't change partial pressures of reactants/products. Their concentrations unchanged. $Q$ unchanged → equilibrium unchanged.

At constant P (e.g., adding gas to an unconfined balloon system), V increases — partial pressures of all species decrease equally. $Q$ changes if forward/reverse have different $\Delta n_{\text{gas}}$.

This subtle distinction matters for industrial reactors: constant V (most common) vs constant P designs respond differently.
</ExA>
