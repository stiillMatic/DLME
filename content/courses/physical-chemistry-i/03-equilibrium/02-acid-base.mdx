# Acid-Base Equilibria and pH

## The Big Picture

Acid-base chemistry is the chemistry of proton (H‚Āļ) transfer. Every biological molecule, every drug, every food, every soil interacts via acid-base equilibria. The concentration of H‚Āļ ‚ÄĒ quantified as **pH** ‚ÄĒ controls protein function, drug solubility, soil fertility, ocean health. The mathematics of acid-base equilibria is essentially the equilibrium tools of this chapter, applied to one specific (and ubiquitous) class of reactions.

## Definitions

**Br√łnsted-Lowry:**
- Acid: donates H‚Āļ
- Base: accepts H‚Āļ

A **conjugate acid-base pair** differs by one H‚Āļ.

Strong acids ($K_a > 1$, fully dissociate): HCl, HNO‚āÉ, H‚āāSO‚āĄ, HClO‚āĄ.
Weak acids: acetic, carbonic, organic acids, etc.

## $K_a$ and pH

For $HA \rightleftharpoons H^+ + A^-$:

$$K_a = \frac{[H^+][A^-]}{[HA]}, \quad pK_a = -\log K_a$$

$$pH = -\log[H^+]$$

Water self-ionizes: $K_w = [H^+][OH^-] = 10^{-14}$ at 25¬įC. So $pH + pOH = 14$.

| pH | Acidity |
|---|---|
| 0-3 | Strongly acidic |
| 4-6 | Weakly acidic |
| 7 | Neutral |
| 8-10 | Weakly basic |
| 11-14 | Strongly basic |

Each pH unit = 10√ó change in $[H^+]$.

## Henderson-Hasselbalch

For a buffer solution (weak acid + conjugate base):

$$pH = pK_a + \log\frac{[A^-]}{[HA]}$$

When $[A^-] = [HA]$: $pH = pK_a$. Buffer is most effective near this pH.

## Buffers

A buffer resists pH change when small amounts of acid/base are added. Composition: weak acid + its conjugate base (or weak base + conjugate acid).

Common: acetate buffer ($pK_a$ 4.76), phosphate ($pK_a$ 7.2), Tris ($pK_a$ 8.1).

**Buffer capacity** = how much acid/base it can absorb before pH shifts significantly. Highest when $[A^-] \approx [HA]$ and concentrations are large.

## Titration

Add base to acid (or vice versa). At equivalence point: moles base = moles acid (originally). For strong acid + strong base, equivalence at pH 7. For weak acid + strong base, equivalence at pH > 7 (basic conjugate).

**Half-equivalence:** half of acid is converted to conjugate base ‚ÄĒ pH = pKa.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Strong vs concentrated."**

Strength is about $K_a$ (extent of dissociation). Concentration is amount per volume. 1 M acetic acid is concentrated but weak; $10^{-5}$ M HCl is dilute but strong.

**Misconception 2: "pH 7 is always neutral."**

True at 25¬įC. At higher T, $K_w$ increases (water self-ionization is endothermic), so neutral pH < 7. At body temperature (37¬įC), neutral pH ‚Čą 6.8.

**Misconception 3: "Buffers prevent any pH change."**

Buffers minimize but don't eliminate pH change. Adding enough acid/base exceeds capacity ‚Üí buffer fails.

## Engineering Connection

- **Blood buffering:** carbonate-bicarbonate system maintains blood pH at 7.4 ¬Ī 0.05 ‚ÄĒ critical for life.
- **Drug formulation:** pH controls solubility, stability, absorption (gastric vs intestinal pH).
- **Wastewater treatment:** pH adjustment for precipitation of metals, microbial activity.
- **Soil chemistry:** pH determines nutrient availability (Al toxicity at low pH, P availability at neutral).
- **Industrial processes:** chemical synthesis often requires specific pH.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** What's the pH of 0.01 M HCl?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Strong acid, fully dissociated. $[H^+] = 0.01$ M. pH = 2.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Acetic acid $K_a = 1.8\times 10^{-5}$. pH of 0.1 M solution?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Let $x = [H^+]$. $K_a = x^2/(0.1 - x) \approx x^2/0.1$ (for $x \ll 0.1$).

$x^2 = 1.8\times 10^{-6}$, $x = 1.34\times 10^{-3}$.

pH ‚Čą 2.87. Weak acid ‚ÄĒ only ~1.3% dissociated.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Acetate buffer: 0.1 M acetic acid + 0.1 M sodium acetate. pH?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$pH = pK_a + \log([A^-]/[HA]) = 4.74 + 0 = 4.74$.

Equal concentrations ‚Üí pH = pKa. Most effective buffering range.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Blood buffer.** Carbonic acid $H_2CO_3$ ($pK_a$ 6.35). At blood pH 7.4, ratio of bicarbonate to carbonic acid?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$7.4 = 6.35 + \log([HCO_3^-]/[H_2CO_3]) \Rightarrow \log(\text{ratio}) = 1.05 \Rightarrow \text{ratio} \approx 11.2$.

About 11:1 bicarbonate to carbonic acid in blood. The CO‚āā from metabolism converts to H‚āāCO‚āÉ, which is buffered. Lungs adjust CO‚āā to regulate.

Combined with kidney HCO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ regulation, this is the body's primary acid-base regulation.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why are buffers most effective at $pH \approx pK_a$?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Henderson-Hasselbalch: $pH = pK_a + \log([A^-]/[HA])$.

Near $pH = pK_a$, ratio $[A^-]/[HA] \approx 1$. Adding small amount of acid converts some $A^-$ to $HA$, changing the ratio only slightly ‚Üí small pH change.

Far from $pK_a$, ratio is very different from 1. Small changes in $[A^-]$ or $[HA]$ produce big log changes ‚Üí big pH change.

This is why buffer choice depends on target pH ‚ÄĒ pick acid with $pK_a$ closest to desired pH.
</ExA>
