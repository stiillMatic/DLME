# Gibbs Free Energy and Spontaneity

## The Big Picture

At constant T and P (the usual chemistry conditions), the criterion for spontaneity is $\Delta G < 0$, where **Gibbs free energy** $G = H - TS$. This single quantity packages the competition between enthalpy (energy minimization) and entropy (disorder maximization) into one universal test. Whenever you see "Will this reaction happen?" — Gibbs is the answer.

## Definition

$$G = H - TS$$

For a process at constant T:

$$\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$$

**Spontaneity:**
- $\Delta G < 0$: spontaneous
- $\Delta G = 0$: equilibrium
- $\Delta G > 0$: non-spontaneous (reverse is spontaneous)

## Four Cases

| $\Delta H$ | $\Delta S$ | Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| < 0 | > 0 | Always spontaneous |
| > 0 | < 0 | Never spontaneous |
| < 0 | < 0 | Spontaneous at low T |
| > 0 | > 0 | Spontaneous at high T |

Crossover temperature: $T = \Delta H/\Delta S$. At this T, $\Delta G = 0$ (equilibrium).

## Standard Gibbs Energy

$\Delta G^\circ$ refers to reaction at standard states (1 bar gas, 1 M solute, pure liquid/solid). Tabulated for many reactions.

For reaction $aA + bB \to cC + dD$ at non-standard conditions:

$$\Delta G = \Delta G^\circ + RT\ln Q$$

where $Q = \dfrac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b}$ is the **reaction quotient**.

At equilibrium $\Delta G = 0$, so $\Delta G^\circ = -RT\ln K$.

## Useful Work

$\Delta G$ = maximum non-expansion (electrical, etc.) work obtainable from a process at constant T, P.

- Combustion: only $\Delta H$ is realized as heat (assuming no work device).
- Fuel cell: up to $\Delta G$ realized as electricity.

So fuel cells are more efficient than combustion engines (which are Carnot-limited).

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "$\Delta G < 0$ means fast reaction."**

Only means *possible*. Kinetic barriers can make $\Delta G < 0$ reactions slow (diamond → graphite, glucose oxidation in body without enzyme).

**Misconception 2: "Standard states are 25°C."**

Standard states refer to **composition/state** (1 bar, 1 M, etc.), not temperature. Tables often given at 25°C but $\Delta G^\circ$ depends on T.

**Misconception 3: "$\Delta G$ is the energy of the reaction."**

$\Delta G$ is the *maximum useful work* — usually less than $\Delta H$ (some energy spent on entropy contribution).

## Engineering Connection

- **Fuel cells:** electrical work = $-\Delta G$.
- **Batteries:** voltage $E^\circ = -\Delta G^\circ/(nF)$.
- **Catalysis:** can't change $\Delta G$, only accelerate approach.
- **Process design:** $\Delta G$ analysis tells you which direction to push equilibrium.
- **Drug design:** binding free energies $\Delta G_{\text{bind}}$ determine drug efficacy.
- **Phase diagrams:** equilibrium lines are where $\Delta G = 0$.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** $\Delta H = -100$ kJ, $\Delta S = -200$ J/K. Spontaneous at 300 K? At 600 K?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
At 300 K: $\Delta G = -100 - 300(-0.200) = -100 + 60 = -40$ kJ. **Spontaneous.**

At 600 K: $\Delta G = -100 + 120 = +20$ kJ. **Non-spontaneous.**

Crossover at $T = 100/0.2 = 500$ K. Below: spontaneous; above: not.

Typical of exothermic reactions with $\Delta S < 0$ (like ammonia synthesis).
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** $\Delta G^\circ = -10$ kJ. $K$ at 25°C?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\ln K = -\Delta G^\circ/RT = 10000/(8.314\cdot 298) = 4.04$.

$K = e^{4.04} \approx 56.7$.

Modest $\Delta G^\circ$ gives $K \sim 10^{1-2}$. Strong drive ($\Delta G^\circ < -30$ kJ) gives $K > 10^5$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3. Engineering: Fuel cell.** Hydrogen-oxygen fuel cell: $\Delta G^\circ = -237$ kJ/mol at 25°C. Cell voltage?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$E^\circ = -\Delta G^\circ/(nF) = 237000/(2\cdot 96485) = 1.229$ V.

Theoretical maximum cell voltage. Real cells deliver ~0.7-0.9 V at useful current due to overpotentials.

Stacks of cells (300+ cells × 0.7 V) power hydrogen vehicles.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Conceptual.** Why does dissolving salt in water happen even when $\Delta H_{\text{soln}} > 0$ (endothermic)?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Dissolution increases entropy substantially (ordered ionic crystal → disordered solvated ions).

If $T\Delta S > \Delta H$, then $\Delta G < 0$ → spontaneous despite endothermicity.

This is why ice-cold "instant cold packs" work: ammonium nitrate dissolves endothermically; entropy gain drives the process; solution cools down absorbing heat.

Entropy-driven processes are everywhere — micelle formation, protein denaturation, gas dissolution.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5.** Why is ammonia synthesis run at high pressure but moderate (not maximum) temperature?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$N_2 + 3H_2 \to 2NH_3$: $\Delta H < 0$ (exothermic), $\Delta S < 0$ (4 mol gas → 2 mol gas).

**High P** favors products (Le Chatelier: fewer moles gas).

**Low T** thermodynamically favored ($\Delta G$ more negative). But low T kinetically slow.

**Compromise: ~450°C** — fast enough for reasonable rates, low enough for OK equilibrium. Iron catalyst reduces $E_a$.

This is the **Haber process** — feeds half the world's protein via fertilizer. Optimal conditions are a thermodynamic-kinetic compromise.
</ExA>
