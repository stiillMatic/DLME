# Chapter 1 Practice — Laws of Thermodynamics

<ExQ>
**1.** 200 J heat absorbed, system expands against 1 atm by 0.5 L. $\Delta U$?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$w = -P\Delta V = -1\cdot 0.5 = -0.5$ L·atm = -50.65 J.

$\Delta U = q + w = 200 - 50.65 = 149.4$ J.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** $\Delta H_{\text{vap}}$ for ethanol = 38.6 kJ/mol at 351 K. $\Delta S_{\text{vap}}$?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\Delta S = 38600/351 = 110$ J/(mol·K).

(Trouton's rule: most liquids have $\Delta S_{\text{vap}} \sim 88$ J/(mol·K). Ethanol slightly higher due to hydrogen bonding in liquid.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** $\Delta S^\circ$ for $N_2(g) + 3H_2(g) \to 2NH_3(g)$? (Standard tables: $S^\circ$ N₂=192, H₂=131, NH₃=193 J/(mol·K).)
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\Delta S^\circ = 2\cdot 193 - 192 - 3\cdot 131 = 386 - 192 - 393 = -199$ J/K.

Negative: 4 mol gas → 2 mol gas (less disorder).

Despite this, ammonia synthesis is spontaneous at low T because $\Delta H$ is sufficiently negative. At higher T, $T\Delta S$ overwhelms $\Delta H$ and reaction reverses.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Refrigerator COP.** Refrigerator takes heat from $T_C = 277$ K (4°C) and rejects at $T_H = 313$ K (40°C). Maximum COP?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\text{COP}_{\max} = T_C/(T_H - T_C) = 277/36 \approx 7.7$.

Means: for every 1 J of work input, can move 7.7 J of heat from cold side.

Real refrigerators: COP ~3-4 (compressor losses, heat exchanger inefficiencies). Carnot limit cannot be approached.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why does diamond → graphite (more stable form) happen so slowly at room temperature?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Diamond → graphite is thermodynamically favored ($\Delta G < 0$), but kinetically blocked by high activation energy.

Need to rearrange C-C bonds. At room T, virtually no atoms have enough thermal energy → reaction takes geological timescales.

This is the **kinetic vs thermodynamic** distinction: second law tells you what *can* happen, but not how fast. Diamonds are stable enough for human timescales — "Diamonds are forever," kinetically.

At high T (e.g., 1000 °C in absence of O₂), conversion to graphite is observable.
</ExA>
