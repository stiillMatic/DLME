# Entropy and the Second Law

## The Big Picture

The first law allows any energy-conserving process. The **second law** picks out which actually happen: only those increasing the **entropy** of the universe. Entropy quantifies "disorder" or "number of microstates"; for chemistry, it dictates which reactions go forward, which equilibria are favored, and how much useful work you can extract. Without entropy, there's no chemistry — only equilibrium and reversal.

## Entropy

Statistical definition (Boltzmann):

$$S = k_B\ln\Omega$$

where $\Omega$ is the number of microstates consistent with the macroscopic state, $k_B = 1.38\times 10^{-23}$ J/K.

Thermodynamic definition (Clausius):

$$dS = \delta q_{\text{rev}}/T$$

Both equivalent.

## The Second Law

$$\Delta S_{\text{universe}} \geq 0$$

For any spontaneous process. Equality only for reversible processes.

Subdivide: $\Delta S_{\text{univ}} = \Delta S_{\text{sys}} + \Delta S_{\text{surr}} \geq 0$.

The system's entropy can decrease — but only if surroundings' entropy increases more.

## Entropy Trends

| Process | $\Delta S_{\text{sys}}$ |
|---|---|
| Gas → liquid | Negative (more order) |
| Solid → gas (sublimation) | Positive (more disorder) |
| Compression of gas | Negative |
| Heating | Positive |
| Mixing | Positive |
| Reactions producing more gas molecules | Positive |

## Calculating Entropy Change

**Phase changes (reversible at $T$ of transition):**
$\Delta S = \Delta H/T$.

E.g., water → vapor at 373 K: $\Delta H_{\text{vap}} = 40.7$ kJ/mol → $\Delta S_{\text{vap}} = 109$ J/(mol·K).

**Heating at constant P:**
$\Delta S = nC_P\ln(T_2/T_1)$.

**Mixing of ideal gases:**
$\Delta S_{\text{mix}} = -nR\sum x_i\ln x_i > 0$.

## Third Law: Reference State

The entropy of a perfect crystal at $T = 0$ K is zero.

This sets an absolute scale. Tabulated "standard molar entropies" $S^\circ$ are absolute, unlike enthalpy (which is relative to elements at standard state).

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Entropy = disorder."**

Loose analogy. Real definition: number of accessible microstates. Some "ordered-looking" states have high entropy (e.g., uniform mixture); some "disordered-looking" have lower.

**Misconception 2: "Spontaneous = exothermic."**

False. Endothermic processes can be spontaneous if entropy gain compensates ($\Delta G < 0$). E.g., NH₄NO₃ dissolving in water absorbs heat but happens spontaneously (large entropy gain from mixing).

**Misconception 3: "Second law forbids local order."**

You can decrease entropy *locally* by exporting more entropy elsewhere. Life, refrigeration, computers all do this. The universe-level entropy still increases.

## Engineering Connection

- **Heat engines:** Carnot efficiency $\eta = 1 - T_C/T_H$ — entropy sets the ceiling for engine efficiency.
- **Refrigeration:** must input work to "pump heat uphill" — Coefficient of Performance derives from entropy.
- **Chemical reactors:** equilibrium depends on $\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$ — entropy matters.
- **Materials engineering:** disorder controls glass formation, alloying.
- **Information theory:** Shannon entropy parallels thermodynamic entropy — basis for compression, communication.
- **Cosmology and arrow of time:** the second law gives a preferred direction to time itself.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** $\Delta H_{\text{fus}}$ of ice = 6.01 kJ/mol at 273 K. $\Delta S$ of melting?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\Delta S = \Delta H/T = 6010/273 = 22.0$ J/(mol·K).

Positive: melting increases disorder.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Sign of $\Delta S_{\text{sys}}$ for $2H_2(g) + O_2(g) \to 2H_2O(l)$?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
3 mol gas → 0 mol gas (liquid). Big decrease in entropy.

$\Delta S_{\text{sys}} < 0$.

(Despite this, the reaction is spontaneous because $\Delta H \ll 0$ — exothermic enough to drive the surroundings' entropy up.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Why does $\Delta S_{\text{mix}} = -nR\sum x_i\ln x_i$ always positive (for ideal solutions)?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
For each mole fraction $0 < x_i < 1$: $\ln x_i < 0$, so $x_i\ln x_i < 0$, so $-x_i\ln x_i > 0$.

Sum of positive numbers > 0.

Mixing always increases entropy (more accessible configurations). Need work to *demix*.

This is why osmosis, diffusion, gas mixing all spontaneous.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Carnot limit.** A power plant operates between 600 K and 300 K. Maximum efficiency?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\eta_{\text{max}} = 1 - 300/600 = 0.5 = 50\%$.

Real plants achieve 30-40% (irreversibilities). Combined-cycle plants approach 60% by using waste heat in secondary cycles.

This is an entropy-based ceiling — no engine can exceed it. Even infinite engineering improvement can only approach (not exceed) Carnot.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why does life exist if it locally decreases entropy?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Living organisms maintain internal order (low entropy) by exporting more entropy to surroundings.

Metabolism: take in low-entropy food (concentrated chemical energy), excrete high-entropy waste (CO₂, H₂O, heat).

Net effect on universe: entropy increases.

So life doesn't violate the second law — it locally circumvents it by being open to exchange. **Steady-state far-from-equilibrium systems** are the modern thermodynamic framework for biology.
</ExA>
