# Energy, Heat, and the First Law

## The Big Picture

The **first law of thermodynamics** is the statement of energy conservation applied to chemical systems. Internal energy ($U$) changes only through heat ($q$) and work ($w$). For chemical processes (mostly at constant pressure), **enthalpy** ($H = U + PV$) is more useful — its changes directly equal the heat absorbed. The first law is the bookkeeping that lets us predict heats of reactions, calorimetry data, and energy balances in any chemical process.

## The First Law

$$\Delta U = q + w$$

- $\Delta U$ = change in internal energy (state function).
- $q$ = heat added to system.
- $w$ = work done on system.

**Sign convention** (IUPAC, modern): both $q$ and $w$ are positive when energy enters the system.

For an ideal gas at constant external pressure: $w = -P_{\text{ext}}\Delta V$.

## State vs Path Functions

**State functions** (depend only on initial/final states):
- $U, H, S, G, T, P, V$

**Path functions** (depend on path):
- $q, w$

You can compute $\Delta U$ for any path, but $q$ and $w$ separately depend on how the system gets from A to B.

## Enthalpy: Constant-Pressure Heat

For most chemical reactions, we work at constant pressure (open beaker, atmospheric).

$\Delta H = \Delta U + \Delta(PV) = \Delta U + P\Delta V$ (constant P).

Rearrange: $\Delta U = \Delta H - P\Delta V$.

And $w = -P\Delta V$.

So $q = \Delta U - w = \Delta H - P\Delta V + P\Delta V = \Delta H$.

**At constant pressure: $q_p = \Delta H$**. This is why enthalpy is THE quantity for chemical reactions.

## Heat Capacity

$$C = q/\Delta T$$

For constant volume: $C_V = (\partial U/\partial T)_V$.
For constant pressure: $C_P = (\partial H/\partial T)_P$.

For ideal gases: $C_P - C_V = nR$.

## Hess's Law

Enthalpy is a state function. The enthalpy change of a reaction is the sum of enthalpies of its steps — **independent of the path**.

$$\Delta H_{\text{total}} = \Delta H_1 + \Delta H_2 + \ldots$$

Hess's law allows computing reaction enthalpies that can't be measured directly, by summing measurable steps.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "$\Delta H = q$ always."**

Only at constant pressure. At constant volume, $\Delta U = q$. Different conditions, different relationships.

**Misconception 2: "Heat and energy are interchangeable terms."**

**Energy** is a state property (system has it). **Heat** is a transfer (between system and surroundings). Once heat enters, it's no longer "heat" — it's internal energy.

**Misconception 3: "$\Delta H < 0$ means spontaneous."**

Not generally. Spontaneity requires $\Delta G < 0$, which includes entropy. Some exothermic reactions are not spontaneous; some endothermic are (driven by entropy).

## Engineering Connection

- **Combustion engineering:** heats of combustion (Δ$H_{\text{comb}}$) determine fuel energy content.
- **Refrigeration cycles:** computing work and heat through phase changes.
- **Calorimetry:** measure $\Delta H$ for reaction → predict scale-up heat removal needs.
- **Bond dissociation energies:** $\Delta H$ values for breaking specific bonds drive synthesis design.
- **Material processing:** annealing, melting, vaporization all governed by energy/enthalpy considerations.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Gas expands at constant $P = 1$ atm from 5 L to 15 L. Work done on system?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$w = -P_{\text{ext}}\Delta V = -1\,\text{atm}\cdot 10\,\text{L} = -10\,\text{L·atm}$.

Convert: $-10\cdot 101.3 = -1013$ J.

Negative — gas did work *on* surroundings, system lost energy.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** 100 J heat added, gas does 30 J work on surroundings. $\Delta U$?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$q = +100$ J, $w = -30$ J.

$\Delta U = q + w = +70$ J.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Combustion: $C(s) + O_2 \to CO_2(g)$, $\Delta H = -394$ kJ/mol. Heat to burn 12 g (1 mol) carbon?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\Delta H = -394$ kJ/mol → exothermic, system releases 394 kJ to surroundings.

For 1 mol (12 g): **394 kJ released**.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Fuel cells.** Hydrogen oxidation $H_2 + \tfrac{1}{2}O_2 \to H_2O$, $\Delta H = -286$ kJ/mol. Maximum heat from burning 1 kg H₂?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
1 kg H₂ = 500 mol.

Heat: $500\cdot 286 = 143000$ kJ = 143 MJ.

Energy density: 143 MJ/kg — about 3× gasoline by mass. (But H₂ storage is bulky by volume.)

In a fuel cell, most of this energy becomes electricity ($\Delta G$); some is unavoidable heat (Carnot losses), but more efficiently used than direct combustion.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why is enthalpy useful for chemistry but internal energy is more fundamental?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$U$ is the truly conserved quantity (first law). But chemistry usually happens at constant pressure (open vessel, atmospheric).

At constant P, $\Delta H = q$ — directly measurable from calorimetry without correcting for $PV$ work.

$\Delta U$ requires knowing $\Delta(PV)$ for reactions involving gas changes. Inconvenient.

So enthalpy is a **convenient state function for constant-P processes**, while $U$ is the underlying conserved quantity for any process.

For reactions involving gases, $\Delta H - \Delta U = \Delta n_{\text{gas}}RT$. For solid/liquid-only reactions, $\Delta H \approx \Delta U$.
</ExA>
