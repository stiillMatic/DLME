# Chapter 3 Practice — Radiation

<ExQ>
**1.** A blackbody at 1500 K emits how much per m²?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$E_b = \sigma T^4 = 5.67\times 10^{-8}\cdot (1500)^4 = 287$ kW/m².
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Compute the dominant wavelength via Wien's law for 6000 K (sun's surface).
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\lambda_{\max} = 2898/T = 2898/6000 = 0.483$ μm. Visible light, peaking in green-blue.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** A surface ($\varepsilon = 0.7$) at 800 K in 300 K surroundings. Net radiation per m²?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\dot q = \varepsilon\sigma(T_s^4 - T_\infty^4) = 0.7\cdot 5.67\times 10^{-8}\cdot(800^4 - 300^4)$
$= 3.97\times 10^{-8}\cdot 4.10\times 10^{11} - \text{negligible}$
$= 16{,}260$ W/m² ≈ 16.3 kW/m².
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Conceptual.** Why does emissivity matter when designing radiative coolers?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Radiative heat transfer scales with $\varepsilon$. High emissivity (≈1) maximizes radiation. Low (mirror-like) minimizes it.

For radiator: want high $\varepsilon$ in IR (where surface emits) — black coatings.

For thermal insulation in vacuum (cryogenic): want low $\varepsilon$ — mirrors and reflective foils.

Spacecraft radiators paint white (high IR emissivity, low solar absorption) to dump heat without absorbing too much sunlight.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Engineering.** Two infinite parallel plates: $T_1 = 500$ K, $T_2 = 300$ K, both $\varepsilon = 0.5$. Find net radiative flux.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
For two parallel plates:
$\dot q = \dfrac{\sigma(T_1^4 - T_2^4)}{1/\varepsilon_1 + 1/\varepsilon_2 - 1}$
$= \dfrac{5.67\times 10^{-8}\cdot(500^4 - 300^4)}{2 + 2 - 1}$
$= \dfrac{5.67\times 10^{-8}\cdot 5.44\times 10^{10}}{3} = 1029$ W/m².
</ExA>
