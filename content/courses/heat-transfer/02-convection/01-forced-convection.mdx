# Forced Convection Correlations

## The Big Picture

In **forced convection**, fluid flows past a heated surface due to pumps, fans, or external motion. Heat transfer rate is enormously enhanced compared to pure conduction because the fluid carries away thermal energy as it flows. Engineering uses **dimensionless correlations** (Nusselt-Reynolds-Prandtl) to predict heat transfer coefficients without solving the full Navier-Stokes equations every time.

## Newton's Law of Cooling

$$\dot Q = h A (T_s - T_\infty)$$

where $h$ is the **convective heat transfer coefficient** (W/m²·K). The challenge: predicting $h$.

Typical values:
- Free convection (air): 5–25
- Forced convection (air): 25–250
- Forced convection (water): 100–10,000
- Boiling: 10,000–100,000 W/m²·K

## Dimensionless Numbers

**Reynolds number** (flow regime):
$$Re = \rho V L/\mu$$

**Prandtl number** (fluid property):
$$Pr = c_p\mu/k$$

**Nusselt number** (dimensionless heat transfer):
$$Nu = hL/k$$

The strategy: correlate $Nu$ as a function of $Re$ and $Pr$, then back out $h = Nu\cdot k/L$.

## Common Correlations

**Flow over flat plate (laminar, $Re < 5\times 10^5$):**
$$Nu_x = 0.332\,Re_x^{1/2}\,Pr^{1/3}$$

**Flow in pipes (turbulent, Dittus-Boelter):**
$$Nu = 0.023\,Re^{0.8}\,Pr^{0.4}$$

(Heating; for cooling, exponent on $Pr$ is 0.3.)

**Cross-flow over a cylinder:**
$$Nu = 0.3 + \frac{0.62 Re^{1/2}Pr^{1/3}}{[1 + (0.4/Pr)^{2/3}]^{1/4}}\left[1 + \left(\frac{Re}{282{,}000}\right)^{5/8}\right]^{4/5}$$

(Churchill-Bernstein, valid wide range.)

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "$h$ is a property of the fluid."**

$h$ depends on geometry, flow regime, and fluid — not just the fluid alone. The same water gives very different $h$ in different configurations.

**Misconception 2: "Higher Re always means higher heat transfer."**

Generally true (turbulence enhances mixing), but at extreme $Re$ skin friction grows and pumping power may dominate. Optimal design balances both.

**Misconception 3: "$Nu = 1$ means no convection."**

$Nu = 1$ means heat transfer equals pure conduction across the fluid layer. Convection enhances above $Nu = 1$. Pure conduction limits typically $Nu \sim O(1)$.

## Engineering Connection

- **Heat exchangers:** sizing tubes, fins, and surfaces uses Nusselt correlations.
- **Electronic cooling:** CPU heatsinks designed via fan speed → $Re$ → $h$.
- **Boiler/condenser design:** boiling and condensation have specialized correlations.
- **Aircraft de-icing:** convective heating of wing surfaces by hot air ducts.
- **Process equipment:** chemical reactors, refineries, food processing — all involve forced convection.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
Air at 20°C ($\rho = 1.2$, $\mu = 1.8\times 10^{-5}$, $k = 0.026$, $Pr = 0.7$) flows at 5 m/s over a 1-m flat plate at 80°C. Find heat transfer coefficient at $x = 0.5$ m (laminar).
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$Re_x = 1.2\cdot 5\cdot 0.5/1.8\times 10^{-5} = 167{,}000$ (laminar, $< 5\times 10^5$).

$Nu_x = 0.332(167000)^{0.5}(0.7)^{1/3} = 0.332\cdot 408.7\cdot 0.888 = 120.5$.

$h_x = Nu_x\cdot k/x = 120.5\cdot 0.026/0.5 = 6.27$ W/m²·K.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
Water in a 5 cm pipe at 2 m/s, $\rho = 1000$, $\mu = 10^{-3}$, $k = 0.6$, $Pr = 7$. Find $h$ for heating.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$Re = 1000\cdot 2\cdot 0.05/10^{-3} = 100{,}000$ (turbulent).

$Nu = 0.023(100000)^{0.8}(7)^{0.4} = 0.023\cdot 10000\cdot 2.18 = 501$.

$h = Nu\cdot k/D = 501\cdot 0.6/0.05 = 6{,}012$ W/m²·K. (Very high — water is excellent for cooling.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Conceptual.** Why does forced convection have higher $h$ than free convection?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Forced convection drives fluid faster past the surface, replacing warmed fluid with cool fluid more quickly. This thins the thermal boundary layer and increases the temperature gradient at the wall (hence $h = -k(\partial T/\partial y)/(T_s - T_\infty)$).

Free convection relies only on buoyancy from heated fluid, which is much weaker than typical pump/fan-driven flows.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Engineering.** A CPU dissipates 100 W over 4 cm² area. Surface temp must stay below 70°C; ambient is 25°C. Required $h$?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\dot Q = hA\Delta T \Rightarrow h = 100/(4\times 10^{-4}\cdot 45) = 5560$ W/m²·K.

This is high — way beyond natural convection. Achieved with fins + fans + (sometimes) liquid cooling. Real CPUs use heat pipes, large finned heatsinks, and high-speed fans.
</ExA>
