# 3D Coordinates and Distance

## The Big Picture

Before doing calculus in 3D, we need a coordinate system to describe points, distances, and basic shapes. The 3D Cartesian system $(x, y, z)$ is the most common, but cylindrical $(r, \theta, z)$ and spherical $(\rho, \phi, \theta)$ coordinates are essential for problems with axial or origin-centered symmetry. Choosing the right coordinate system can transform a hard integral into a routine one.

## The 3D Cartesian System

A point in 3D: $(x, y, z)$. Origin at $(0, 0, 0)$. Three axes mutually perpendicular, oriented by **right-hand rule**.

**Distance formula:**
$$d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2 + (z_2 - z_1)^2}$$

(3D Pythagoras — extension of 2D distance.)

**Sphere of radius $R$ centered at $(a, b, c)$:**
$$(x-a)^2 + (y-b)^2 + (z-c)^2 = R^2$$

**Plane through $(x_0, y_0, z_0)$ with normal $(a, b, c)$:**
$$a(x-x_0) + b(y-y_0) + c(z-z_0) = 0$$

## Cylindrical Coordinates

$x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$, $z = z$.

Use when the problem has rotational symmetry around the $z$-axis (cylinders, paraboloids, cones).

## Spherical Coordinates

$x = \rho\sin\phi\cos\theta$, $y = \rho\sin\phi\sin\theta$, $z = \rho\cos\phi$.

- $\rho$: distance from origin (≥ 0)
- $\phi$: angle from $+z$-axis (in $[0, \pi]$) — **polar angle**
- $\theta$: azimuthal angle in $xy$-plane

Use when the problem has spherical symmetry (point sources, balls, planets).

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Cylindrical $r$ is the same as spherical $\rho$."**

Different! In cylindrical, $r$ is distance from the $z$-axis (perpendicular). In spherical, $\rho$ is distance from origin. They coincide only in the $xy$-plane ($\phi = \pi/2$).

**Misconception 2: "Always use Cartesian."**

Symmetry should drive coordinate choice. Spherical for spheres, cylindrical for cylinders, Cartesian for boxes. Wrong choice = unnecessary algebra.

**Misconception 3: "$(\rho, \phi, \theta)$ ordering is universal."**

Some texts use $(r, \theta, \phi)$ or $(\rho, \theta, \phi)$ with $\theta$ as polar. Always state your convention. Physics often uses different convention than math!

## Engineering Connection

- **Robotic kinematics:** end-effector position naturally cylindrical or spherical depending on arm geometry.
- **CFD around bodies:** flow past sphere → spherical coordinates; flow past cylinder → cylindrical.
- **Antenna patterns:** spherical coordinates for radiation directionality.
- **Astronomical observations:** RA/Dec is essentially spherical.
- **Polar diagrams in materials science:** stereographic projections from spherical coordinates.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Distance between $(1, 2, 3)$ and $(4, 6, 7)$?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\sqrt{9 + 16 + 16} = \sqrt{41} \approx 6.40$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Sphere through origin with radius 5 centered at $(0, 0, 5)$. Equation?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$x^2 + y^2 + (z-5)^2 = 25$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Convert $(2, 2, 3)$ Cartesian to cylindrical.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$r = \sqrt{4+4} = 2\sqrt 2$, $\theta = \pi/4$, $z = 3$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: GPS.** Two points on Earth (radius 6371 km) at $(\phi_1, \theta_1)$ and $(\phi_2, \theta_2)$ in spherical. Distance formula uses arc length, not Cartesian. Why?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
GPS distance is along the surface (Earth's surface is curved). Cartesian distance "tunnels" through Earth.

For two points: arc length = $R\cdot\arccos[\sin\phi_1\sin\phi_2\cos(\theta_1-\theta_2) + \cos\phi_1\cos\phi_2]$ (great circle distance).

Pure Cartesian would underestimate ~ for distant points (e.g., NY to Tokyo: tunnel ~10000 km vs surface ~10800 km).
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why is the polar angle $\phi$ measured from the $+z$-axis (in math), not from the equator (as in geography)?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Convention. In math/physics, $\phi$ from $+z$ means $\phi = 0$ at north pole, $\phi = \pi$ at south pole. Equator is $\phi = \pi/2$.

In geography, latitude is measured from equator: equator = 0, poles = ±90°.

Lat = $\pi/2 - \phi$ (in radians). Always state which convention.
</ExA>
