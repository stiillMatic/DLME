# Triple Integrals and Cylindrical/Spherical

## The Big Picture

Triple integrals extend the idea to 3D: integrate over solid regions to get volumes, masses, moments, total fields. Cylindrical and spherical coordinates are essential when the region or integrand has corresponding symmetry — they convert intractable Cartesian integrals into routine ones.

## Triple Integral

$$\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV$$

In Cartesian: $dV = dx\,dy\,dz$. Six orderings possible — pick the easiest.

**Volume:** $V = \iiint_E\,dV$.
**Mass:** $M = \iiint_E\rho\,dV$.

## Cylindrical Coordinates

$x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$, $z = z$.

$dV = r\,dr\,d\theta\,dz$.

Use for cylinders, paraboloids, cones — anything with axis symmetry.

## Spherical Coordinates

$x = \rho\sin\phi\cos\theta$, $y = \rho\sin\phi\sin\theta$, $z = \rho\cos\phi$.

$dV = \rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta$.

Use for spheres, cones from origin, "ice cream cones."

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "The Jacobian factor is just bookkeeping."**

It's the volume element of the new coordinates. Forgetting it gives totally wrong volumes/masses.

**Misconception 2: "Spherical is always best for 3D."**

Spherical is for **point** symmetry (origin-centered). Cylindrical is for **axial** symmetry. Different problems.

**Misconception 3: "Inverse Jacobian also works."**

Use $|J|$, not $1/|J|$. The Jacobian is volume scaling factor for *new variables in terms of old*.

## Engineering Connection

- **Mass of irregular bodies:** $M = \iiint\rho\,dV$ for 3D density distributions.
- **Center of mass:** $\bar{\mathbf{r}} = (1/M)\iiint\rho\mathbf{r}\,dV$.
- **Moment of inertia:** $I = \iiint\rho r^2\,dV$ — for rigid-body rotation.
- **Total field flux** through a volume.
- **Heat content of an irregular object.**

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Volume of cube $[0, 1]^3$ via triple integral.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\iiint_{[0,1]^3}\,dV = 1\cdot 1\cdot 1 = 1$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Volume of sphere of radius $R$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\pi\int_0^R\rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta = 2\pi\cdot 2\cdot R^3/3 = 4\pi R^3/3$ ✓.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Volume of cylinder radius 2, height 5.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2\int_0^5 r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi\cdot 2\cdot 5 = 20\pi \approx 62.83$ ✓.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Moment of inertia.** Solid cylinder, mass $M$, radius $R$, height $h$. Find $I_z$ about the axis.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Density: $\rho_0 = M/(\pi R^2 h)$.

$I_z = \iiint\rho_0(x^2+y^2)\,dV = \rho_0\int_0^h\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^R r^2\cdot r\,dr\,d\theta\,dz$

$= \rho_0\cdot h\cdot 2\pi\cdot R^4/4 = \rho_0\cdot\pi R^2 h\cdot R^2/2 = MR^2/2$.

Standard cylinder formula.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why does $\rho^2\sin\phi$ vanish at the poles in spherical coordinates?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
At $\phi = 0$ or $\pi$, all azimuthal angles $\theta$ collapse to a single point on the $z$-axis. No azimuthal extent → zero volume element.

This is why spherical coordinates are *singular* at the poles — a small numerical issue but a real geometric fact.

Engineers solving PDEs in spherical coords often use modified schemes near the poles to avoid this singularity.
</ExA>
