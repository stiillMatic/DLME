# Partial Derivatives and the Gradient

## The Big Picture

For functions of multiple variables, "the derivative" is replaced by **partial derivatives** (one per variable) and the **gradient** (a vector packaging all of them). The gradient is the multivariable analog of the derivative — it points "uphill" on the function and gives the rate of change in any direction. Mastering partials and gradients unlocks optimization, sensitivity analysis, and machine learning.

## Partial Derivative

For $f(x, y)$, the partial w.r.t. $x$ is:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h, y) - f(x, y)}{h}$$

Treat all other variables as constants — differentiate normally.

**Example:** $f(x, y) = x^2 y + \sin(xy)$.

$f_x = 2xy + y\cos(xy)$, $f_y = x^2 + x\cos(xy)$.

## Higher-Order Partials

$f_{xx} = \partial^2 f/\partial x^2$, $f_{xy} = \partial^2 f/\partial y\partial x$, etc.

**Clairaut's theorem:** for sufficiently smooth functions, $f_{xy} = f_{yx}$. Mixed partials commute.

## The Gradient

$$\nabla f = \left\langle\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}, \ldots, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\right\rangle$$

A *vector field* — at each point, a vector.

**Properties:**
1. Direction of steepest ascent of $f$.
2. Magnitude $\|\nabla f\|$ = rate of steepest ascent.
3. Perpendicular to level sets $f = c$.

## Tangent Plane

For surface $z = f(x, y)$ near $(a, b)$:

$$z = f(a, b) + f_x(a, b)(x-a) + f_y(a, b)(y-b)$$

This is the *linearization* of $f$.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Partials measure total change."**

Partial = change with one variable held fixed. **Total** change requires the chain rule (next lesson) summing all partial contributions.

**Misconception 2: "$\nabla f = 0$ means max or min."**

Means *critical point*. Could also be saddle. Need second-derivative test.

**Misconception 3: "The gradient is a function value."**

The gradient is a *vector*. Has direction and magnitude. Different from a scalar function.

## Engineering Connection

- **Heat flux:** $\mathbf{q} = -k\nabla T$ — Fourier's law in differential form.
- **Fluid potential flow:** $\mathbf{v} = \nabla\phi$ for irrotational flow.
- **Electric field:** $\mathbf{E} = -\nabla V$ from electric potential.
- **Optimization & ML:** gradient descent updates against $\nabla L$ to minimize loss.
- **Stress tensor:** components are partials of displacement field.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** $f(x, y) = x^2 + 3xy - y^3$. Find $f_x, f_y$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$f_x = 2x + 3y$, $f_y = 3x - 3y^2$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** $\nabla f$ for $f = e^{x}\cos y$ at $(0, 0)$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$f_x = e^x\cos y = 1$, $f_y = -e^x\sin y = 0$.

$\nabla f(0, 0) = (1, 0)$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Verify $f_{xy} = f_{yx}$ for $f = x^2y - y^3 + e^{xy}$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$f_x = 2xy + ye^{xy}$. $f_{xy} = 2x + e^{xy} + xy e^{xy}$.

$f_y = x^2 - 3y^2 + xe^{xy}$. $f_{yx} = 2x + e^{xy} + xy e^{xy}$.

Equal ✓.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Heat sink.** $T(x, y) = 100 e^{-(x^2+y^2)/4}$ °C. Find direction of fastest cooling at $(1, 0)$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\nabla T = -100 e^{-(x^2+y^2)/4}\cdot (x/2, y/2)$.

At $(1, 0)$: $-100 e^{-1/4}\cdot(0.5, 0) = -38.94(0.5, 0) = (-19.47, 0)$.

Steepest *increase* is in $-\hat x$ direction. Steepest *decrease* (fastest cooling) is $+\hat x$ direction (away from origin).

Heat flows from hot to cold, i.e., $-\nabla T$ direction = $+\hat x$ — outward from the hottest point.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why is the gradient perpendicular to level curves?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
On a level curve $f = c$, $f$ is constant. So directional derivative along the curve = 0.

Directional derivative = $\nabla f\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}_{\text{tangent}}$ = 0 means $\nabla f\perp$ tangent.

Geometrically: gradient points "uphill," level curves are "constant altitude" — naturally perpendicular.
</ExA>
