# Fundamental Theorems: Green, Stokes, Divergence

## The Big Picture

The three classical theorems of vector calculus all share the same structure: an integral over a boundary equals an integral of a derivative over the interior. This is the multivariable generalization of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus. They aren't just abstract math — they ARE the conservation laws of physics in their natural form.

## Green's Theorem (2D)

For positively oriented closed curve $C$ enclosing region $D$ in the plane:

$$\oint_C P\,dx + Q\,dy = \iint_D\left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)\,dA$$

The integrand on the right is the (scalar) 2D curl. So:

$$\oint_C\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_D(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\hat{\mathbf{k}}\,dA$$

Circulation around boundary = total curl inside.

## Stokes' Theorem (3D)

For oriented surface $S$ with boundary curve $\partial S$:

$$\oint_{\partial S}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_S(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot d\mathbf{S}$$

Circulation around boundary = flux of curl through surface.

3D generalization of Green's theorem.

## Divergence Theorem (Gauss)

For closed surface $\partial V$ enclosing volume $V$, outward normal:

$$\oiint_{\partial V}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_V\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}\,dV$$

Outward flux through surface = total divergence (net source) inside.

## Unifying View

All three theorems say: **integral of "boundary" = integral of "derivative on interior."**

In differential geometry, all are special cases of the **generalized Stokes' theorem**: $\int_{\partial M}\omega = \int_M d\omega$.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "These are independent results."**

They're all variations of the same underlying principle. Divergence theorem is "Gauss = Stokes for closed surfaces in 3D."

**Misconception 2: "Stokes only works for simple shapes."**

Works for any oriented surface with consistent boundary. Surface can be complex; only orientation matters.

**Misconception 3: "Divergence theorem requires outward normal."**

Yes — that's the convention. Inward normal flips sign, giving incorrect physics. Always state convention.

## Engineering Connection

- **Conservation of mass (continuity equation):** $\partial\rho/\partial t + \nabla\cdot(\rho\mathbf{v}) = 0$ — apply div theorem to control volume.
- **Maxwell's equations:** Gauss's law, Ampère's law are instances of these theorems.
- **Heat equation derivation:** apply div theorem to heat flux to get the PDE form.
- **Finite element method:** divergence theorem converts strong-form PDEs to weak-form integrals.
- **Reynolds transport theorem:** bridge between Lagrangian and Eulerian fluid descriptions.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Use Green: $\oint_C(y\,dx + x\,dy)$ around unit circle.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\partial Q/\partial x - \partial P/\partial y = 1 - 1 = 0$. Integral = $\iint_D 0\,dA = 0$.

(Alternatively: field is conservative with potential $xy$.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Divergence theorem: flux of $\mathbf{F} = (x, y, z)$ through unit sphere.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = 3$.

Flux $= 3\cdot V_{\text{sphere}} = 3\cdot 4\pi/3 = 4\pi$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Stokes: $\oint_C(y\,dx - x\,dy + z\,dz)$ for $C$ = unit circle in $xy$-plane CCW.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = (0, 0, -2)$ (curl).

By Stokes, integral = $\iint_S(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\hat{\mathbf{k}}\,dA = -2\pi$ over unit disk.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Continuity.** Apply div theorem to $\mathbf{F} = \rho\mathbf{v}$ to derive integral form of mass conservation.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\oiint\rho\mathbf{v}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint\nabla\cdot(\rho\mathbf{v})\,dV$

In differential form: $\partial\rho/\partial t + \nabla\cdot(\rho\mathbf{v}) = 0$.

Integrating over volume: rate of mass loss = net outward mass flow.

This is the integral form of mass conservation, derived from div theorem applied to mass flux.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why does Stokes' theorem give the same answer for any surface bounded by $C$?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
The line integral $\oint_C\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$ depends only on $C$. So $\iint_S\nabla\times\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S}$ must too.

Mathematically, this requires $\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}) = 0$ — which is always true.

Physically: surface flux of curl is invariant under deformation of surface (with fixed boundary). This invariance is essential for the consistency of Faraday's law and similar EM relations.
</ExA>
