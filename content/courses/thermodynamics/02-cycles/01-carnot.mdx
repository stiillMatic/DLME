# Carnot Cycle and Efficiency Limits

## The Big Picture

The Carnot cycle is the *theoretically most efficient* heat engine operating between two temperature reservoirs. No real engine can exceed Carnot efficiency. Understanding why — and what causes real engines to fall short — is essential for designing engines, refrigerators, and any cyclic thermal device. The Carnot result is a profound consequence of the second law.

## The Carnot Cycle

A reversible cycle composed of four processes:

1. **Isothermal expansion** at $T_H$ (heat $Q_H$ absorbed)
2. **Adiabatic expansion** ($T$ drops from $T_H$ to $T_L$)
3. **Isothermal compression** at $T_L$ (heat $Q_L$ rejected)
4. **Adiabatic compression** ($T$ rises from $T_L$ to $T_H$)

## Carnot Efficiency

$$\eta_{\text{Carnot}} = 1 - \frac{T_L}{T_H}$$

(Temperatures in Kelvin.)

**Examples:**
- $T_H = 600$ K, $T_L = 300$ K: $\eta = 50\%$
- $T_H = 1500$ K, $T_L = 300$ K: $\eta = 80\%$ (high-temperature gas turbine)

## Why No Real Engine Reaches Carnot

Real engines have:
- **Finite-time heat transfer** (not isothermal in reality)
- **Friction**
- **Non-reversibility** (mixing, turbulence)
- **Heat loss** through walls

Each of these *increases entropy* and reduces efficiency below Carnot.

## Refrigerators and Heat Pumps

Run a Carnot cycle in reverse:

**COP (cooling):** $\text{COP}_{\text{ref}} = T_L/(T_H - T_L)$
**COP (heating):** $\text{COP}_{\text{HP}} = T_H/(T_H - T_L)$

A heat pump delivers more heat than the work input — by "scooping up" ambient heat.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Carnot is achievable in practice."**

It's a *theoretical limit* — only achieved in the limit of infinitely slow, frictionless operation. Real engines run finite cycles with finite power, sacrificing some efficiency.

**Misconception 2: "Higher $T_H$ alone improves efficiency."**

Both higher $T_H$ AND lower $T_L$ improve $\eta$. In practice, $T_L$ is constrained by ambient (sea, river, atmosphere) — so improving real engines means raising $T_H$ via better materials.

**Misconception 3: "Efficiency = work output / fuel mass."**

That's *fuel-specific* efficiency. The thermodynamic efficiency is work / heat input. They're related but distinct.

## Engineering Connection

- **Power plants:** modern combined-cycle gas turbines reach ~60% efficiency vs. Carnot ~75% at their operating temperatures.
- **Internal combustion engines:** typically 25-35% efficient — far below Carnot due to non-ideal cycle and friction.
- **Refrigerators:** kitchen fridge has COP ~3–4 vs. Carnot COP ~10 between freezer and ambient.
- **Heat pumps:** for heating, COP > 1 means more heat delivered than electric power consumed — basis of efficient HVAC.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
A Carnot engine operates between 800 K and 300 K. Efficiency?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\eta = 1 - 300/800 = 0.625 = 62.5\%$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
A Carnot engine produces 100 kJ of work, with input from 500 K reservoir and rejecting to 300 K. Find $Q_H$ and $Q_L$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\eta = 1 - 300/500 = 0.4$. So $Q_H = W/\eta = 250$ kJ.

$Q_L = Q_H - W = 150$ kJ.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
A refrigerator removes 200 kJ from inside at 0°C, ambient 30°C. Find min work needed.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
COP$_{\text{Carnot}} = 273/(303 - 273) = 9.1$.

$W_{\text{min}} = Q_L/\text{COP} = 200/9.1 = 22.0$ kJ.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Conceptual.** Why can't entropy decrease in a cycle?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
For a complete cycle, the working fluid returns to its initial state, so $\Delta S_{\text{system}} = 0$ (state function).

But the *universe's* entropy changes: $\Delta S_{\text{univ}} = \Delta S_{\text{system}} + \Delta S_{\text{surr}} \geq 0$.

For a reversible (Carnot) cycle: $\Delta S_{\text{univ}} = 0$. For real cycles: $> 0$.

The "entropy generation" inside is what makes real cycles less efficient than Carnot.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Engineering.** Two heat engines: Engine A (Carnot) at $T_H = 1000$ K, $T_L = 300$ K. Engine B (Carnot) at $T_H = 600$ K, $T_L = 300$ K. Compare efficiencies.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\eta_A = 1 - 0.3 = 70\%$.
$\eta_B = 1 - 0.5 = 50\%$.

Higher $T_H$ → higher efficiency. This is why power plants push for ever-higher steam/gas temperatures (limited by metallurgy of turbine blades).
</ExA>
