# Electric Charge and Coulomb's Law

## The Big Picture

Electromagnetism is the second of the four fundamental forces, governing all electrical and magnetic phenomena. It begins with **electric charge** and **Coulomb's law** — the inverse-square force between charges, mathematically identical to gravity but enormously stronger. Understanding this allows us to construct the **electric field** $\mathbf{E}$, which becomes the central object of electrostatic analysis.

## Coulomb's Law

The force on charge $q_1$ due to $q_2$ separated by $\mathbf{r}_{12}$:

$$\mathbf{F}_{12} = \frac{q_1 q_2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r_{12}^2}\hat{\mathbf{r}}_{12}$$

where $\varepsilon_0 = 8.854\times 10^{-12}$ F/m is the **permittivity of free space**.

Like charges repel; opposite charges attract.

## Electric Field

A point charge $q$ creates field at position $\mathbf{r}$:

$$\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}\hat{\mathbf{r}}$$

Force on test charge: $\mathbf{F} = q_t\mathbf{E}$.

The field is a **vector field** existing throughout space, even where no test charge is.

## Superposition

Total field from multiple charges = vector sum of individual fields:

$$\mathbf{E}_{\text{total}} = \sum_i\frac{q_i}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r_i^2}\hat{\mathbf{r}}_i$$

For continuous charge distributions: integrate $d\mathbf{E} = (dq/4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2)\hat{\mathbf{r}}$ over the source.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Electric field requires test charges to exist."**

No — the field exists due to source charges, regardless of whether a test charge is there. The test charge merely *probes* the field.

**Misconception 2: "$1/r^2$ means field is weak far away."**

Yes for point charges. But fields from sheets of charge fall off as $1/r$ or even constant — depends on geometry. Always check the source distribution.

**Misconception 3: "Coulomb force and gravity are similar in strength."**

Coulomb force between two electrons exceeds gravity by ~$10^{42}$ times. Electrostatic forces dominate at atomic and molecular scales.

## Engineering Connection

- **Capacitors:** electric field stored between plates → energy storage.
- **Insulator design:** dielectric breakdown when field exceeds material's strength.
- **Particle accelerators:** electric fields accelerate charged particles to high energies.
- **Inkjet printers:** charged droplets deflected by electric fields onto paper.
- **Electrostatic precipitators:** charged dust particles collected on grounded plates.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Two charges, $+1$ μC and $+2$ μC, separated by 0.1 m. Force?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$F = (1\times 10^{-6})(2\times 10^{-6})/(4\pi\cdot 8.85\times 10^{-12}\cdot 0.01)$
$= 2\times 10^{-12}/(1.11\times 10^{-12}) = 1.8$ N (repulsive).
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Field at 2 m from a $+5$ μC point charge?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$E = 5\times 10^{-6}/(4\pi\cdot 8.85\times 10^{-12}\cdot 4) = 1.12\times 10^4$ V/m, radially outward.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3. Engineering: Capacitor.** Two parallel plates, area 0.01 m², separation 1 mm, charged to 100 V. Field between plates?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$E = V/d = 100/0.001 = 10^5$ V/m = 100 kV/m.

(Below typical air breakdown of 3 MV/m.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Conceptual.** Why is Coulomb's law analogous to Newton's gravity but charges can be negative?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Both inverse-square. But mass is always positive (no anti-gravity); charge has sign.

Consequence: gravity is always attractive (huge masses still pull). Charges can attract OR repel — and net charge of macroscopic objects is usually near zero (matter is mostly neutral).

This is why gravity dominates at large scales (planets) despite being so much weaker — because charges cancel.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5.** Three charges: $+q$ at $(0,0)$, $-q$ at $(d,0)$, $+q$ at $(0,d)$. Find force on charge at origin.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
From $-q$ at $(d,0)$: attractive, magnitude $kq^2/d^2$, in $+x$ direction.

From $+q$ at $(0,d)$: repulsive, magnitude $kq^2/d^2$, in $-y$ direction.

Total: $(kq^2/d^2, -kq^2/d^2)$, magnitude $\sqrt 2 kq^2/d^2$, at 45° below $+x$ axis.

(Where $k = 1/(4\pi\varepsilon_0)$.)
</ExA>
