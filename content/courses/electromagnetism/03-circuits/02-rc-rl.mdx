# RC and RL Circuits

## The Big Picture

Combining resistors with capacitors (RC) or inductors (RL) gives circuits with **time-dependent response**: exponential charging, discharging, settling. The characteristic time scale (RC for capacitive, L/R for inductive) determines filter cutoffs, oscillator periods, and signal shaping. These first-order circuits are the building blocks of every electronic system.

## RC Charging Circuit

A resistor $R$ in series with capacitor $C$, switched onto voltage source $V_0$:

$$V_C(t) = V_0(1 - e^{-t/\tau}), \quad \tau = RC$$

After **one time constant** $\tau$: $V_C \approx 63\%$ of final.
After 5τ: $V_C \approx 99.3\%$ — essentially settled.

Current: $i(t) = (V_0/R)e^{-t/\tau}$, starting at $V_0/R$ and decaying.

## RC Discharging

Capacitor charged to $V_0$, switched onto resistor:

$$V_C(t) = V_0 e^{-t/\tau}$$

Energy dissipated in resistor: equals initial capacitor energy $\tfrac{1}{2}CV_0^2$.

## RL Circuit Build-up

Inductor $L$ in series with resistor $R$, switched onto $V_0$:

$$I(t) = \frac{V_0}{R}(1 - e^{-t/\tau}), \quad \tau = L/R$$

Inductor "resists" current change initially; current builds exponentially toward steady value $V_0/R$.

## RL Decay

Open the switch (or short the source): inductor maintains current temporarily:

$$I(t) = I_0 e^{-t/\tau}$$

## Frequency Response (Filters)

For AC inputs at angular frequency $\omega$:

**RC low-pass:**
$$|V_{\text{out}}/V_{\text{in}}| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + (\omega RC)^2}}$$

Cutoff frequency: $f_c = 1/(2\pi RC)$. Below $f_c$: passes; above: attenuates.

**RC high-pass:** swap output across resistor — passes high, blocks low.

**Bandpass:** combine R, L, C — passes a range. Used in radio tuning.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Capacitor 'fills up' all at once."**

Voltage rises exponentially. Asymptotic — never quite reaches final value, just close enough.

**Misconception 2: "Inductor opposes all current."**

Inductor opposes **change** in current. Once current is steady, inductor acts as a simple wire (no voltage drop).

**Misconception 3: "RC time = RC product."**

Yes, but only with consistent units. With R in Ω and C in F: $\tau$ in seconds. With kΩ and μF: $\tau$ in ms.

## Engineering Connection

- **Camera flash:** capacitor charges slowly from battery, discharges quickly through xenon tube.
- **Pacemakers:** RC circuit times the heartbeat pulses.
- **Audio crossovers:** RC filters separate bass/treble for different speakers.
- **Digital logic timing:** RC delays determine clock skew, setup/hold times.
- **Power supplies:** RC filtering smooths rectified AC into stable DC.
- **Spark plugs:** inductor stores energy, discharges rapidly to create spark.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** $R = 1$ kΩ, $C = 10$ μF. Time constant?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\tau = RC = 10^3\cdot 10\times 10^{-6} = 0.01$ s = 10 ms.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Capacitor discharges from 5 V to 1 V through 100 Ω. $C = 100$ μF. Time?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$V(t) = V_0 e^{-t/\tau}$, $\tau = RC = 10^{-2}$ s.

$1 = 5e^{-t/0.01} \Rightarrow t = -0.01\ln(0.2) = 0.016$ s = 16 ms.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** RC low-pass: $R = 10$ kΩ, $C = 0.1$ μF. Cutoff frequency?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$f_c = 1/(2\pi RC) = 1/(2\pi\cdot 10^4\cdot 10^{-7}) \approx 159$ Hz.

Passes audio bass, attenuates higher frequencies.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Camera flash.** $C = 100$ μF charged to 300 V. Energy stored, discharge time through 0.5 Ω xenon tube?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Energy: $\tfrac{1}{2}CV^2 = 0.5\cdot 10^{-4}\cdot 9\times 10^4 = 4.5$ J.

$\tau = RC = 0.5\cdot 10^{-4} = 50$ μs. Discharge in ~250 μs (5τ).

Peak current: $V/R = 600$ A. Average power during flash: ~18 kW. Bright! Same energy as a $1.5$ W bulb running for 3 seconds, all packed into microseconds.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why does opening a switch on a coil produce a spark?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Inductor maintains current — when switch opens, current would have to drop to 0 abruptly: $dI/dt\to\infty$, so $V_L = -L\,dI/dt\to\infty$.

This high voltage breaks down the air across the switch contacts → arc/spark.

Same principle:
- Ignition coil in cars: ~30 kV from inductor discharge → spark plug.
- Stun gun: capacitor-driven inductor produces high voltage.
- Snubber circuits: protect switches by absorbing inductive transients.

This effect (inductive kickback) is the reason "always handle inductors carefully."
</ExA>
