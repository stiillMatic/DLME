# Chapter 3 Practice — Circuit Fundamentals

<ExQ>
**1.** Resistor 100 Ω, current 0.5 A. Voltage and power?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$V = IR = 50$ V. $P = VI = 25$ W.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Simplify: 6 Ω in series with parallel combination of 4 Ω and 12 Ω.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Parallel: $4\cdot 12/(4 + 12) = 3$ Ω.

Total: $6 + 3 = 9$ Ω.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3. RC time constant.** $R = 1$ kΩ, $C = 100$ μF. Time constant?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\tau = RC = 1000\cdot 10^{-4} = 0.1$ s = 100 ms.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Power transmission.** A 1 km wire of resistance 0.1 Ω/km carries 100 A or 1000 V at 10 A. Compare power losses.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$R_{\text{wire}} = 0.1$ Ω.

Case 1 (100 A): $P_{\text{loss}} = I^2 R = 1000$ W.
Case 2 (10 A): $P_{\text{loss}} = 10$ W — 100× less.

Same power transferred, but high voltage minimizes losses. This is the fundamental reason for high-voltage transmission lines.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why does an inductor "resist" current changes but allow steady current?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$V = L\,dI/dt$. Steady current → $dI/dt = 0$ → $V = 0$ across inductor (acts as a wire).

Sudden current change → large $dI/dt$ → large back-EMF → opposes the change.

This is why inductors smooth current in power supplies and oppose ripple.
</ExA>
