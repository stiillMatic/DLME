# Chapter 1 Practice — Foundations of Quantum Mechanics

<ExQ>
**1.** UV photon at $\lambda = 200$ nm. Energy in eV?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$E = hc/\lambda = 1240\text{ eV·nm}/200\text{ nm} = 6.2$ eV.

Enough to ionize many atoms — why UV is harmful and ozone layer matters.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Proton with kinetic energy 1 MeV. de Broglie wavelength?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$p = \sqrt{2mE} = \sqrt{2\cdot 1.67\times 10^{-27}\cdot 1.6\times 10^{-13}}$ kg·m/s $\approx 2.31\times 10^{-20}$.

$\lambda = h/p \approx 2.87\times 10^{-14}$ m = 28.7 fm.

Sub-nuclear scale. Used in particle physics scattering experiments.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** Bullet (5 g) at 300 m/s. de Broglie wavelength?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$p = 1.5$ kg·m/s. $\lambda = 4.4\times 10^{-34}$ m.

Hopelessly small. Macroscopic objects don't show wave behavior.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Electron microscope.** 100 keV electrons. de Broglie wavelength?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
At 100 keV, relativistic effects matter: $E_{\text{total}} = mc^2 + KE = 511 + 100 = 611$ keV.

$p c = \sqrt{E^2 - (mc^2)^2} = \sqrt{611^2 - 511^2} \approx 336$ keV.

$\lambda = hc/(pc) = 1240/(336000) \approx 3.7\times 10^{-3}$ nm = 3.7 pm.

About 100,000× smaller than visible light → atomic resolution. This is why electron microscopes can image individual atoms.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Can you violate the uncertainty principle with a clever measurement?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
No. The bound $\Delta x\Delta p \geq \hbar/2$ is a property of the quantum state, not of measurements.

You can measure either $x$ or $p$ very precisely — but the act of doing so leaves the conjugate variable highly uncertain (collapses state).

Modern "weak measurement" can extract partial information without full collapse, but the *uncertainty product* remains bounded. This is mathematically rigorous, not a soft principle.

Bell inequality experiments confirm: no local hidden-variable theory reproduces quantum predictions.
</ExA>
