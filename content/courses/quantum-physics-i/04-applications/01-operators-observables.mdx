# Operators, Eigenvalues, and Observables

## The Big Picture

In quantum mechanics, **observables** (measurable quantities like position, momentum, energy) are represented by **Hermitian operators**. Measurement of an observable returns one of its eigenvalues; the probability is given by the squared overlap between the state and the corresponding eigenstate. This postulate â€” together with state evolution via SchrĂ¶dinger â€” is the operational core of quantum theory.

## Key Operators

| Observable | Operator |
|---|---|
| Position | $\hat x = x\cdot$ |
| Momentum | $\hat p = -i\hbar\partial/\partial x$ |
| Energy | $\hat H = \hat p^2/(2m) + V(\hat x)$ |
| Angular momentum (z) | $\hat L_z = -i\hbar\partial/\partial\phi$ |
| Spin | $\hat S_i = (\hbar/2)\sigma_i$ (Pauli matrices) |

## Hermitian Operators

$\hat O$ is Hermitian if $\int\psi^* (\hat O\phi)\,dx = \int(\hat O\psi)^*\phi\,dx$ for all $\psi, \phi$.

**Consequences:**
- Eigenvalues are real (physical observables must be).
- Eigenstates with different eigenvalues are orthogonal.
- Eigenstates form a complete basis â€” any state expands in them.

## Measurement Postulate

If $\hat O$ has eigenvalues $\{o_n\}$ and eigenstates $\{|o_n\rangle\}$:

1. Measurement of $O$ returns some $o_n$.
2. Probability of $o_n$: $P(o_n) = |\langle o_n|\psi\rangle|^2$.
3. After measurement, $|\psi\rangle$ collapses to $|o_n\rangle$.

If the spectrum is continuous (position, momentum), replace sums with integrals.

## Commutators and Compatibility

Two observables are **compatible** (can be simultaneously measured precisely) iff their operators commute: $[\hat A, \hat B] = 0$.

Famous non-commuting pair: $[\hat x, \hat p] = i\hbar$ â†’ cannot simultaneously know both.

Compatible observables share a complete set of common eigenstates.

## Ehrenfest's Theorem

Expectation values evolve "classically":

$$\frac{d\langle\hat O\rangle}{dt} = \frac{1}{i\hbar}\langle[\hat O, \hat H]\rangle + \langle\partial\hat O/\partial t\rangle$$

For position and momentum:
$d\langle x\rangle/dt = \langle p\rangle/m$, $d\langle p\rangle/dt = -\langle\partial V/\partial x\rangle$.

These recover Newton's equations in the classical limit.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Measurement returns expectation value."**

Returns an eigenvalue â€” *some specific value*, not the average. Expectation value is the average *over many measurements* of identically prepared systems.

**Misconception 2: "State collapse is just changing your knowledge."**

Quantum collapse is unlike classical Bayesian updating â€” interference experiments demonstrate that wavefunctions are physical, and collapse is a real physical event (though the mechanism is interpretation-dependent).

**Misconception 3: "If $[\hat A, \hat B] = 0$, both have the same eigenstates."**

Commuting observables share a complete *set* of common eigenstates, not that all eigenstates of one are eigenstates of the other.

## Engineering Connection

- **Stern-Gerlach experiments:** measure spin components â€” Hermitian Pauli operators.
- **NMR/MRI:** measure nuclear spin operators in time-varying fields.
- **Polarization measurements:** photon polarization operators on a 2D Hilbert space.
- **Spectroscopy:** absorption lines = energy eigenvalue differences.
- **Tunneling current in STM:** related to local density of states (eigenstate sum at energy E).

<ExQ>
**1.** $\hat A = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0\\ 0 & -1\end{pmatrix}$. Eigenvalues and eigenvectors?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Already diagonal. Eigenvalues: $+1, -1$. Eigenvectors: $(1, 0)^T, (0, 1)^T$.

(This is $\hat\sigma_z$ in spin-1/2 â€” spin "up" and "down" along $z$.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Show $\hat x$ is Hermitian.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\int\psi^*(\hat x\phi)\,dx = \int\psi^*(x\phi)\,dx = \int(x\psi)^*\phi\,dx = \int(\hat x\psi)^*\phi\,dx$ âś“

(Real-valued multiplicative operators are automatically Hermitian.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** $[\hat L_x, \hat L_y]$ in terms of $\hat L_z$? (Without computing in detail.)
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Famous angular momentum commutation: $[\hat L_x, \hat L_y] = i\hbar\hat L_z$.

Cyclic permutations: $[\hat L_y, \hat L_z] = i\hbar\hat L_x$, $[\hat L_z, \hat L_x] = i\hbar\hat L_y$.

This algebra defines $SU(2)$ â€” the symmetry group of rotations in QM.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Quantum bit.** A qubit state is $|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle$. Measure $\hat\sigma_z$. Outcomes and probabilities?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\hat\sigma_z$ eigenvalues: $+1$ for $|0\rangle$, $-1$ for $|1\rangle$.

Probabilities: $P(+1) = |\alpha|^2$, $P(-1) = |\beta|^2$.

Normalization requires $|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1$.

After measurement, state collapses to $|0\rangle$ or $|1\rangle$. This is the basic readout in quantum computing.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why must observables correspond to Hermitian operators?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Physical measurement outcomes are real numbers. Eigenvalues of Hermitian operators are guaranteed real. Eigenvalues of non-Hermitian operators can be complex.

Also: Hermitian operators have complete orthonormal eigenstates, ensuring well-defined probabilities sum to 1.

These mathematical requirements correspond exactly to physical demands: real outcomes, total probability conservation. Hermitian operators are *the* natural mathematical structure for observables.
</ExA>
