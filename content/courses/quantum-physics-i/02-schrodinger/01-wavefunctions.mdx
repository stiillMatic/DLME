# Wavefunctions and Probability

## The Big Picture

A quantum particle's state is fully described by a **wavefunction** $\psi(x, t)$ â€” a complex-valued function. Born's rule says $|\psi(x, t)|^2$ is the probability density of finding the particle at position $x$. This single mathematical object replaces the classical "particle has position $x$ and momentum $p$" description, encoding *all* the system's properties (with attendant uncertainty).

## Probabilistic Interpretation

$|\psi(x)|^2\,dx$ = probability of finding the particle in $[x, x + dx]$.

Normalization:

$$\int|\psi(x)|^2\,dx = 1$$

The particle is *somewhere* (probability = 1).

## Expectation Values

$$\langle x\rangle = \int x|\psi|^2\,dx$$

$$\langle p\rangle = \int\psi^*\hat p\psi\,dx = -i\hbar\int\psi^*\frac{\partial\psi}{\partial x}\,dx$$

The expectation values give average measurement results.

## Operators

Position operator: $\hat x = x$ (multiplication).
Momentum operator: $\hat p = -i\hbar\partial/\partial x$.
Hamiltonian: $\hat H = \hat p^2/(2m) + V(x) = -(\hbar^2/2m)\partial^2/\partial x^2 + V(x)$.

Each observable corresponds to an operator. Measurement outcomes are eigenvalues of these operators.

## Superposition

The SchrĂ¶dinger equation is linear â†’ solutions superpose. If $\psi_1$ and $\psi_2$ are valid wavefunctions, so is $c_1\psi_1 + c_2\psi_2$.

This is the source of all quantum interference. Two paths can interfere because their wavefunctions add as complex numbers â€” phase matters.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "$\psi$ has direct physical meaning."**

$\psi$ itself is complex â€” not directly observable. Only $|\psi|^2$ and other functionals of $\psi$ have physical meaning.

**Misconception 2: "Particle is at $\langle x\rangle$."**

$\langle x\rangle$ is the average position from many measurements. Each individual measurement returns some specific value, with probability $|\psi(x)|^2$.

**Misconception 3: "After measurement, $\psi$ is gone."**

After measurement, $\psi$ "collapses" to an eigenstate of the measured observable. Future evolution proceeds from this new $\psi$.

## Engineering Connection

- **Quantum dots:** size-dependent wavefunctions â†’ controllable optical/electronic properties.
- **Tunneling diodes:** wavefunction "leakage" through barriers â†’ fast switching.
- **STM (scanning tunneling microscope):** images electron wavefunctions at surfaces.
- **Quantum computing:** qubit state = superposition $\alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle$ â€” complex amplitudes encoded in physical systems.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** Normalize $\psi(x) = A e^{-x^2/2}$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\int|\psi|^2\,dx = A^2\int e^{-x^2}\,dx = A^2\sqrt\pi$.

Normalize: $A^2 = 1/\sqrt\pi$, so $A = \pi^{-1/4}$.

(Gaussian wavefunction â€” relevant to ground state of harmonic oscillator.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** For $\psi(x) = (\alpha/\pi)^{1/4}e^{-\alpha x^2/2}$, find $\langle x\rangle$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
By symmetry: $|\psi|^2$ is even function, $x|\psi|^2$ is odd â†’ integral is 0.

$\langle x\rangle = 0$. The Gaussian is centered at origin.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3. Engineering: Quantum dot.** 1D quantum dot of width 5 nm. What's the ground-state expectation value of $x$ for a particle in this well (with $\psi \propto \sin(\pi x/L)$ centered)?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
With well $[0, L]$, $\psi = \sqrt{2/L}\sin(\pi x/L)$.

$\langle x\rangle = (2/L)\int_0^L x\sin^2(\pi x/L)\,dx$.

Use $\sin^2 u = (1-\cos 2u)/2$:

$\langle x\rangle = (2/L)\int_0^L(x/2)(1 - \cos(2\pi x/L))\,dx = (1/L)\cdot L^2/2 - (2/L)\int_0^L(x/2)\cos(2\pi x/L)\,dx$.

The second integral is 0 by integration by parts (odd in shifted coordinate).

So $\langle x\rangle = L/2 = 2.5$ nm â€” the center of the well, by symmetry.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Conceptual.** Why must $\psi$ be continuous and (typically) differentiable?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Probability density $|\psi|^2$ must be a well-defined finite function (continuous in most cases).

SchrĂ¶dinger equation involves $\partial^2\psi/\partial x^2$, requiring $\psi$ and $\partial\psi/\partial x$ to be continuous.

Exceptions: infinite potential barriers (psi = 0 abruptly) and delta-function potentials (psi continuous, derivative jumps). Both are idealizations of real situations.

Boundary conditions are essential for finding allowed wavefunctions â€” exactly as for classical waves.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5.** Show that $\hat p\hat x - \hat x\hat p = -i\hbar$ (the canonical commutation relation).
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Apply to test function $f(x)$:

$\hat p\hat x f = -i\hbar\partial_x(xf) = -i\hbar(f + xf') = -i\hbar f + x\hat p f$.

$\hat x\hat p f = x\hat p f$.

Difference: $\hat p\hat x f - \hat x\hat p f = -i\hbar f$.

So $[\hat p, \hat x] = \hat p\hat x - \hat x\hat p = -i\hbar$ (or $[\hat x, \hat p] = i\hbar$).

This non-commutativity is the **mathematical root** of the uncertainty principle. Operators that don't commute have a fundamental "trade-off" between their precise eigenstates.
</ExA>
