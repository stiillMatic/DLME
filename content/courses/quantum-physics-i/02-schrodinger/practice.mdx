# Chapter 2 Practice — The Schrödinger Equation

<ExQ>
**1.** Normalize $\psi(x) = A x e^{-x}$ on $[0, \infty)$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\int_0^\infty A^2 x^2 e^{-2x}\,dx$. Use $\int_0^\infty x^n e^{-\alpha x}\,dx = n!/\alpha^{n+1}$:

$= A^2\cdot 2!/2^3 = A^2/4$.

Normalize: $A = 2$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Verify $\psi(x, t) = e^{i(kx - \omega t)}$ is a stationary state for a free particle.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
TISE for free particle: $\hat H\phi = E\phi$ with $\phi = e^{ikx}$. $\hat H\phi = (\hbar^2 k^2/2m)\phi$ ✓ (E = $\hbar^2 k^2/2m$).

Time evolution: $\phi e^{-iEt/\hbar} = e^{i(kx - Et/\hbar)} = e^{i(kx - \omega t)}$ with $\omega = E/\hbar$ ✓.

Free particle plane wave is a stationary state.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3.** $\hat p$ operator on $\psi = e^{ikx}$. Eigenvalue?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\hat p\psi = -i\hbar\partial_x e^{ikx} = -i\hbar(ik)e^{ikx} = \hbar k\psi$.

Eigenvalue: $\hbar k$ — the momentum.

Plane waves are momentum eigenstates.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Engineering: Quantum well width.** GaAs/AlGaAs quantum well with infinite walls, want $E_1 = 100$ meV for electron ($m_e^* = 0.067 m_e$). Find well width.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$E_1 = \hbar^2\pi^2/(2m^* L^2)$.

$L^2 = \hbar^2\pi^2/(2m^* E_1)$.

$m^* = 0.067\cdot 9.11\times 10^{-31} = 6.10\times 10^{-32}$ kg.
$E_1 = 0.1\cdot 1.6\times 10^{-19} = 1.6\times 10^{-20}$ J.

$L^2 = (1.055\times 10^{-34})^2\pi^2/(2\cdot 6.10\times 10^{-32}\cdot 1.6\times 10^{-20})$
$\approx 1.097\times 10^{-67}\cdot 9.87/(1.95\times 10^{-51})\approx 5.55\times 10^{-16}$ m².

$L \approx 23.6$ nm. Realistic for MBE-grown quantum well lasers.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5. Conceptual.** Why are stationary states called "stationary" even though their phase oscillates?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
The *observable* (probability density $|\psi|^2$) is time-independent. All expectation values of physical observables are constant in stationary states.

The phase oscillation is "unobservable" by single measurement — only interference between different stationary states reveals it.

So "stationary" refers to physical observables, not to the wavefunction itself. The wavefunction's phase oscillates at rate $E/\hbar$, but this doesn't affect any single measurement.
</ExA>
