# Time-Dependent and Time-Independent Schrödinger Equations

## The Big Picture

The Schrödinger equation governs how wavefunctions evolve in time. It is the quantum analog of Newton's second law — replacing $F = ma$ for classical particles. The time-independent form arises by separation of variables and gives **stationary states** with definite energies. Solving the Schrödinger equation for various potentials reveals the structure of matter, atoms, molecules, and condensed phases.

## Time-Dependent Schrödinger Equation

$$i\hbar\frac{\partial\psi(x, t)}{\partial t} = \hat H\psi(x, t)$$

For a single particle: $\hat H = -\dfrac{\hbar^2}{2m}\dfrac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2} + V(x)$.

In words: rate of change of $\psi$ equals action of $\hat H$ on $\psi$ — quantum equation of motion.

## Separation of Variables

If $V(x)$ doesn't depend on time, look for solutions $\psi(x, t) = \phi(x)T(t)$.

Substituting:
$$\frac{i\hbar}{T}\frac{dT}{dt} = \frac{\hat H\phi}{\phi} = E$$

(Separation constant $E$ — has units of energy.)

This gives two equations:

**Time:** $T(t) = e^{-iEt/\hbar}$.

**Spatial (time-independent Schrödinger):**

$$\hat H\phi(x) = E\phi(x)$$

An **eigenvalue equation**. Solutions $\phi_n(x)$ with eigenvalues $E_n$ are **stationary states**.

## Stationary States

$\psi_n(x, t) = \phi_n(x)e^{-iE_n t/\hbar}$.

$|\psi_n|^2 = |\phi_n|^2$ — probability density is time-independent.

But the wavefunction itself oscillates in time (the phase $e^{-iEt/\hbar}$). This phase enables interference when states are superposed.

## General Solution

$\psi(x, t) = \sum_n c_n\phi_n(x)e^{-iE_n t/\hbar}$.

Any initial wavefunction decomposes into eigenstates; each evolves with its own phase. Different energies → different phase rates → time-evolution.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Stationary states don't move."**

Probability density is stationary, but the wavefunction (with phase) evolves. This evolution matters when stationary states superpose.

**Misconception 2: "$E$ is always real."**

For bound states with Hermitian $H$, yes. Open systems (decay, resonances) can have complex $E$ → exponential decay of probability.

**Misconception 3: "Schrödinger equation is derivable from classical mechanics."**

It's not — Schrödinger essentially **postulated** it (guided by de Broglie and Hamilton-Jacobi). Its justification is empirical: it makes correct predictions.

## Engineering Connection

- **Semiconductor band structure:** Schrödinger equation in periodic potential → energy bands → conductors/insulators.
- **Quantum cascade lasers:** designed energy levels using engineered quantum wells.
- **Tunnel junction devices:** transmission probabilities from Schrödinger solutions.
- **NMR spectroscopy:** measures transitions between Schrödinger eigenstates of nuclear spins.

## Practice Problems

<ExQ>
**1.** A free particle has $V = 0$. Plane-wave solution?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Try $\psi = e^{i(kx - \omega t)}$. $\hat H\psi = -(\hbar^2/2m)(-k^2)\psi = (\hbar^2 k^2/2m)\psi$.

$i\hbar(-i\omega)\psi = \hbar\omega\psi$.

So $\hbar\omega = \hbar^2 k^2/(2m) \Rightarrow E = p^2/(2m)$ ✓.

Plane waves are eigenstates of momentum and energy for free particles.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**2.** Spatial part of stationary state has $E_1 = 1$ eV, $E_2 = 4$ eV. Time evolution of $\psi_1 + \psi_2$?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\psi(t) = \phi_1 e^{-iE_1 t/\hbar} + \phi_2 e^{-iE_2 t/\hbar}$.

$|\psi|^2 = |\phi_1|^2 + |\phi_2|^2 + 2\text{Re}[\phi_1^*\phi_2 e^{-i(E_2-E_1)t/\hbar}]$.

Cross term oscillates with frequency $(E_2 - E_1)/\hbar = 3\,\text{eV}/\hbar$.

Probability density oscillates at this **transition frequency** — basis of optical absorption.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**3. Engineering: Quantum well laser.** Energy difference between $n = 1$ and $n = 2$ states is 0.8 eV. Wavelength of emitted photon?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\lambda = hc/\Delta E = 1240/0.8 = 1550$ nm.

Telecom wavelength — used in fiber optic communications. Quantum well lasers are engineered to emit at specific wavelengths via well-width tuning.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**4. Conceptual.** Why does the time-dependent equation contain $i$ and the time-independent one doesn't?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
The $i\hbar\partial_t$ in TDSE creates oscillation rather than decay — phases rotate.

After separation: spatial equation $H\phi = E\phi$ has no $i$ because the eigenvalue $E$ is real (for bound states of Hermitian $H$). All the imaginary unit is absorbed into $e^{-iEt/\hbar}$.

This is why stationary states have definite energy but oscillating phase: the same eigenvalue problem in time independent form.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**5.** Show that for a Hermitian Hamiltonian, energy eigenvalues are real.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$H\phi = E\phi$. Multiply on left by $\phi^*$ and integrate:

$\int\phi^* H\phi\,dx = E\int|\phi|^2\,dx$.

Hermitian property: $\int\phi^* H\phi\,dx = \int(H\phi)^*\phi\,dx = E^*\int|\phi|^2\,dx$.

Comparing: $E = E^*$ → real.

This is why bound-state energies are real numbers — observable quantities, eigenvalues of Hermitian operators. The mathematical structure guarantees physical sensibility.
</ExA>
