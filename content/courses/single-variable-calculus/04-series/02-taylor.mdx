# Taylor and Maclaurin Series

## The Big Picture

Most functions in engineering aren't polynomials. But **polynomials are easy to compute, differentiate, and integrate**, while transcendental functions (sine, exponential, logarithms) are hard. The Taylor series is the bridge: it represents virtually any smooth function as an infinite polynomial, with error that can be made as small as we want by including more terms. This idea — replacing a hard function by a polynomial near a point — is the foundation of numerical analysis, linearization, perturbation theory, and most engineering approximations. **If you understand Taylor series deeply, you understand half of computational science.**

## Intuition First — Building a Function from Its Derivatives

Imagine you know everything about a function at a single point: its value $f(a)$, its slope $f'(a)$, its curvature $f''(a)$, and so on. Can you reconstruct the function near $a$?

Astonishingly, yes — provided $f$ is "nice enough" (analytic). The Taylor series uses each derivative to add a layer of detail to the approximation:

- $f(a)$: zeroth-order — just the value at the point
- Adding $f'(a)(x-a)$: first-order — the tangent line, using slope info
- Adding $\tfrac{1}{2}f''(a)(x-a)^2$: second-order — the *parabola* matching slope and curvature
- And so on, with each higher derivative pinning down a new term

By including infinitely many terms, you reconstruct the original function exactly. By keeping only the first $N$ terms, you get an *approximation* whose error shrinks rapidly as $N$ grows (for analytic functions and $x$ close enough to $a$).

This is the philosophical heart of approximation: **near a point, smooth things look like polynomials**.

## From Linearization to the Full Series

You've already met the linear approximation: near $x = a$,

$$f(x) \approx f(a) + f'(a)(x - a)$$

This is the *tangent line*. It's accurate for small $|x - a|$.

Now we extend the idea. The next-order correction is quadratic:

$$f(x) \approx f(a) + f'(a)(x - a) + \frac{f''(a)}{2}(x - a)^2$$

The factor $1/2$ is the only "non-obvious" piece. Where does it come from?

**Derivation.** Suppose $f(x) = c_0 + c_1(x-a) + c_2(x-a)^2 + c_3(x-a)^3 + \cdots$ — assume $f$ has such a power series. Differentiate repeatedly and evaluate at $x = a$:

- $f(a) = c_0 \Rightarrow c_0 = f(a)$
- $f'(a) = c_1 \Rightarrow c_1 = f'(a)$
- $f''(a) = 2c_2 \Rightarrow c_2 = f''(a)/2$
- $f'''(a) = 6c_3 \Rightarrow c_3 = f'''(a)/6$
- General: $f^{(n)}(a) = n!\,c_n \Rightarrow c_n = f^{(n)}(a)/n!$

The factorials $n!$ come from successive differentiation of $(x-a)^n$. They give us:

$$\boxed{f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x - a)^n}$$

This is the **Taylor series of $f$ centered at $a$**. When $a = 0$, it's called the **Maclaurin series**.

## Standard Maclaurin Series

These should be memorized:

| Function | Maclaurin series | Domain of convergence |
|---|---|---|
| $e^x$ | $\sum_{n=0}^\infty \dfrac{x^n}{n!}$ | all $x$ |
| $\sin x$ | $\sum_{n=0}^\infty \dfrac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}$ | all $x$ |
| $\cos x$ | $\sum_{n=0}^\infty \dfrac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{(2n)!}$ | all $x$ |
| $1/(1-x)$ | $\sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n$ | $\lvert x\rvert < 1$ |
| $\ln(1+x)$ | $\sum_{n=1}^\infty \dfrac{(-1)^{n+1} x^n}{n}$ | $-1 < x \leq 1$ |
| $(1+x)^\alpha$ | $\sum_{n=0}^\infty \binom{\alpha}{n}x^n$ | $\lvert x\rvert < 1$ |

The first three are *entire* — they converge everywhere. The others have a finite **radius of convergence**.

## Why $e^x$ Is Special

Notice $e^x = 1 + x + \tfrac{x^2}{2} + \tfrac{x^3}{6} + \cdots$. Differentiating term by term gives the same series back. That's why $e^x$ is its own derivative. The series structure makes this property visually obvious.

## Linearization and Small-Angle Approximations

In engineering, we *truncate* Taylor series for tractable formulas. The most famous:

- **Small-angle approximations** (truncate after first term):
$$\sin\theta \approx \theta, \qquad \cos\theta \approx 1, \qquad \tan\theta \approx \theta$$

These are exact in the limit $\theta\to 0$ and accurate to ~1% for $|\theta| < 0.2$ rad. They're how we linearize pendulum equations, control systems, and lifting-line theory.

- **Second-order approximations** (more accurate):
$$\sin\theta \approx \theta - \theta^3/6, \qquad \cos\theta \approx 1 - \theta^2/2$$

- **Approximate $(1+x)^n$** for small $x$:
$$(1+x)^n \approx 1 + nx \qquad \text{(linear in $x$)}$$

This is how we estimate tiny percentage changes (e.g., temperature effect on resistance).

- **Logarithm and exponential:**
$$\ln(1 + x) \approx x \quad \text{for small } x$$
$$e^x \approx 1 + x \quad \text{for small } x$$

## Error Bounds (Taylor's Theorem)

Truncating at order $N$ leaves an **error** (remainder):

$$R_N(x) = \frac{f^{(N+1)}(\xi)}{(N+1)!}(x - a)^{N+1}$$

for some $\xi$ between $a$ and $x$. This is **Lagrange's form of the remainder**.

The error scales as $(x-a)^{N+1}$ — get closer to $a$ or use higher order, and error shrinks rapidly. This is why Taylor approximations are so powerful: for smooth functions and small $|x-a|$, accuracy is excellent with very few terms.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Compute $e^{0.1}$ to four decimal places using Maclaurin series.

$$e^{0.1} = 1 + 0.1 + \frac{0.01}{2} + \frac{0.001}{6} + \frac{0.0001}{24} + \cdots$$

$$\approx 1 + 0.1 + 0.005 + 0.000167 + 0.00000417 + \cdots \approx 1.10517$$

The exact value is $1.10517...$, so 4 decimals is accurate after just 5 terms.

**Example 2.** Approximate $\sin(0.2)$ to three decimal places.

$$\sin(0.2) = 0.2 - \frac{0.008}{6} + \frac{(0.2)^5}{120} - \cdots = 0.2 - 0.001333 + 0.00000267 - \cdots \approx 0.1987$$

Exact: $0.19867...$. The third-order truncation $\sin\theta \approx \theta - \theta^3/6$ gives 4-decimal accuracy.

**Example 3.** Find the Maclaurin series of $f(x) = 1/(1+x^2)$.

Use the geometric formula with $-x^2$ in place of $x$:

$$\frac{1}{1+x^2} = \frac{1}{1 - (-x^2)} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty(-x^2)^n = \sum_{n=0}^\infty(-1)^n x^{2n}$$

Convergent for $|x| < 1$. Integrating term by term gives the Taylor series of $\arctan x$.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Taylor series equals the function everywhere."**

Only inside the radius of convergence. Outside, the series diverges and gives nonsense. Worse, some functions (like $f(x) = e^{-1/x^2}$ at $x = 0$) have Taylor series that converge to *zero* but the function isn't zero — they aren't analytic.

**Misconception 2: "More terms always give better accuracy."**

Within the radius of convergence and for nice functions, yes. Outside it, more terms can make things *worse*. Always check convergence.

**Misconception 3: "Linear approximation is good enough."**

Depends on the application. For small parameters (small angles, weak fields), yes. For larger parameters, you need quadratic or higher-order terms — or a different expansion point closer to your $x$.

**Misconception 4: "If a function has all derivatives at $a$, its Taylor series converges to it."**

Almost always true in physics/engineering, but not formally. Counterexamples exist (functions whose series converge to something else). For *analytic* functions — those equal to their Taylor series — this works.

**Misconception 5: "Taylor series only work near $x = 0$."**

The center $a$ can be anywhere. Choose $a$ close to where you'll evaluate. For $\sin(\pi/3 + 0.01)$, expand around $\pi/3$ (where $\sin$ and $\cos$ are simple), not $0$.

## Engineering Connection

Taylor expansion is everywhere in engineering math:

- **Linearization for control systems:** nonlinear models are linearized around an operating point using a first-order Taylor expansion. Every transfer function in classical control is a *linear* approximation of nonlinear dynamics.
- **Finite differences:** $f'(x) \approx (f(x+h) - f(x))/h$ is a Taylor truncation. The error is $O(h)$ for forward difference, $O(h^2)$ for centered difference.
- **Numerical ODE integration:** Runge-Kutta methods are essentially Taylor series for the unknown solution truncated at various orders.
- **Perturbation theory in mechanics:** when a parameter $\varepsilon$ is small, expand the solution as $u = u_0 + \varepsilon u_1 + \varepsilon^2 u_2 + \cdots$ — a Taylor series in $\varepsilon$.
- **Aircraft stability:** equations of motion are expanded around steady flight; only first-order terms are retained for linear stability analysis.
- **Beam analysis:** the Euler-Bernoulli theory comes from a Taylor expansion of stress through the cross-section, retaining only the linear strain term.

In every case, the underlying idea is: **near a point, replace the hard function with its tangent polynomial, and analyze that instead.**

### Application

<ExQ>
Use small-angle approximation to simplify $\sin(0.05) - 0.05$, and estimate the error.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\sin\theta \approx \theta - \theta^3/6$. So $\sin(0.05) - 0.05 \approx -(0.05)^3/6 = -0.000125/6 \approx -2.08\times 10^{-5}$.

Error of the linear approximation $\sin\theta \approx \theta$: about $2.08\times 10^{-5}$, or $\sim 0.04\%$. Excellent for many engineering applications.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
Find the Maclaurin series for $f(x) = \ln(1 + 2x)$ up to the $x^4$ term.

Use $\ln(1+u) = u - u^2/2 + u^3/3 - u^4/4 + \cdots$ with $u = 2x$:

$$\ln(1+2x) = 2x - \frac{(2x)^2}{2} + \frac{(2x)^3}{3} - \frac{(2x)^4}{4} + \cdots = 2x - 2x^2 + \frac{8x^3}{3} - 4x^4 + \cdots$$

<ExQ>
A pendulum has period $T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}\bigl(1 + \tfrac{\theta_0^2}{16} + O(\theta_0^4)\bigr)$. Estimate the percent correction to small-angle period for $\theta_0 = 30° = \pi/6$ rad.
</ExQ>

$\theta_0 = \pi/6 \approx 0.524$ rad, $\theta_0^2 \approx 0.274$.

Correction factor: $1 + 0.274/16 = 1 + 0.0171$.

The period is about $1.71\%$ longer than the small-angle estimate — significant for accurate timekeeping. This is why grandfather-clock pendulums use small swings (typically $< 4°$).

### Challenge

<ExQ>
Use the Maclaurin series of $\sin x$ and $\cos x$ to derive Euler's formula $e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Maclaurin: $e^z = 1 + z + z^2/2! + z^3/3! + z^4/4! + \cdots$

Substitute $z = i\theta$:
$$e^{i\theta} = 1 + i\theta + \frac{(i\theta)^2}{2!} + \frac{(i\theta)^3}{3!} + \frac{(i\theta)^4}{4!} + \cdots$$
$$= 1 + i\theta - \frac{\theta^2}{2!} - i\frac{\theta^3}{3!} + \frac{\theta^4}{4!} + i\frac{\theta^5}{5!} - \cdots$$

Group real and imaginary parts:
$$= \left(1 - \frac{\theta^2}{2!} + \frac{\theta^4}{4!} - \cdots\right) + i\left(\theta - \frac{\theta^3}{3!} + \frac{\theta^5}{5!} - \cdots\right)$$
$$= \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$$

The connection between exponentials and trig functions emerges naturally from the Taylor series of $e^z$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
Use the Taylor series of $f(x) = \sqrt{1 + x}$ around $x = 0$ to estimate $\sqrt{1.04}$ to three decimal places.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$f(x) = (1+x)^{1/2}$, $f'(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}(1+x)^{-1/2}$, $f''(x) = -\tfrac{1}{4}(1+x)^{-3/2}$.

At $x = 0$: $f = 1$, $f' = 1/2$, $f'' = -1/4$.

$$\sqrt{1+x} \approx 1 + \frac{x}{2} - \frac{x^2}{8}$$

At $x = 0.04$: $1 + 0.02 - 0.0002 = 1.0198$.

Exact: $\sqrt{1.04} = 1.01980...$ — three decimals correct.

Linear approximation alone gives $1.020$, accurate to 3 decimals here. The quadratic term is the small correction.
</ExA>

### Conceptual

<ExQ>
**True or False.** A Taylor series always converges to the function it represents on the entire real line. Justify.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**False.** Two things can fail:

1. **Limited radius of convergence.** $1/(1-x) = \sum x^n$ only converges for $|x| < 1$, despite $1/(1-x)$ being defined for all $x \neq 1$.

2. **Non-analytic functions.** $f(x) = e^{-1/x^2}$ (with $f(0) = 0$) has all derivatives equal to 0 at $x = 0$. So its Maclaurin series is identically 0, but $f$ is *not* zero anywhere except at 0. The series converges (to 0), but **not to $f$**.

For most "everyday" engineering functions ($\sin$, $\cos$, $e^x$, polynomials, etc.), the Taylor series does converge to the function on its domain, but the general statement isn't universal.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Why the factorial?** Where does the $n!$ in the Taylor formula come from? Derive it from the relation $f^{(n)}(a) = n! c_n$ in your own words.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Suppose $f(x) = \sum c_n(x-a)^n$. Differentiating term $c_n(x-a)^n$ once gives $n c_n(x-a)^{n-1}$. Differentiating again: $n(n-1)c_n(x-a)^{n-2}$. After $n$ differentiations: $n(n-1)(n-2)\cdots 1\cdot c_n = n!\,c_n$.

When we evaluate at $x = a$, all higher-power terms vanish, leaving:
$$f^{(n)}(a) = n!\,c_n \implies c_n = \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}$$

The factorial is "what's needed to undo the repeated differentiation." Without it, coefficients would be wrong by a factor of $n!$ — the series wouldn't reproduce $f$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Use the right tool.** For each, indicate whether you'd use Taylor series at 0, Taylor series at another point, or some other method:
(a) $\sin(0.1)$
(b) $\sin(45°)$
(c) $e^{10}$
(d) $\ln(0.95)$
(e) $\cos(\pi/3 + 0.001)$
</ExQ>
<ExA>
(a) **Taylor at 0** — small input, Maclaurin works perfectly.

(b) **Direct or Taylor at $\pi/4$** — $\sin(45°) = \sqrt 2/2$ is a known value. If approximating, expand around $\pi/4$.

(c) **Not Taylor** — $e^{10}$ is a large value; Maclaurin series converges slowly. Use direct computation: $e^{10} = (e^5)^2$, etc.

(d) **Taylor at 0** with $\ln(1+x)$, $x = -0.05$ — small perturbation from 1.

(e) **Taylor at $\pi/3$** — expand around the nearby known point.

The general principle: **expand around the nearest "easy" point**, not always 0.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Error bound.** For the truncation $\sin x \approx x - x^3/6 + x^5/120$, what is the maximum error on $[-1, 1]$?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Lagrange remainder: $|R_5(x)| \leq \dfrac{|f^{(7)}(\xi)|}{7!}|x|^7$.

For $\sin x$, all derivatives are bounded by 1: $|f^{(7)}(\xi)| \leq 1$.

$|R_5(x)| \leq \dfrac{|x|^7}{7!} = \dfrac{|x|^7}{5040}$.

Maximum on $[-1, 1]$: at $x = \pm 1$, error $\leq 1/5040 \approx 1.98 \times 10^{-4}$.

So 5-term Taylor approximates $\sin x$ to within $0.0002$ on $[-1, 1]$. **Five terms = four decimal places of accuracy** — extremely good.
</ExA>

### Engineering Synthesis

<ExQ>
**Linearization for control.** A nonlinear system has $\dot y = -\sin y + u$. Find the linearized model around the operating point $y = 0$, $u = 0$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Around $y = 0$: $\sin y = y - y^3/6 + \cdots \approx y$ (first-order).

Linearized system:
$$\dot y \approx -y + u$$

This is a stable first-order linear system with time constant $\tau = 1$. Standard control-theory techniques apply.

**Validity:** the linearization is accurate for small $|y|$. Once $|y|$ approaches $\pi/2$ or so, nonlinear effects (the $-y^3/6$ correction) become important.

This is **the** standard procedure in control engineering: design with a linear model around an operating point, then validate that excursions stay small.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Beam deflection approximation.** For a slender beam, exact deflection involves $\sqrt{1 + (w'(x))^2}$ in the curvature formula. Show that under small-slope assumptions ($|w'| \ll 1$), the curvature simplifies to $w''(x)$ to leading order.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Exact curvature:
$$\kappa = \frac{w''}{(1 + (w')^2)^{3/2}}$$

For small $w'$: $(1 + (w')^2)^{3/2} = 1 + \tfrac{3}{2}(w')^2 + O((w')^4) \approx 1$ to leading order.

So $\kappa \approx w''$ for small slopes.

This is exactly the approximation that gives the linear Euler-Bernoulli equation $EI w'' = M$ — the foundation of every beam-bending calculation in undergraduate ME. Beyond small slopes, you need *large-deflection theory*.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Multi-step.** Use the Maclaurin expansions of $\sin x$ and $e^x$ to estimate $\dfrac{e^x - 1}{\sin x}$ to second order in $x$ near $x = 0$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$e^x - 1 = x + x^2/2 + x^3/6 + \cdots$

$\sin x = x - x^3/6 + \cdots$

$$\frac{e^x - 1}{\sin x} = \frac{x + x^2/2 + x^3/6 + \cdots}{x - x^3/6 + \cdots}$$

Factor $x$ from both: $\dfrac{1 + x/2 + x^2/6 + \cdots}{1 - x^2/6 + \cdots}$.

Expand $(1 + a)/(1 + b) \approx (1 + a)(1 - b) \approx 1 + a - b$ for small $a, b$:

$\approx 1 + x/2 + x^2/6 - (-x^2/6) = 1 + x/2 + x^2/3 + O(x^3)$.

So $\dfrac{e^x - 1}{\sin x} \approx 1 + \dfrac{x}{2} + \dfrac{x^2}{3}$ near 0.

Series manipulation is faster than L'Hôpital + algebra for many engineering limits.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Convergence radius via ratio test.** Find the radius of convergence of $\sum_{n=1}^\infty\dfrac{n!\,x^n}{(2n)!}$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\dfrac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} = \dfrac{(n+1)!\,x^{n+1}/(2n+2)!}{n!\,x^n/(2n)!} = x\cdot\dfrac{(n+1)}{(2n+1)(2n+2)} = x\cdot\dfrac{1}{2(2n+1)}$

For convergence: $|x|/2(2n+1) \to 0$ as $n\to\infty$ (regardless of $x$).

So the series converges for **all** real $x$. Radius of convergence: $\boxed{\infty}$.

This is the kind of analysis used to design power-series solutions for ODEs (Frobenius method, Bessel functions, etc.).
</ExA>
