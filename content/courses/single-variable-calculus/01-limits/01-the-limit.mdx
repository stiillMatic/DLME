# The Concept of a Limit

## The Big Picture

The limit is the foundational idea of all calculus. Every derivative and every integral is, at its heart, a limit. Without limits, we cannot make sense of *instantaneous* rates of change or *continuous* sums — and these two ideas are what allow us to describe motion, deformation, heat flow, and essentially every continuous physical phenomenon. If you understand limits deeply, the rest of calculus becomes natural.

## Intuition First — The Approach Game

Forget formulas for a moment. Imagine you're walking toward a wall. With each step, you cover half the remaining distance. You never actually touch the wall, but the distance to it shrinks toward zero. The "limit" of your distance is **zero**, even though you never reach it.

The limit captures this idea: **what value does a function get arbitrarily close to as we approach a point** — independent of whether we ever land exactly on that point.

Now imagine driving toward a town. As you get closer (3 km, 2 km, 1 km, 100 m, 10 m), the town's name gets clearer on the horizon. The "limit" of what you see is the town itself. You don't need to step inside to know it's there.

> **Key insight:** A limit is about *destination behavior*, not about what happens at the destination.

This is the only way mathematics can talk about division by zero, instantaneous velocity, or infinite sums — without ever actually performing those impossible operations. We approach them.

## From the Approach Game to the Definition

Suppose I claim:

$$\lim_{x \to 2} (3x + 1) = 7$$

How would you check this? You'd plug in values *near* 2 — but not 2 itself — and see if $3x+1$ is close to 7:

| $x$ | $3x+1$ |
|---|---|
| 1.9 | 6.7 |
| 1.99 | 6.97 |
| 1.999 | 6.997 |
| 2.001 | 7.003 |
| 2.01 | 7.03 |
| 2.1 | 7.3 |

The function values are clustering around 7. The closer $x$ gets to 2, the closer $3x+1$ gets to 7.

Now, the trickier case. What about $\dfrac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1}$ at $x = 1$?

Plug in $x = 1$ directly: $\dfrac{0}{0}$. **Undefined.** Yet the function still has clear behavior near $x = 1$:

| $x$ | $(x^2-1)/(x-1)$ |
|---|---|
| 0.9 | 1.9 |
| 0.99 | 1.99 |
| 1.001 | 2.001 |
| 1.01 | 2.01 |

The values approach 2. Why? Algebraically: $\dfrac{x^2-1}{x-1} = \dfrac{(x-1)(x+1)}{x-1} = x+1$ for $x \neq 1$.

Even though the function has a "hole" at $x=1$, near that hole the function behaves like $x+1$, which equals 2 at $x=1$. **The limit fills in the hole.**

## Formal Definition

$$\boxed{\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L}$$

means: for any tolerance $\varepsilon > 0$ on the output, we can find a tolerance $\delta > 0$ on the input such that whenever $0 < |x - a| < \delta$, we have $|f(x) - L| < \varepsilon$.

In words: **you tell me how close to $L$ you want $f(x)$ to be — I'll tell you how close to $a$ you need to keep $x$.**

The condition $0 < |x - a|$ excludes $x = a$ itself. The limit doesn't care about $f(a)$; it only cares about behavior *near* $a$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Compute $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 3}\frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3}$.

Direct substitution: $\dfrac{0}{0}$. Indeterminate. Factor:

$$\frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3} = \frac{(x-3)(x+3)}{x-3} = x + 3 \quad (x \neq 3)$$

Now substitute: $\lim_{x\to 3}(x+3) = 6$. The limit is **6**.

The factor of $(x-3)$ in numerator and denominator was a "removable singularity" — once cancelled, the function is smooth at $x=3$.

**Example 2.** Compute $\displaystyle\lim_{h \to 0}\frac{(2+h)^2 - 4}{h}$.

This is a classic derivative-style limit. Direct substitution: $0/0$. Expand:

$$\frac{(2+h)^2 - 4}{h} = \frac{4 + 4h + h^2 - 4}{h} = \frac{4h + h^2}{h} = 4 + h \quad (h \neq 0)$$

So $\lim_{h\to 0}(4 + h) = 4$. The limit is **4**.

Notice how the algebra "cancels the smallness" — the offending $h$ in the denominator is killed by an $h$ in the numerator. This pattern recurs in *every* derivative calculation.

**Example 3.** Compute $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin x}{x}$.

This famous limit *cannot* be solved by algebra. Numerical evidence:

| $x$ (rad) | $\sin x / x$ |
|---|---|
| 0.1 | 0.99833 |
| 0.01 | 0.999983 |
| 0.001 | 0.99999983 |

The limit is **1**. Geometrically, when $x$ is small, the arc length on a unit circle ($x$) and the chord length ($\sin x$) are nearly equal — they approach the same value as $x \to 0$. This limit is the *reason* the derivative of $\sin x$ is $\cos x$.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "The limit equals the function value."**

The limit is *independent* of $f(a)$. A function might be undefined at $a$, or have a different value at $a$, and still have a limit there. Example:

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} x+1 & x \neq 2 \\ 100 & x = 2 \end{cases}$$

$\lim_{x\to 2}f(x) = 3$ even though $f(2) = 100$. The limit only sees neighbors, not the point itself.

**Misconception 2: "If $f(a)$ is undefined, the limit doesn't exist."**

Not at all. As we saw, $(x^2-1)/(x-1)$ is undefined at $x=1$ but has a perfect limit there. The undefined nature at the point and the existence of a limit are completely separate questions.

**Misconception 3: "$0/0$ means the limit is zero (or undefined)."**

$0/0$ is *indeterminate* — it's a signal that we need to do algebra, not a final answer. The limit could be any number, infinity, or genuinely not exist. It depends on *how* numerator and denominator approach zero relative to each other. This is why "L'Hôpital's rule" exists — to handle exactly these cases.

**Misconception 4: "Plugging in is always wrong / always right."**

Plugging in $x = a$ works *if* $f$ is continuous at $a$. For polynomials, sums, products, and quotients (where defined), it works. For pieces with holes or jumps, it fails. The skill is recognizing which case you're in.

## One-Sided Limits

Sometimes a function approaches different values from the left and right of a point:

$$\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) \quad \text{(from below)} \qquad \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) \quad \text{(from above)}$$

The two-sided limit $\lim_{x\to a}f(x)$ exists *if and only if* both one-sided limits exist and are equal.

**Example:** $f(x) = |x|/x$ is $-1$ for $x < 0$ and $+1$ for $x > 0$. The left limit at 0 is $-1$, the right limit is $+1$. These don't match → the two-sided limit does not exist.

## Limits at Infinity

The same idea works as $x \to \infty$:

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{x} = 0$$

This says "as $x$ gets arbitrarily large, $1/x$ gets arbitrarily close to zero." The "approach" is now a journey to infinity rather than to a finite point.

For rational functions, divide numerator and denominator by the highest power of $x$:

$$\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{3x^2 + 2}{x^2 - 1} = \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{3 + 2/x^2}{1 - 1/x^2} = \frac{3 + 0}{1 - 0} = 3$$

## Engineering Connection

Limits underlie *every* engineering calculation involving "rates":

- **Instantaneous velocity:** $v(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0}\dfrac{x(t+\Delta t) - x(t)}{\Delta t}$. Without limits, "instantaneous" has no meaning — speed would only be defined as an average over an interval.
- **Stress at a point:** $\sigma = \lim_{\Delta A \to 0}\dfrac{\Delta F}{\Delta A}$. This is how we move from "force on an area" (a coarse measure) to "stress at a point" (the field quantity in solid mechanics).
- **Convergence in numerical methods:** Newton's method, Runge-Kutta integration, and finite element analysis all rely on limits to guarantee that successive approximations approach the true answer.

When you compute a stress, a temperature gradient, or a fluid velocity at a point, you are silently invoking a limit.

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 5}\frac{x^2 - 25}{x - 5}$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$0/0$ form. Factor: $\dfrac{(x-5)(x+5)}{x-5} = x+5$ for $x\neq 5$.

$$\lim_{x\to 5}(x+5) = \boxed{10}$$

### Application

<ExQ id="svc-limit-app-1" answer="6">
Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{h \to 0}\frac{(3+h)^2 - 9}{h}$. What does this limit represent geometrically?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Expand: $(3+h)^2 - 9 = 9 + 6h + h^2 - 9 = 6h + h^2$.

$$\frac{6h + h^2}{h} = 6 + h \to \boxed{6}$$

**Geometric meaning:** This is the slope of the tangent line to $f(x) = x^2$ at $x = 3$ — i.e., $f'(3)$. Every derivative is exactly this kind of limit.
</ExA>

<ExQ id="svc-limit-app-2" answer="2">
Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{4x^3 - x + 2}{2x^3 + 5x^2}$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Divide top and bottom by $x^3$:
$$\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{4 - 1/x^2 + 2/x^3}{2 + 5/x} = \frac{4 - 0 + 0}{2 + 0} = \boxed{2}$$
</ExA>

<ExQ>
Does $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0}\sin\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)$ exist? Justify.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
As $x \to 0^+$, $1/x \to +\infty$, so $\sin(1/x)$ oscillates between $-1$ and $+1$ infinitely often. It does **not** approach any single value.

The limit **does not exist** — the function oscillates wildly near 0.

This is different from "going to infinity" — it just doesn't settle.
</ExA>

### Challenge

<ExQ>
Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} x \sin\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)$ using the Squeeze Theorem.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
We know $-1 \leq \sin(1/x) \leq 1$ for all $x \neq 0$.

Multiply by $|x|$ (valid since $|x| \geq 0$): $-|x| \leq x\sin(1/x) \leq |x|$.

As $x \to 0$, both bounds $-|x|$ and $|x|$ tend to 0. By the **Squeeze Theorem**, the function trapped between them must also tend to 0.

$$\lim_{x\to 0}x\sin(1/x) = \boxed{0}$$

Even though $\sin(1/x)$ oscillates, multiplying by $x$ shrinks the oscillation amplitude to zero.
</ExA>

<ExQ id="svc-limit-challenge-2" answer="2" unit="m/s">
A particle moves so that its position at time $t$ (seconds) is $s(t) = t^3 - 3t^2 + 2t$ meters. Use limits to find the instantaneous velocity at $t = 2$ s.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$$v(2) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{s(2+h)-s(2)}{h}$$

$s(2) = 8 - 12 + 4 = 0$.

$s(2+h) = (2+h)^3 - 3(2+h)^2 + 2(2+h)$. Expand:
$(2+h)^3 = 8 + 12h + 6h^2 + h^3$
$3(2+h)^2 = 3(4+4h+h^2) = 12 + 12h + 3h^2$
$2(2+h) = 4 + 2h$

$s(2+h) = (8+12h+6h^2+h^3) - (12+12h+3h^2) + (4+2h) = 2h + 3h^2 + h^3$

$$\frac{s(2+h)-s(2)}{h} = \frac{2h+3h^2+h^3}{h} = 2 + 3h + h^2$$

$$v(2) = \lim_{h\to 0}(2+3h+h^2) = \boxed{2\ \text{m/s}}$$
</ExA>

### Conceptual

<ExQ>
**True or False.** If $\lim_{x\to a}f(x) = L$, then $f(a) = L$. Justify your answer with a specific example.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**False.** The limit only describes behavior *near* $a$, not at $a$.

**Counterexample:** $f(x) = \begin{cases}x+1 & x \neq 2 \\ 100 & x = 2\end{cases}$.

Here $\lim_{x\to 2}f(x) = 3$ (since $f$ behaves like $x+1$ everywhere near $x = 2$), but $f(2) = 100$.

So $\lim \neq f(a)$ in general. The two are equal only when $f$ is **continuous** at $a$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Sketch a function** $f$ that is defined for all $x$, with $\lim_{x\to 1}f(x) = 5$ but $f(1) = 2$. Is your $f$ continuous at $x = 1$? Why or why not?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Possible $f$: a smooth curve passing through $(0, 4)$, $(0.5, 4.5)$, and approaching $5$ as $x \to 1$ from both sides — but with an isolated point at $(1, 2)$.

For example: $f(x) = \begin{cases}x+4 & x \neq 1 \\ 2 & x = 1\end{cases}$.

**Not continuous** at $x = 1$, because the limit ($5$) does not equal $f(1)$ ($= 2$). This is a *removable discontinuity*: redefining $f(1) = 5$ would patch it.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Conceptual:** Suppose $\lim_{x\to 0}f(x) = 7$ and $\lim_{x\to 0}g(x)$ does not exist. Can $\lim_{x\to 0}[f(x) + g(x)]$ exist? Justify.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**No** — the limit cannot exist.

**Argument by contradiction:** Suppose $\lim(f + g) = M$. Then $\lim g = \lim[(f+g) - f] = M - 7$, which would mean $\lim g$ exists. Contradicts the hypothesis.

**Pattern to remember:** the sum of (a function with limit) + (a function without limit) cannot have a limit. The "well-behaved" piece can't fix the "ill-behaved" piece.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Estimation.** Without algebra, estimate $\lim_{x\to 0}\dfrac{e^x - 1}{x}$ by computing $f(x)$ at $x = 0.1, 0.01, 0.001$. What is the limit, and why?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
- $f(0.1) = (e^{0.1} - 1)/0.1 \approx 1.0517$
- $f(0.01) = (e^{0.01} - 1)/0.01 \approx 1.00502$
- $f(0.001) \approx 1.0005$

Values cluster near $1$, so the limit is $\boxed{1}$.

**Why?** This is the derivative of $e^x$ at $x = 0$. Since $(e^x)' = e^x$, we get $f'(0) = e^0 = 1$. The numerical pattern *is* this derivative.
</ExA>

### Engineering Synthesis

<ExQ>
**Heat conduction.** A long rod has temperature profile $T(x, t) = T_\infty + (T_0 - T_\infty)\,\text{erf}(x/(2\sqrt{\alpha t}))$ with $\text{erf}(z) \to 0$ as $z \to 0^+$. What is $\lim_{t\to\infty}T(x, t)$ for fixed $x > 0$? Interpret physically.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
As $t\to\infty$: $x/(2\sqrt{\alpha t}) \to 0^+$, so $\text{erf}(\cdot) \to 0$.

$$\lim_{t\to\infty}T(x,t) = T_\infty + (T_0 - T_\infty)\cdot 0 = T_\infty$$

**Physical interpretation:** Far in the future, the rod equilibrates to ambient temperature $T_\infty$ everywhere — the initial perturbation has fully diffused away. The limit captures *long-time* asymptotic behavior, an essential tool for analyzing transient heat transfer.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Reynolds number scaling.** A drag coefficient $C_D(Re) = 24/Re + 0.5$ for a sphere in a flow with Reynolds number $Re$. Find $\lim_{Re \to 0}C_D$ and $\lim_{Re\to\infty}C_D$. What flow regime does each limit describe?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
- $Re \to 0$: $24/Re \to \infty$, so $C_D \to \infty$. **Stokes flow regime** — viscous drag dominates, $C_D$ grows without bound.
- $Re \to \infty$: $24/Re \to 0$, so $C_D \to 0.5$. **Inertial regime** — viscosity becomes negligible, $C_D$ approaches a constant (Newton's drag law).

This piecewise asymptotic structure is the basis of dimensional analysis in fluid mechanics.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Combined.** Let $f(x) = \dfrac{x^2 - 4x + 3}{x^2 - 1}$. Find all points where $f$ has a limit but is not defined. At each such point, what value would make $f$ continuous?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Factor: $f(x) = \dfrac{(x-1)(x-3)}{(x-1)(x+1)}$.

**$x = 1$:** $0/0$. Cancel: $f(x) = (x-3)/(x+1) \to (1-3)/(1+1) = -1$. Limit exists, but $f(1)$ undefined. Define $f(1) = -1$ to patch.

**$x = -1$:** numerator $= (-1-1)(-1-3) = 8 \neq 0$, denominator $= 0$. Limit blows up to $\pm\infty$. **Not removable.**

So the only patchable point is $x = 1$ with patch value $-1$.

This kind of analysis appears constantly in engineering when transfer functions or response curves have algebraic cancellations.
</ExA>
