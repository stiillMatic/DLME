# The Definite Integral and the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

## The Big Picture

The definite integral does for "summing infinitely many infinitesimal pieces" what the derivative does for "infinitesimal change." It computes accumulated quantities: total work, total mass, total flow, area under a curve. The **Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC)** then makes a stunning claim: the two great operations of calculus — differentiation and integration — are **inverses** of each other. This isn't an accident; it's the deepest result in elementary calculus, and it's what lets us actually compute integrals without summing forever.

## Intuition First — Areas, Sums, and Integrals

Imagine driving for one hour at varying speed. You want the total distance traveled. If your speed were constant at 60 km/h, the answer is easy: $60 \times 1 = 60$ km. But if your speed varied?

Approximate. Break the hour into 60 minutes. In each minute, assume the speed is roughly constant. Add up minute-by-minute distances. The total is approximately the distance traveled — and the approximation gets better as we use more, smaller intervals.

In the limit of infinitely many infinitesimal intervals, this sum becomes an **integral**:

$$\text{distance} = \int_0^1 v(t)\,dt$$

Geometrically, you're computing the **area under the velocity-vs-time curve**. Integrals and areas are inseparable in 1D.

## From Riemann Sums to the Definite Integral

To define $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ rigorously:

1. Partition $[a, b]$ into $n$ subintervals of width $\Delta x = (b-a)/n$.
2. In each subinterval, pick a sample point $x_i^*$.
3. Form the **Riemann sum**:

$$S_n = \sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i^*)\Delta x$$

This sum represents the total area of $n$ rectangles approximating the region under the curve.

4. Take $n \to \infty$:

$$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = \lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i^*)\Delta x$$

If this limit exists (and the same value is reached regardless of how sample points $x_i^*$ are chosen), then $f$ is **integrable** on $[a, b]$ and the integral equals the limit.

For continuous functions, this limit always exists.

## The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

**Part 1 (FTC1):** If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$ and $F(x) = \displaystyle\int_a^x f(t)\,dt$, then $F$ is differentiable and $F'(x) = f(x)$.

In words: **integrating with a variable upper limit and then differentiating gets you back where you started**.

**Part 2 (FTC2, the Evaluation Theorem):** If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$ and $F$ is *any* antiderivative of $f$, then:

$$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a)$$

In words: **to compute a definite integral, find an antiderivative and evaluate it at the endpoints**.

This is the bridge: integration (a process of summing) is computed via antidifferentiation (the inverse of differentiation).

## Why FTC Is Astonishing

Before the FTC, integrals were defined as limits of sums — a *process*. Antiderivatives were defined as inverses of derivatives — completely different.

The FTC says these two seemingly unrelated operations are tightly linked: every integral evaluation reduces to finding an antiderivative. We don't actually compute Riemann sums by hand; we use FTC2 and find $F$.

This is *why* differentiation and integration are studied together. The connection isn't deeply hidden — it's a direct consequence of how change accumulates.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** $\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi}\sin x\,dx$.

Antiderivative: $F(x) = -\cos x$.

$$\int_0^\pi\sin x\,dx = -\cos\pi - (-\cos 0) = -(-1) - (-1) = 1 + 1 = 2$$

The area under one arch of $\sin x$ is exactly $2$ — a beautiful result.

**Example 2.** $\displaystyle\int_1^4 (3x^2 - 2x + 1)\,dx$.

Antiderivative: $F(x) = x^3 - x^2 + x$.

$$F(4) - F(1) = (64 - 16 + 4) - (1 - 1 + 1) = 52 - 1 = 51$$

**Example 3.** Find $\dfrac{d}{dx}\displaystyle\int_1^x \sqrt{1 + t^3}\,dt$.

By FTC1, the derivative is the integrand evaluated at the upper limit:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_1^x\sqrt{1+t^3}\,dt = \sqrt{1+x^3}$$

The antiderivative of $\sqrt{1+t^3}$ has no elementary form, but we don't need it — FTC1 gives us the derivative directly.

## Properties of Definite Integrals

| Property | Identity |
|---|---|
| Reverse limits | $\int_a^b f = -\int_b^a f$ |
| Equal limits | $\int_a^a f = 0$ |
| Splitting | $\int_a^b f = \int_a^c f + \int_c^b f$ |
| Linearity | $\int_a^b(\alpha f + \beta g) = \alpha\int_a^b f + \beta\int_a^b g$ |

Splitting is especially useful for piecewise integrands or when you want to integrate over symmetric or geometric regions.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "$\int_a^b f$ equals the area."**

Almost. It equals the **signed area** — positive where $f > 0$, negative where $f < 0$. For total geometric area, integrate $|f|$.

**Misconception 2: "If $\int_a^b f = 0$, then $f = 0$ everywhere on $[a, b]$."**

False. The signed areas could cancel. $\int_{-1}^1 x\,dx = 0$ even though $x \neq 0$ except at the origin.

**Misconception 3: "Apply FTC blindly even if $f$ is discontinuous."**

FTC requires continuity (or at most a finite number of jumps). For genuinely discontinuous $f$, you must split the integral at the discontinuities.

**Misconception 4: "I have to find an antiderivative; if it doesn't exist in closed form, I can't compute the integral."**

False. Many definite integrals can be computed numerically (Simpson's rule, Gaussian quadrature) even when no closed-form antiderivative exists. The integral *exists*; we just compute it differently.

**Misconception 5: "FTC1 says the integral equals the function."**

FTC1 says the *derivative* of the integral (with variable upper limit) equals the function. The integral itself is generally a different function (the antiderivative).

## Engineering Connection

The definite integral computes **accumulated quantities** — fundamental in every engineering field:

- **Work done by a variable force:** $W = \int_{x_1}^{x_2} F(x)\,dx$
- **Mass of a non-uniform rod:** $m = \int_0^L \rho(x)A(x)\,dx$
- **Total heat transfer:** $Q = \int_{t_1}^{t_2}\dot{Q}(t)\,dt$
- **Center of mass:** $\bar{x} = \dfrac{\int x\rho\,dV}{\int \rho\,dV}$
- **Strain energy in a beam:** $U = \dfrac{1}{2}\int_0^L\dfrac{M^2(x)}{EI}\,dx$
- **Volumetric flow rate through a cross-section:** $Q = \int_A v\,dA$
- **Total electric charge:** $Q = \int_t i(t)\,dt$

Whenever a "rate" or "density" varies and you need a "total," it's an integral.

The FTC makes these calculations tractable: instead of summing infinitely many infinitesimal contributions, find an antiderivative and evaluate at endpoints.

## Practice Problems

### Warm-up

<ExQ>
Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^2 (x^2 + 3)\,dx$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Antiderivative: $F(x) = x^3/3 + 3x$.

$$F(2) - F(0) = (8/3 + 6) - 0 = \frac{8}{3} + 6 = \frac{26}{3}$$
</ExA>

<ExQ>
Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_1^4\frac{1}{\sqrt{x}}\,dx$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$\int x^{-1/2}\,dx = 2\sqrt{x} + C$.

$$\left[2\sqrt{x}\right]_1^4 = 2(2) - 2(1) = \boxed{2}$$
</ExA>

### Application

<ExQ>
A force $F(x) = 100 - 10x$ N acts on an object as it moves from $x = 0$ to $x = 8$ m. Find the work done.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$$W = \int_0^8 (100 - 10x)\,dx = \left[100x - 5x^2\right]_0^8 = 800 - 320 = \boxed{480\ \text{J}}$$
</ExA>

<ExQ>
Find $\dfrac{d}{dx}\displaystyle\int_2^x e^{-t^2}\,dt$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
By FTC1, evaluate the integrand at the upper limit:
$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_2^x e^{-t^2}\,dt = e^{-x^2}$$

(The antiderivative of $e^{-t^2}$ has no elementary form, but FTC1 gives the derivative for free.)
</ExA>

<ExQ>
Find the area between the curve $y = x^2$ and the $x$-axis from $x = 0$ to $x = 3$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Since $x^2 \geq 0$, signed area = geometric area:
$$\int_0^3 x^2\,dx = \left[\frac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^3 = 9$$
</ExA>

### Challenge

<ExQ>
Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_{-2}^{2}|x|\,dx$ by splitting at $x = 0$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$|x| = -x$ for $x < 0$, $|x| = x$ for $x \geq 0$.

$$\int_{-2}^0(-x)\,dx + \int_0^2 x\,dx = \left[-\frac{x^2}{2}\right]_{-2}^0 + \left[\frac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^2 = (0-(-2)) + (2 - 0) = \boxed{4}$$
</ExA>

<ExQ>
A simply supported beam of length $L = 6$ m carries a triangular load $q(x) = 1000 \cdot x/L$ N/m. The total load is $W = \int_0^L q(x)\,dx$. Compute $W$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$$W = \int_0^6 \frac{1000 x}{6}\,dx = \frac{1000}{6}\cdot\frac{x^2}{2}\bigg|_0^6 = \frac{1000}{6}\cdot 18 = \boxed{3000\ \text{N}}$$

This equals the area of the triangle $\tfrac{1}{2}\cdot 6\cdot 1000 = 3000$ — geometric reasoning confirms the integral.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
The temperature in a 2-meter rod at time $t = 0$ is $T(x) = 50\sin(\pi x/2)$ °C. Find the average temperature, defined as $\bar{T} = \frac{1}{L}\int_0^L T(x)\,dx$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$$\bar{T} = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^2 50\sin(\pi x/2)\,dx = 25\int_0^2\sin(\pi x/2)\,dx$$

Antiderivative of $\sin(\pi x/2)$: $-\dfrac{2}{\pi}\cos(\pi x/2)$.

$$\bar{T} = 25\cdot\left[-\frac{2}{\pi}\cos(\pi x/2)\right]_0^2 = 25\cdot\frac{-2}{\pi}\bigl(\cos\pi - \cos 0\bigr) = 25\cdot\frac{-2}{\pi}(-2) = \frac{100}{\pi} \approx \boxed{31.8°\text{C}}$$
</ExA>

### Conceptual

<ExQ>
**True or False.** $\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ equals the area between $f$ and the $x$-axis on $[a, b]$. Justify with an example.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**False (in general).** The integral gives **signed area** — positive where $f > 0$, negative where $f < 0$.

Counterexample: $\int_{-1}^1 x\,dx = 0$. The two regions have equal magnitude but opposite signs, so they cancel. The geometric (unsigned) area is $1/2 + 1/2 = 1 \neq 0$.

For geometric area, integrate $|f(x)|$, splitting the integral wherever $f$ changes sign.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Without computing.** Determine the sign of each integral, and explain:
(a) $\int_{-2}^2 x^3\,dx$
(b) $\int_0^2 x^3\,dx$
(c) $\int_{-1}^1 \cos(x)\,dx$
(d) $\int_0^{2\pi}\sin x\,dx$
</ExQ>
<ExA>
(a) **Zero** — odd function on symmetric interval.
(b) **Positive** — integrand positive throughout.
(c) **Positive** — even function, both halves positive.
(d) **Zero** — full period of sine cancels.

Symmetry arguments save algebra and develop intuition. They are routinely used in ME (e.g., zero net force on a symmetric body in symmetric flow).
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**FTC concept.** What does the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus say about the relationship between $f(x)$ and $G(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\,dt$? Why is this surprising?
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**Statement (FTC1):** If $f$ is continuous, then $G(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\,dt$ is differentiable, and $G'(x) = f(x)$.

**Why surprising:** integration is a *summing* process — accumulating all values of $f$ from $a$ up to $x$. Differentiation is an *instantaneous rate* operation. The FTC says these two seemingly different operations are *inverses*: differentiating an integral with variable upper limit gives back the integrand.

This connection is what makes calculus *useful*: instead of computing infinite Riemann sums, we just find an antiderivative.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Symmetry trap.** What is $\displaystyle\int_{-1}^1\dfrac{1}{x^2}\,dx$? Compute and reflect.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
**Trap:** the integrand is undefined at $x = 0$. The integral is **improper** and actually **diverges** ($\to +\infty$).

Naively applying FTC: $[-1/x]_{-1}^1 = -1 - 1 = -2$? That's nonsense — a positive integrand can't have negative integral.

**Lesson:** FTC requires continuity on the entire interval. When the integrand has singularities, you must split the integral and analyze improper limits carefully. Many students lose points here.
</ExA>

### Engineering Synthesis

<ExQ>
**RMS value.** For a sinusoidal signal $v(t) = V_0\sin(\omega t)$, the root-mean-square value is $V_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{T}\int_0^T v^2(t)\,dt}$ where $T = 2\pi/\omega$ is one period. Compute $V_{\text{rms}}$.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
$$V_{\text{rms}}^2 = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T V_0^2\sin^2(\omega t)\,dt = \frac{V_0^2}{T}\int_0^T\frac{1-\cos(2\omega t)}{2}\,dt$$

$$= \frac{V_0^2}{T}\cdot\frac{1}{2}\left[t - \frac{\sin(2\omega t)}{2\omega}\right]_0^T = \frac{V_0^2}{T}\cdot\frac{T}{2} = \frac{V_0^2}{2}$$

$$V_{\text{rms}} = \frac{V_0}{\sqrt 2}$$

This is the classic AC formula: 110 V RMS in your wall outlet means a peak of $110\sqrt 2 \approx 156$ V.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Heat dissipated.** A resistor with $R = 100\,\Omega$ has current $i(t) = 2e^{-t}$ A flowing for $t \in [0, 5]$ s. Find the total heat dissipated.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Power: $P(t) = i^2 R = 400 e^{-2t}$ W.

Heat: $Q = \int_0^5 P\,dt = 400\int_0^5 e^{-2t}\,dt = 400\cdot\left[-\frac{e^{-2t}}{2}\right]_0^5 = 200(1 - e^{-10})$.

$\approx 200(1 - 0.0000454) \approx 199.99$ J.

Almost all energy is dissipated in the first second or two — exponential decay concentrates contributions near $t=0$.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Volume by slicing.** A solid has triangular cross-sections perpendicular to the $x$-axis with base on the $x$-axis from $0$ to $4$ m. The base of each triangle is $b(x) = \sqrt{x}$ and its height is $h(x) = 2x$. Find the volume.
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Each cross-section has area $A(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}b(x)h(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}\sqrt{x}\cdot 2x = x^{3/2}$.

$$V = \int_0^4 x^{3/2}\,dx = \left[\frac{2x^{5/2}}{5}\right]_0^4 = \frac{2}{5}\cdot 32 = \boxed{12.8\text{ m}^3}$$

Volume by slicing converts a 3D problem into a 1D integral — a key technique in fluid mechanics and structural design.
</ExA>

<ExQ>
**Variable load.** A beam of length $L$ carries a triangular load $q(x) = q_0 x/L$. Find the total downward force, and locate its centroid (point of resultant).
</ExQ>
<ExA>
Total force:
$$F = \int_0^L q_0 x/L\,dx = \frac{q_0}{L}\cdot\frac{L^2}{2} = \frac{q_0 L}{2}$$

Centroid:
$$\bar x = \frac{1}{F}\int_0^L x\cdot q(x)\,dx = \frac{2}{q_0 L}\int_0^L \frac{q_0 x^2}{L}\,dx = \frac{2}{L^2}\cdot\frac{L^3}{3} = \frac{2L}{3}$$

The resultant of a triangular distributed load is $q_0 L/2$ acting at $2L/3$ from the start. Standard structural engineering result.
</ExA>
